# Margaret Atwood

## name
Margaret Eleanor Atwood

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
Canada (Anglophone)

## years active
1961–present

## monumental works
- **The Handmaid's Tale** (1985)
    - form: novel
    - Theocratic Republic of Gilead enslaves fertile women as reproductive vessels; central text of late-20th-century dystopia.
- **Oryx and Crake** (2003) / **The Year of the Flood** (2009) / **MaddAddam** (2013)
    - form: novel trilogy
    - Biotech-apocalypse trilogy: a genetically engineered post-human species inherits the world after a pandemic.
- **The Testaments** (2019)
    - form: novel
    - Booker-winning sequel to *The Handmaid's Tale*.

## themes
dystopia, gender, biology, power, religion/myth, ecology

## influences
- Orwell (Atwood has repeatedly cited *Nineteen Eighty-Four*; her CBC Massey Lectures and numerous essays).
- Puritan New England history (her own ancestor Mary Webster inspired *The Handmaid's Tale*'s epigraph).
- Northrop Frye (her teacher at the University of Toronto).

## influenced
- A vast public readership and a generation of dystopian-feminist writers, though Atwood is cautious about the "SF" label and prefers "speculative fiction" — Le Guin famously disagreed with her terminology (documented in their *Guardian* exchange, 2009, and in *In Other Worlds*, 2011).
- The televised *Handmaid's Tale* (2017–) has had cross-cultural political impact in ways few SF novels have matched.

## translation history
- Translated into 40+ languages.
    - **The Handmaid's Tale**: French (*La Servante écarlate*, tr. Sylviane Rué, 1987, Laffont); German (*Der Report der Magd*, tr. Helga Pfetsch, 1987); Japanese (*侍女の物語*, tr. 斎藤英治 Saitō Eiji, 1990, Shinchōsha); Chinese, Korean, Russian, Arabic editions widely available.
    - **Oryx and Crake**: widely translated, including French (tr. Michèle Albaret-Maatsch), Spanish, German, Japanese.
- Full translator bibliography at margaretatwood.ca and publisher (McClelland & Stewart) records.

## notable recognition
- Booker Prize (2000, *The Blind Assassin*; 2019 shared, *The Testaments*).
- Arthur C. Clarke Award (1987, *The Handmaid's Tale* — the inaugural winner).
- Governor General's Literary Award; Companion of the Order of Canada; PEN Pinter Prize; Franz Kafka Prize.

## brief bio
Margaret Atwood was born in Ottawa and educated at the University of Toronto and Harvard. Her output spans poetry, literary fiction, criticism, and speculative fiction, and she has been a central figure in Canadian letters since the 1970s. *The Handmaid's Tale* (1985) and the MaddAddam trilogy (2003–13) are the most-discussed works of late-20th- and early-21st-century dystopian SF. Her critical books *In Other Worlds* (2011) and *Negotiating with the Dead* (2002) address the SF tradition explicitly. She has been a prolific public essayist on climate, authoritarianism, and reproductive rights.
