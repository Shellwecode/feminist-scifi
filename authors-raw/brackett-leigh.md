# Leigh Brackett

## name
Leigh Douglass Brackett

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1940–1978

## monumental works
- **The Long Tomorrow** (1955)
    - form: novel
    - Post-nuclear pastoral in which a Mennonite-descended America has outlawed cities; often cited as one of the finest post-apocalyptic novels of the 1950s.
- **The Sword of Rhiannon** (1953)
    - form: novel
    - Mars-set planetary romance; paradigm of the form.
- **The Ginger Star** / Eric John Stark books (1974–76)
    - form: novel trilogy
    - Late-career return to planetary adventure with the Stark character originally developed in the 1940s.
- **"The Queer Ones"** (1957)
    - form: novella
    - Hybrid-child / hidden-alien story.

## themes
post-apocalypse, space-opera, weird/horror, colonialism

## influences
- Pulp adventure (Burroughs, Merritt), Hollywood noir (she worked as a screenwriter — *The Big Sleep*, *Rio Bravo*, *The Long Goodbye*, and a draft of *The Empire Strikes Back*).

## influenced
- Married to and collaborated with Edmond Hamilton.
- Ray Bradbury has publicly credited Brackett as a mentor — he finished her novella "Lorelei of the Red Mist" (1946) after she departed to work on *The Big Sleep*. (Documented in Bradbury's introductions and in the *Planet Stories* printing history.)
- Later writers of feminist space opera and planetary romance (C.J. Cherryh, Catherine Asaro) inherit her adventure-plus-atmosphere template, though direct self-reported influence is less often documented.
- Credited on *The Empire Strikes Back* (1980) after her death; her draft shaped later revisions.

## translation history
- *The Long Tomorrow* translated into French (*Le Grand Lendemain*, tr. uncertain, 1964 approximate) and other languages.
- Stark stories translated into French, German, Italian editions from the 1960s onward — translator records partial.

## notable recognition
- Guest of Honour at the 1965 Cincinnati Worldcon (Midwestcon).
- Posthumous Hugo nomination for *The Empire Strikes Back* screenplay.
- Regarded as "the Queen of Space Opera" (phrase used in multiple obituaries and SFE entry).

## brief bio
Leigh Brackett broke into the pulps in 1940 and became one of the defining voices of planetary romance — her Mars and Venus stories shaped the genre's visual imagination. Her Hollywood career (working for Howard Hawks on *The Big Sleep*, among others) ran in parallel with her SF output. *The Long Tomorrow* (1955) marked a shift to serious post-atomic fiction. Late in life she returned to the Stark stories and completed a first-draft screenplay of *The Empire Strikes Back* shortly before her death from cancer in 1978. She was married to fellow SF writer Edmond Hamilton from 1946.
