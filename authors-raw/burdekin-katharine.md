# Katharine Burdekin

## name
Katharine Penelope Burdekin (née Cade; wrote as Murray Constantine)

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United Kingdom (Anglophone)

## years active
1922–1960 (published 1922–mid-1940s; rediscovered 1985)

## monumental works
- **Swastika Night** (1937, as Murray Constantine)
    - form: novel
    - Seven hundred years into a Nazi-dominated future, women are kept as livestock and Hitler is worshipped as God. Predates *1984* by twelve years.
- **Proud Man** (1934, as Murray Constantine)
    - form: novel
    - Genderless utopian visitor from the future critiques 1930s England; explicit gender-SF.
- **The End of This Day's Business** (written c. 1935; published posthumously 1989)
    - form: novel
    - Mirror-image matriarchal dystopia; men are the subordinate sex.

## themes
gender, dystopia, power, religion/myth

## influences
- Feminist and socialist writing of the interwar period; Olaf Stapledon's speculative sweep (stylistic kinship, though direct influence not self-reported).

## influenced
- Rediscovered by Daphne Patai in 1985, who identified Murray Constantine as Burdekin. Now routinely cited as an overlooked foremother of feminist dystopia.
- Margaret Atwood's *The Handmaid's Tale* (1985) appeared the year *Swastika Night* was being republished in its new identifying imprint; critics have drawn parallels though not direct influence.

## influenced by
(see above)

## translation history
- *Swastika Night*: translated into German (*Hakenkreuznacht*), Italian, Spanish; translator records partial.
- Other novels remain English-only in most cases.

## notable recognition
- Rediscovered by feminist-SF scholarship; reprinted by The Feminist Press (1985) with Daphne Patai's introduction.
- Now a standard reference in feminist dystopia scholarship.

## brief bio
Katharine Burdekin published six novels under her own name in the 1920s–30s, then four under the pseudonym Murray Constantine — a pseudonym so successful that her identity was lost for four decades. *Swastika Night* (1937) is now recognised as perhaps the first anti-fascist dystopia, explicitly imagining a future where Nazi victory has produced a totalised patriarchal theocracy. She lived quietly with her partner Isabel Allan-Burns from 1926 until her death in 1963. The Daphne Patai re-attribution in 1985 restored her to literary history.
