# Pat Cadigan

## name
Patricia Oren Kearney Cadigan

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States / United Kingdom (Anglophone)

## years active
1978–present

## monumental works
- **Mindplayers** (1987)
    - form: novel
    - A technician who jacks into other minds as therapy; one of the first long-form feminist cyberpunk works.
- **Synners** (1991)
    - form: novel
    - Direct neural interface to media networks in near-future LA; won the Arthur C. Clarke Award.
- **Fools** (1992)
    - form: novel
    - Memory-theft and identity-layering in a shared-consciousness future; Arthur C. Clarke Award winner.
- **"The Girl-Thing Who Went Out for Sushi"** (2012)
    - form: novelette
    - Body-modified Jovian sushi workers; late-career Hugo winner (Best Novelette, 2013).

## themes
cyberpunk, AI/machine-consciousness, embodiment, gender, labor

## influences
- Philip K. Dick (self-reported; her "what is real?" moves are often compared and she's credited the lineage).
- Ellen Datlow (as editor at *Omni*, Datlow shepherded many of Cadigan's early short stories).
- Co-traveller with the cyberpunk movement (Gibson, Sterling, Shiner, Shirley) — Cadigan is the only woman typically named in the first-wave cyberpunk group.

## influenced
- Often called "the Queen of Cyberpunk" (phrase used in multiple SF reference works).
- Melissa Scott, Kathleen Ann Goonan, Justina Robson work in her lineage; Robson has mentioned Cadigan in interviews.

## translation history
- *Synners*: French (*Synthèse*, tr. Jean Bonnefoy, J'ai Lu, 1993); German (*Synners*, Heyne); Japanese (*シナーズ*, tr. 嶋田洋一 Shimada Yōichi, 1994, Hayakawa); Italian.
- *Mindplayers*: French (tr. Jean Bonnefoy); German (Heyne); Japanese (tr. details partial).
- *Fools*: French and German editions.

## notable recognition
- Arthur C. Clarke Award (1992, *Synners*; 1995, *Fools*) — only writer to win twice until Chris Beckett.
- Hugo Award (2013, "The Girl-Thing Who Went Out for Sushi").
- Locus Awards.
- Inkpot Award (2013).

## brief bio
Pat Cadigan was born in Schenectady and raised in Fitchburg, Massachusetts. She worked as an editor for the Kansas City-based SF small press *Shayol* and at Hallmark before moving to full-time writing. Her 1980s short fiction in *Omni* and *Asimov's* placed her at the centre of the cyberpunk cohort; she is the only woman usually included in the first-wave cyberpunk canon. She moved to England in 1996 and lives in London. In the 2010s she wrote publicly about surviving cancer and continued producing short fiction including her Hugo-winning Jovian "sushi" series.
