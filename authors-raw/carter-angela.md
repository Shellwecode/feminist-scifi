# Angela Carter

## name
Angela Olive Carter (née Stalker)

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United Kingdom (Anglophone)

## years active
1966–1992

## monumental works
- **The Passion of New Eve** (1977)
    - form: novel
    - An Englishman kidnapped by a matriarchal cult in desert America is surgically made into a woman; gender-SF as feverish allegory.
- **Heroes and Villains** (1969)
    - form: novel
    - Post-apocalyptic tribal England: Professors, Barbarians, Out People.
- **The Infernal Desire Machines of Doctor Hoffman** (1972)
    - form: novel
    - Reality-generating war of images in a Latin-American-coded dictatorship.
- **The Bloody Chamber** (1979)
    - form: short-story collection
    - Feminist retellings of European fairy tales; more weird-horror than SF, but central to her speculative corpus.

## themes
gender, power, weird/horror, dystopia, embodiment, post-apocalypse

## influences
- Surrealism, de Sade, Baudelaire (Carter wrote *The Sadeian Woman* in 1978); Alejo Carpentier and magical realism.
- Japanese literature and cinema (she lived in Tokyo 1969–72).

## influenced
- Her reshaping of fairy tales has been claimed by Neil Gaiman, Kelly Link, Karen Russell, Helen Oyeyemi, and many others as a formative model (stated in interviews and introductions).
- Salman Rushdie has called her "one of the most individual, distinctive and extraordinary voices of her generation" (introduction to *Burning Your Boats*, 1995).

## translation history
- *The Passion of New Eve*: French (*La Passion de l'Ève nouvelle*, tr. Fortunato Israël, Christian Bourgois), German, Japanese (*新しいイヴの受難*, tr. 国書刊行会, 1999), Italian, Spanish.
- *The Bloody Chamber*: widely translated into French, German, Japanese, Italian, Spanish, Korean, Chinese.
- Translator records partial for some editions.

## notable recognition
- James Tait Black Memorial Prize (1985, *Nights at the Circus*).
- Cheltenham Festival Award.
- Posthumous canonisation: *Times* ranked her among the "50 greatest British writers since 1945" (2008).

## brief bio
Angela Carter was born in Eastbourne, studied medieval literature at Bristol, and wrote a thesis-length corpus of fiction, journalism, criticism, and radio drama in her 51 years. Her two years in Tokyo (1969–72) and subsequent periods in the US and Australia marked her fiction with a polyglot, anti-provincial register. Her overlap with SF is most explicit in *Heroes and Villains* and *The Passion of New Eve*, but her entire corpus sits inside the speculative. She taught at the University of East Anglia's creative writing programme, where her students included Kazuo Ishiguro. She died of lung cancer in 1992, aged 51.
