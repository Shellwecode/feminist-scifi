# Gabriela Damián Miravete

## name
Gabriela Damián Miravete

## primary writing language
Spanish

## country / region
Mexico (Latin American)

## years active
2010s–present

## monumental works
- **"Soñarán en el jardín" / "They Will Dream in the Garden"** (2016)
    - form: short story
    - Holographic reconstructions of murdered women, installed in a garden so their memories are not erased. English tr. Adrian Demopulos, published in *Latin American Literature Today* and elsewhere. James Tiptree Jr. Award 2018.
- **La tradición de Judas** (short fiction, various)
    - form: short stories
    - Appeared in Mexican speculative-fiction venues.
- **"On the Path of the Wolves" / "La ruta de los lobos"** (anthologised)
    - form: short story

## themes
gender, power, religion/myth, weird/horror, time

## influences
- The Mexican literary-fantastic tradition (Elena Garro, Amparo Dávila).
- Ursula K. Le Guin (cited in panel appearances and interviews).
- The Mexican feminist activist tradition — "They Will Dream in the Garden" responds directly to Mexico's femicide crisis.

## influenced
- Part of the Mexicx (Mexican) speculative-fiction cohort including Alberto Chimal, Bef, Andrea Chapela; Damián Miravete is also active as a film critic and essayist.
- Co-founder of the "Cúmulo de Tesla" collective of Mexican science-fiction creators.

## translation history
- **"They Will Dream in the Garden"**: English tr. **Adrian Demopulos**, in *Latin American Literature Today* (2018), and reprinted in the *Tiptree Award Anthology* material; also in the *Strange Horizons* community. James Tiptree Jr. Award winner, 2018.
- Other short stories have appeared in English in *Latin American Literature Today*, *Samovar*, and small-press anthologies.
- No English-language book-length collection as of the mid-2020s — flag.

## notable recognition
- James Tiptree Jr. Award (2018, "They Will Dream in the Garden") — she was the first Latin American woman to win this award.

## brief bio
Gabriela Damián Miravete is a Mexico City-based writer, film critic, and speculative-fiction essayist. Her Tiptree Award win in 2018 placed contemporary Mexican feminist SF into the US-feminist-SF prize conversation for the first time. Her fiction responds directly to the politics of femicide and state violence in contemporary Mexico, using speculative form to imagine forms of memory and resistance. English-language availability of her work is primarily through online magazines and short anthology appearances.
