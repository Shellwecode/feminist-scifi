# Tananarive Due

## name
Tananarive Priscilla Due

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (African diaspora)

## years active
1995–present

## monumental works
- **My Soul to Keep** (1997) — first of the African Immortals series; continued with *The Living Blood* (2001), *Blood Colony* (2008), *My Soul to Take* (2011).
    - form: novel series
    - Immortals descended from an Ethiopian line whose blood heals; a secret thousand-year lineage.
- **The Good House** (2003)
    - form: novel
    - Haunted-house novel in California with Black Southern spiritual practices.
- **Ghost Summer: Stories** (2015)
    - form: short-story collection
    - British Fantasy Award winner.
- **The Reformatory** (2023)
    - form: novel
    - A Black child sent to a Jim Crow-era Florida reform school sees ghosts; horror-historical.

## themes
race, religion/myth, weird/horror, family/kinship, biology

## influences
- Octavia E. Butler — direct, extensive, and public; Due teaches a course on Butler at UCLA titled "The Sunken Place" and produced the Shudder documentary *Horror Noire*.
- Stephen King, Shirley Jackson (acknowledged).

## influenced
- As UCLA lecturer in Afrofuturism and Black horror, Due has shaped contemporary academic engagement with the tradition.
- Jordan Peele credits Due's writing and curriculum as part of the Black-horror renewal; Peele's *Get Out* references the "sunken place" that Due teaches.

## translation history
- The African Immortals series: French, German (*Mein Herz gehört dir*, tr. details partial), Japanese, Dutch.
- **The Reformatory** (2023): French, German forthcoming/recent; specific translator records partial as of mid-2020s.
- Translation coverage is moderate; genre-marketed horror has broader European coverage than SF-marketed titles.

## notable recognition
- American Book Award (2002).
- NAACP Image Award for Outstanding Literary Work (multiple).
- Bram Stoker Award (2023, *The Reformatory*).
- British Fantasy Award (2016, *Ghost Summer*).
- Carl Brandon Society Parallax Award.

## brief bio
Tananarive Due was born in Tallahassee, Florida, the daughter of civil-rights activists (her mother, Patricia Stephens Due, was a key Florida movement figure). She worked as a newspaper columnist before her 1995 horror-fantasy debut *The Between*. Her African Immortals sequence (1997–2011) is among the most sustained works of Black speculative fiction of the late 20th and early 21st centuries. She teaches "Afrofuturism" and "The Sunken Place: Racism, Survival, and Black Horror" at UCLA and produced the documentary *Horror Noire* (Shudder, 2019). She is married to writer Steven Barnes.
