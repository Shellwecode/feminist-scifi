# Finisia Fideli

## name
Finisia Fideli

## primary writing language
Portuguese

## country / region
Brazil (Latin American)

## years active
1970s–2000s (primary SF period)

## monumental works
- **"Os Deuses de Arkhalhag" / "The Gods of Arkhalhag"** (1978)
    - form: short story
    - One of the best-known stories by a woman in the "Primeira Onda" (first wave) of Brazilian SF.
- **"Exercícios de Silêncio" / "Exercises in Silence"** (1988)
    - form: short story
    - Appeared in the anthology *Phantástica Brasileira*.
- Various short-story appearances in Brazilian SF magazines (*Magazine de Ficção Científica*, *Somnium*) from the 1970s onward.

## themes
first-contact, gender, language, dystopia

## influences
- The Brazilian "GRC" (Grupo de Leitores de Ficção Científica) and the *Primeira Onda* milieu around André Carneiro, Jerônymo Monteiro, and Dinah Silveira de Queiroz.
- Anglo-American SF in translation (standard for the Brazilian SF cohort of her generation).

## influenced
- Her early visibility as one of very few women writing SF in the Brazilian *Primeira Onda* (first wave, c. 1960–79) has made her a reference point for later Brazilian women SF writers, though direct self-reported influence statements from later writers are difficult to find in English sources.

## translation history
- Essentially untranslated into English. A handful of stories may have appeared in small-press international anthologies (needs verification).
- **Flag for gaps.md**: Brazilian women SF writers before the 2010s are severely underrepresented in English translation. Fideli is the canonical example of this gap.

## notable recognition
- Reference figure in Roberto de Sousa Causo's histories of Brazilian SF (e.g. *Ficção científica, fantasia e horror no Brasil 1875–1950*, 2003, and follow-up volumes).
- Minor Prêmio Nova (Brazilian SF community award).

## brief bio
Finisia Fideli was one of the few women publishing SF short fiction in Brazil during the *Primeira Onda* (First Wave) of the late 1960s and 1970s. Biographical details in English-language sources are thin; she is documented principally through Causo's Brazilian-SF historical studies and through anthology contents. Her work sits as a reminder that the history of Brazilian women's SF includes pre-2000 figures whose visibility has been limited by both gender and language barriers.
