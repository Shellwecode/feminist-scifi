# Charlotte Perkins Gilman

## name
Charlotte Perkins Gilman

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1860–1935

## monumental works
- **Herland** (serialized 1915 in *The Forerunner*; book 1979)
    - form: novel
    - An all-female utopian society discovered by three American male explorers; an early feminist utopia rooted in cooperative parenting and ecology.
- **"The Yellow Wall-Paper"** (1892)
    - form: short story
    - A woman confined under a "rest cure" descends into a hallucinatory reading of her wallpaper; often classed as proto-weird / proto-feminist horror rather than SF, but frequently grouped with her speculative work.
- **Moving the Mountain** (1911)
    - form: novel
    - An earlier utopian novel, companion to *Herland*.
- **With Her in Ourland** (1916)
    - form: novel
    - Sequel to *Herland* in which a Herlander visits the wartime outer world.

## themes
gender, utopia, ecology, labor, family/kinship

## influences
- Edward Bellamy's *Looking Backward* (1888) and the broader late-19th-century utopian movement; Gilman was active in socialist and Nationalist Club circles.
- Her great-aunt Harriet Beecher Stowe; the reformist tradition of Lester Frank Ward (sociological feminism).

## influenced
- Joanna Russ cites Gilman in *How to Suppress Women's Writing* (1983) and in her essays on feminist utopias.
- Widely cited as a precursor to the 1970s feminist utopia wave (Russ, Piercy, Charnas) — though direct influence statements are clearer in criticism than in self-report.

## translation history
- **Herland**: translated into Spanish, French, German, Japanese, and other languages — largely after its 1979 rediscovery. Specific translator/year data is partial and scattered across editions; e.g. Spanish edition *Dellas, un mundo femenino* (Abraxas, 2002) and various later printings. Japanese translation by 小林富久子 (Kobayashi Fukuko) published 1984 by 川島書店.
- **"The Yellow Wall-Paper"**: extremely widely translated via anthologies; full translator bibliography is beyond easy reach for this phase.

## notable recognition
- Rediscovered as a major feminist writer in the 1970s; *Herland* is a standard text in feminist and SF-studies curricula.

## brief bio
Charlotte Perkins Gilman was a writer, lecturer, and sociologist whose work argued that economic dependency structured women's oppression (*Women and Economics*, 1898). Her speculative fiction — largely published in her self-produced magazine *The Forerunner* — imagined societies reorganised around cooperative childcare, vegetarian ecology, and female autonomy. "The Yellow Wall-Paper" drew on her own experience of Silas Weir Mitchell's "rest cure." *Herland* circulated obscurely until Ann J. Lane's 1979 edition brought it into the feminist canon and into SF genre history. She died by suicide in 1935, having been diagnosed with inoperable breast cancer.
