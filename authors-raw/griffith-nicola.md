# Nicola Griffith

## name
Nicola Griffith

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United Kingdom / United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1988–present

## monumental works
- **Ammonite** (1993)
    - form: novel
    - An anthropologist visits a quarantined all-female planet where a virus killed all men centuries ago; the women reproduce via viral mutation. Tiptree and Lambda winner.
- **Slow River** (1995)
    - form: novel
    - Kidnapped heiress survives in a near-future water-recycling underclass; Nebula winner.
- **Hild** (2013) / **Menewood** (2023)
    - form: historical novel (seventh-century England)
    - Not SF, but central to her speculative-fiction reputation; deep embodied historical imagination.

## themes
gender, biology, ecology, labor, embodiment

## influences
- Joanna Russ and Ursula K. Le Guin — acknowledged in interviews and in Griffith's *Ammonite* afterword.
- James Tiptree Jr. — Griffith has spoken publicly about the Tiptree-Sheldon archive and the Tiptree Award (she is a Tiptree winner, 1994).

## influenced
- Her Lambda Literary Awards advocacy and the "LGBTQ SFF" infrastructure she has helped sustain has been widely credited; Nicola Griffith curates industry-statistics research (on authors' gender and prize outcomes).

## translation history
- *Ammonite*: French (*Ammonite*, tr. Michelle Charrier, 2000, J'ai Lu?); German (*Ammonit*, Heyne); Japanese (tr. details partial); Spanish.
- *Slow River*: French (tr. details partial), German.
- Hild translated more widely given its literary-mainstream crossover.

## notable recognition
- Nebula Award (1995, *Slow River*).
- Tiptree Award (1994, *Ammonite*).
- Lambda Literary Award (multiple).
- Washington State Book Award.

## brief bio
Nicola Griffith was born in Leeds, moved to the US in 1989, and has lived in Seattle since. She has multiple sclerosis, which she has written about candidly in essays. Her SF novels of the 1990s — *Ammonite* and *Slow River* — brought explicit queer content into hard-SF settings in ways that influenced the next generation of LGBTQ SFF. Since the mid-2000s her primary output has been historical fiction centred on the real seventh-century figure of Hild of Whitby, research-dense and notable for tactile physical embodiment of pre-modern women's lives. She is married to writer Kelley Eskridge.
