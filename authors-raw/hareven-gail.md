# Gail Hareven

## name
Gail Hareven / גיל הראבן

## primary writing language
Hebrew

## country / region
Israel (MENA)

## years active
1988–present

## monumental works
- **הכמיהה לקוסם** *Ha-Kemihah la-Kosem* (*The Confessions of Noa Weber*) (2000)
    - form: novel
    - Ostensibly a confession of obsessive love, but formally innovative and darkly comic. Sapir Prize. English tr. Dalya Bilu, Melville House, 2009 — Best Translated Book Award (2010).
- **Short stories in *Alef-Yud* and collections** (*Sometimes in Very Difficult Times*, 1990; *The Dark Road*, 2007; etc.)
    - form: short-story collections
    - Several include explicit SF/fantasy premises (alternate histories, apocalyptic motifs, magical-realist inversions). Hareven is one of the few prominent Israeli authors to write recognizable SF short fiction.
- **Ha-Makom she-ba-Nefesh** / *The Place That Souls Enter* (1997)
    - form: novel
    - Family/political novel with weird undercurrents.

## themes
gender, religion/myth, weird/horror, power, family/kinship, time

## influences
- Israeli literary modernism (Amos Oz, David Grossman) — acknowledged.
- Primo Levi, Italo Calvino — cited in interviews.
- Isaac Bashevis Singer, the Yiddish fantastic tradition — acknowledged.

## influenced
- Cited by younger Israeli SF/fantasy writers as a rare mainstream-literary-prestige presence in Israeli speculative fiction; the Geffen Award and Israeli SF community reference her as a precursor.
- Her Best Translated Book Award (2010) was a major visibility event for Israeli literary fiction in English.

## translation history
- **The Confessions of Noa Weber**: English tr. **Dalya Bilu**, Melville House, 2009. Best Translated Book Award 2010.
- **Lies, First Person** (*Shikurim Shikarim*, 2010): English tr. **Dalya Bilu**, Open Letter Books, 2015.
- **Short stories** translated into English: *Words Without Borders*, *Asymptote*, *Paper Republic* (Hareven has had a sustained relationship with short-fiction translation venues).
- French (Actes Sud, tr. Laurence Sendrowicz); German (*Bis die Sonne aufgeht*, Berlin Verlag); Italian, Dutch, Romanian, Turkish.

## notable recognition
- Sapir Prize (2002, *The Confessions of Noa Weber*).
- Best Translated Book Award, US (2010).
- Prime Minister's Prize for Hebrew Literary Works (multiple).
- International Institute of Modern Letters Fellowship.

## brief bio
Gail Hareven was born in Jerusalem in 1959, studied philosophy and literature at the Hebrew University, and has lived in Jerusalem through her career. She writes novels, short fiction, plays, and newspaper criticism, and has taught creative writing at the Sam Spiegel Film and Television School. Her fiction occupies an unusual Israeli space — ostensibly realist, but consistently open to speculative, weird, or fantastic inversions. Dalya Bilu's 2009 translation made her internationally visible; she remains one of the few Israeli women writers regularly included in international SF/speculative discussions.
