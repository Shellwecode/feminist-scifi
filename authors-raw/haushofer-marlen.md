# Marlen Haushofer

## name
Marlen Haushofer (née Frauendorfer)

## primary writing language
German

## country / region
Austria (European non-English)

## years active
1946–1970

## monumental works
- **Die Wand** (*The Wall*) (1963)
    - form: novel
    - A woman on a mountain holiday vacation finds herself separated from the rest of humanity by an invisible, impenetrable wall; everyone outside is dead. She survives with a dog, a cow, and a cat. A singular post-apocalyptic novel.
- **Himmel, der nirgendwo endet** (*Nowhere Ending Sky*) (1966)
    - form: novel
    - Autobiographical; not strictly SF.
- **Die Mansarde** (*The Loft*) (1969)
    - form: novel
    - Psychological novel; realist with dark undercurrents.

## themes
post-apocalypse, ecology, embodiment, family/kinship, gender, biology

## influences
- Austrian and German post-war literary traditions; Kafka in register.
- Not frequently self-documented — Haushofer gave few interviews and lived largely outside the literary establishment.

## influenced
- *The Wall*'s 1968 Arthur Schnitzler Prize and its rediscovery through feminist publishing in the 1980s made it a key text of solitude and female self-sufficiency in SF criticism.
- Elfriede Jelinek (Nobel laureate) has publicly praised Haushofer. Valeska Grisebach's 2012 film adaptation of *The Wall* (starring Martina Gedeck) broadened international visibility.
- Often discussed in feminist ecocritical scholarship as a predecessor to 2000s climate-fiction solitude narratives.

## translation history
- **The Wall**: English tr. **Shaun Whiteside**, Cleis Press, 1990 (first English edition). Re-issued by Quartet Books (UK), 1999. Re-translated in some editions.
- **Nowhere Ending Sky**: English tr. Amy Kepple Strawser, Ariadne Press, 2011.
- **The Loft**: English tr. Amanda Prantera, Quartet Books, 2011.
- French (*Le Mur invisible*, tr. Liselotte Bodo & Jacqueline Chambon, Actes Sud, 1985; re-issued 2015); Italian, Spanish, Dutch, Swedish, Japanese, Korean editions.

## notable recognition
- Arthur Schnitzler Prize (1968).
- Austrian State Prize for Literature (1963, *Die Wand*).
- Theodor Körner Prize (twice).
- Posthumous feminist canonisation from the 1980s onward.

## brief bio
Marlen Haushofer was born in Frauenstein, Upper Austria in 1920, studied briefly in Vienna and Graz, married a dentist in 1941, and lived in Steyr for most of her adult life. She wrote in the evenings, in the margins of a conventional housewife's life, and her work is strikingly unshowy and often bleak. *Die Wand* (1963) is her most-read book — a quiet, slow, utterly sustained 250 pages of survival on an Austrian alpine plot after an unexplained apocalypse. She died of cancer in 1970, aged 49. Her rediscovery in the 1980s by German feminist presses placed her firmly in the post-apocalyptic-SF canon.
