# Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain

## name
Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain / রোকেয়া সাখাওয়াত হোসেন (also Begum Rokeya)

## primary writing language
Bengali; also English

## country / region
British India (now Bangladesh) (South Asian)

## years active
1902–1932

## monumental works
- **"Sultana's Dream"** (1905)
    - form: short story
    - Written directly in English by Rokeya and published in *The Indian Ladies' Magazine* (Madras, 1905). A feminist utopia: "Ladyland," a country where men are kept in *mardana* (the male equivalent of *purdana*/purdah), and women run a solar-powered, flying-weather-controlled, non-violent society. One of the earliest feminist SF utopias globally and almost certainly the first written by a South Asian woman.
- **Padmarag** / *পদ্মরাগ* (1924)
    - form: novella
    - Bengali-language novella about a women's refuge-community in Calcutta; realist but imbued with utopian speculative energy.
- **Motichur** / *মতিচুর* (Vols. 1, 1904; Vol. 2, 1922)
    - form: essay collections
    - Non-fiction essays in Bengali on women's rights, education, purdah — context for the SF work.
- **Abarodhbashini** / *অবরোধবাসিনী* (*The Secluded Ones*) (1931)
    - form: essay collection
    - Journalistic vignettes of women's confinement under purdah; satirical-reportage.

## themes
utopia, gender, religion/myth, ecology, labor, language

## influences
- Muslim women's education movement in Bengal (her brother Ibrahim Saber encouraged her self-education).
- Direct engagement with the Edwardian feminist press in British India; possible awareness of Western feminist writing of the period (hard to document specifically).

## influenced
- Widely claimed as the foundational text of South Asian feminist SF.
- Repeatedly referenced in Vandana Singh's essays on South Asian SF; Bina Shah has discussed "Sultana's Dream" as a precursor to her own *Before She Sleeps*.
- The Begum Rokeya Memorial Award and Begum Rokeya University in Rangpur, Bangladesh, are named for her; December 9 is "Rokeya Day" in Bangladesh.

## translation history
- **"Sultana's Dream"**: originally written **in English** by Hossain herself. Bengali translation by Hossain as *Sultanar Swapna*, published 1908.
- Reprinted in English in many anthologies globally, including *Sultana's Dream and Selections from The Secluded Ones*, tr./ed. Roushan Jahan, Feminist Press, 1988.
- **Padmarag**: English tr. Barnita Bagchi, Penguin India, 2005 (*Sultana's Dream and Padmarag*).
- Translations into Hindi, Urdu, Tamil, Malayalam, French, Spanish, German, Arabic exist — details for most are partial.

## notable recognition
- Begum Rokeya Day (Bangladesh, December 9) marks her birth anniversary.
- Ekushey Padak (posthumous recognition).
- Routinely named in international lists of foundational feminist SF works.

## brief bio
Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain was born in 1880 in Pairaband, in what is now Rangpur, Bangladesh. She was largely self-taught — her brother secretly taught her English and Bengali against her conservative family's norms. Married into a reform-minded Muslim family, she began publishing essays after her husband's death in 1909 and founded the Sakhawat Memorial Girls' School in Calcutta. "Sultana's Dream" (1905) remains her most-read work and is one of the very earliest — and most cheerful — feminist utopian SF stories in any language. She died in Calcutta in 1932.
