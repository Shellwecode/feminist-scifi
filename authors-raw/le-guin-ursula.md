# Ursula K. Le Guin

## name
Ursula Kroeber Le Guin

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1959–2018

## monumental works
- **The Left Hand of Darkness** (1969)
    - form: novel
    - Ambisexual Gethenians disrupt the envoy's — and the reader's — assumptions about gender. A foundational text of feminist SF and of SF anthropology.
- **The Dispossessed** (1974)
    - form: novel
    - Anarchist moon Anarres contrasted with capitalist Urras; "an ambiguous utopia."
- **The Lathe of Heaven** (1971)
    - form: novel
    - A man whose dreams rewrite reality is manipulated by a therapist with utopian ambitions.
- **The Word for World Is Forest** (1972; revised 1976)
    - form: novella/short novel
    - Anti-colonial fable set on a forest world being clear-cut by human loggers.

## themes
gender, utopia, first-contact, ecology, colonialism, language, AI/machine-consciousness

## influences
- Her parents, anthropologists Alfred and Theodora Kroeber (Le Guin repeatedly cites their work with Ishi and with Yurok/Karuk material).
- Taoism (she produced a version of the *Tao Te Ching* in 2011), Lao Tzu specifically cited.
- Cordwainer Smith and Philip K. Dick (whom she praised and dedicated *The Lathe of Heaven* to).
- Borges, Calvino — acknowledged in her essays.

## influenced
- Essentially every subsequent feminist SF writer has engaged her work. Direct acknowledgements from Joanna Russ, Octavia Butler, Margaret Atwood (who reviewed her), Kim Stanley Robinson (her student at Clarion), N.K. Jemisin, Becky Chambers, China Miéville.
- Translated Angélica Gorodischer's *Kalpa Imperial* into English (2003) — see Gorodischer entry.
- Her essay "The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction" (1986) has been widely adopted across feminist and ecological SF criticism.

## translation history
- Translated into 40+ languages. Selected:
    - **The Left Hand of Darkness**: French (*La Main gauche de la nuit*, tr. Jean Bailhache, 1971, Laffont); Japanese (*闇の左手*, tr. 小尾芙佐 Kominami Fusa, 1978, Hayakawa); German (*Winterplanet*, tr. Gisela Stege, 1974).
    - **The Dispossessed**: French (tr. Henry-Luc Planchat, 1975); Spanish multiple editions; Russian, Polish, Japanese.
    - **The Lathe of Heaven**: French, German, Japanese, Spanish.
- Comprehensive translation bibliography maintained at ursulakleguin.com and ISFDB.

## notable recognition
- Multiple Hugos and Nebulas; Locus Awards; Gandalf Grand Master (1979); SFWA Grand Master (2003); National Book Foundation Medal for Distinguished Contribution to American Letters (2014, controversial acceptance speech).
- Only author to win both the Hugo and Nebula for the same novel twice (*Left Hand*, *Dispossessed*).

## brief bio
Ursula K. Le Guin grew up in Berkeley and the Napa Valley in a household of Kroeber-family anthropology. Her first professional SF sales came in 1962; the Hainish sequence and the Earthsea fantasy sequence began in parallel in the late 1960s. *The Left Hand of Darkness* (1969) and *The Dispossessed* (1974) established her as the dominant literary voice in late-20th-century SF, and her essays ("Is Gender Necessary?", "The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction", the introductions to her own work) reshaped the genre's critical self-understanding. She worked as a translator (Lao Tzu, Gorodischer) and as a mentor across decades. She died in Portland, Oregon, in 2018.
