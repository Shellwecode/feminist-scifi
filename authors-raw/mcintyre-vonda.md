# Vonda N. McIntyre

## name
Vonda Neel McIntyre

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1970–2019

## monumental works
- **Dreamsnake** (1978, expanded from "Of Mist, and Grass, and Sand", 1973)
    - form: novel
    - A healer on a post-nuclear Earth uses genetically engineered snakes for medicine. Hugo and Nebula winner.
- **The Exile Waiting** (1975)
    - form: novel
    - Her first novel; far-future underground city.
- **Superluminal** (1983)
    - form: novel
    - Starship pilots modified to survive FTL travel — embodiment, prosthesis, love across cognitive difference.
- **The Moon and the Sun** (1997)
    - form: novel
    - Alternate-history Louis XIV court novel with captured merfolk; Nebula winner.

## themes
biology, post-apocalypse, embodiment, space-opera, time

## influences
- Ursula K. Le Guin — close correspondent and friend; McIntyre ran the 1971 Clarion West summer workshop and Le Guin was a key early supporter.
- Joanna Russ (fellow early-70s feminist SF cohort, documented mutual friendship).

## influenced
- Co-founded Clarion West workshop in Seattle (1971) — a major teaching node for Pacific Northwest SF, touching writers from Greg Bear to Nisi Shawl.
- Nisi Shawl, Eileen Gunn, Nicola Griffith have cited the Seattle feminist-SF scene she helped sustain.

## translation history
- *Dreamsnake*: French (*Serpent du rêve*, tr. Jean Bonnefoy, 1979, Denoël); German (*Schlangenzauber*, tr. Lore Straßl, Heyne); Japanese (*夢の蛇*, tr. 友枝康子 Tomoeda Yasuko, 1985, Hayakawa); Spanish.
- *Superluminal*: French, German translations; specifics partial.

## notable recognition
- Hugo Award (1979, *Dreamsnake*).
- Nebula Awards (1973 "Of Mist, and Grass, and Sand"; 1978 *Dreamsnake*; 1997 *The Moon and the Sun*).
- Locus Award.
- Inducted into the Science Fiction and Fantasy Hall of Fame posthumously (announced 2022).

## brief bio
Vonda McIntyre wrote her PhD-adjacent studies in genetics at the University of Washington before turning to fiction full-time in 1970. She co-founded the Clarion West Writers Workshop in Seattle in 1971, which became one of the two primary US workshops for emerging SF writers. Her fiction is biologically literate, feminist, and often concerned with medical care and embodiment under technological pressure. She wrote several widely read *Star Trek* novels in the 1980s, including the novelizations of several films. She died of pancreatic cancer in 2019.
