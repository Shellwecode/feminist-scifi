# C. L. Moore

## name
Catherine Lucille Moore

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1933–1963 (primary SF period)

## monumental works
- **"Shambleau"** (1933, *Weird Tales*)
    - form: short story
    - Debut story; introduces adventurer Northwest Smith and an alien Medusa-figure on Mars. A landmark of sensuous, "weird" planetary romance.
- **"No Woman Born"** (1944, *Astounding*)
    - form: novelette
    - A famous dancer rebuilt as a cyborg after a fire; an early serious meditation on embodiment, gender, and posthumanity.
- **Jirel of Joiry stories** (1934 onward, collected *Jirel of Joiry*, 1969)
    - form: collection/short fiction
    - First major sword-and-sorcery heroine in the pulp era.
- **Judgment Night** (1952)
    - form: short novel / collection title
    - Space opera with decadent-empire overtones.

## themes
embodiment, gender, weird/horror, space-opera, AI/machine-consciousness

## influences
- Weird-fiction milieu of *Weird Tales*; influenced by A. Merritt and (by Moore's own admission in interviews) by Edgar Rice Burroughs' planetary romance.

## influenced
- Joanna Russ's *Alyx* stories and later sword-and-sorcery heroines trace to Jirel of Joiry; Russ cites Moore specifically in her critical essays.
- James Tiptree Jr. (Alice Sheldon) acknowledged Moore's "No Woman Born" as formative for her own treatments of gender and embodiment — this is documented in Julie Phillips' biography *James Tiptree, Jr.* (2006).
- Marion Zimmer Bradley and Leigh Brackett worked in the same pulp space; Brackett and Moore were friends.

## translation history
- Stories translated in French, German, Japanese, Spanish anthologies from the 1950s onward. Exact translator/year records are scattered across anthology imprints (e.g. *Shambleau et autres récits*, French, Denoël, 1975, tr. various).
- Full translation bibliography difficult to assemble in English sources — flag for gaps.md.

## notable recognition
- World Fantasy Award for Life Achievement (1981).
- Gandalf Grand Master (1981).
- Inducted into the Science Fiction and Fantasy Hall of Fame (1998).

## brief bio
C. L. Moore sold "Shambleau" to *Weird Tales* in 1933 while working as a bank secretary in Indianapolis; it was a sensation. She married fellow writer Henry Kuttner in 1940 and for the next decade they collaborated intensively, publishing under a swarm of pseudonyms (Lewis Padgett, Lawrence O'Donnell). "No Woman Born" (1944) is now routinely cited as a foundational cyborg narrative, prefiguring feminist embodiment fiction by three decades. After Kuttner's death in 1958 she largely left SF for television work. Her late life was shadowed by Alzheimer's; she died in 1987.
