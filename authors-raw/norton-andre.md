# Andre Norton

## name
Andre Alice Norton (born Alice Mary Norton)

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1934–2005

## monumental works
- **Witch World** (1963)
    - form: novel
    - Founding volume of a long portal-fantasy / SF-fantasy hybrid cycle; women's magic and matrilineal societies central.
- **Daybreak — 2250 A.D.** (a.k.a. *Star Man's Son*, 1952)
    - form: novel
    - Post-nuclear quest novel; one of the first SF bestsellers aimed at younger readers.
- **The Beast Master** (1959)
    - form: novel
    - Native-American-coded protagonist with telepathically bonded animals on a colonised alien world.
- **The Time Traders** (1958)
    - form: novel
    - Cold-War-era time-travel agents.

## themes
post-apocalypse, time, colonialism, family/kinship, space-opera

## influences
- Pulp adventure (Burroughs), historical romance, Native American material culture (uneven; a product of its era).

## influenced
- Her male-coded byline ("Andre") was adopted in part to sell to boys' adventure markets and became a reference point for later writers who wrote under ambiguous names.
- Often cited by later writers — Mercedes Lackey, Lois McMaster Bujold, C.J. Cherryh — as a childhood gateway author. Bujold has repeatedly cited Norton in interviews (e.g. Locus and SF Signal).
- Tanith Lee, Marion Zimmer Bradley, and many YA-adjacent SF writers of the 1970s–80s explicitly cite Norton.

## translation history
- Widely translated into German, French, Russian, Japanese, Polish, Dutch from the 1960s onward. Multiple Russian translations of the Witch World books circulated in the late Soviet era.
- Translator/year specifics vary by volume; some partial records on ISFDB.

## notable recognition
- First woman to be named SFWA Grand Master (1984).
- First woman inducted into the Science Fiction Hall of Fame (1997).
- World Fantasy Life Achievement Award (1998).
- The Andre Norton Nebula Award for YA SF/fantasy was established in her honour (2005).

## brief bio
Andre Norton, born Alice Mary Norton in Cleveland, legally changed her name partly in response to editorial pressure to appear male in the adventure market. She worked as a librarian for the Cleveland Public Library for two decades while publishing prolifically — ultimately more than 130 novels. Her work stitched SF and fantasy together when the genres were more strictly divided, and the Witch World cycle (begun 1963) was one of the first long-running SFF series helmed by a woman. She mentored a generation of women SFF writers; her home in Murfreesboro, Tennessee, housed the "High Hallack" library for genre writers. She died in 2005.
