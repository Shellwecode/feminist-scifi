# Ogawa Yōko

## name
Ogawa Yōko / 小川洋子

## primary writing language
Japanese

## country / region
Japan (East Asian)

## years active
1988–present

## monumental works
- **『密やかな結晶』 *Hisoyaka na Kesshō* (*The Memory Police*) (1994)**
    - form: novel
    - On an unnamed island, objects — and then body parts — disappear from the world and from memory; the Memory Police enforce the forgetting. A central late-20th-century dystopia.
- **『博士の愛した数式』 *Hakase no Ai shita Sūshiki* (*The Housekeeper and the Professor*) (2003)**
    - form: novel
    - Amnesiac mathematics professor with 80-minute memory; not SF but formally central to her speculative sensibility.
- **『妊娠カレンダー』 *Ninshin Karendā* (*Pregnancy Diary*) (1990)**
    - form: novella
    - Unsettling gestational story; Akutagawa Prize winner.
- **『ホテル・アイリス』 *Hoteru Airisu* (*Hotel Iris*) (1996)**
    - form: novel
    - Psychosexual drama; weird-inflected.

## themes
dystopia, weird/horror, embodiment, family/kinship, biology

## influences
- Paul Auster (she has cited in interviews); Anne Frank's *Diary* (she visited the Amsterdam house and has written about its influence).
- Murakami Haruki — often compared, acknowledged as a peer though not always directly cited.

## influenced
- *The Memory Police* has had a major second life in English after Stephen Snyder's 2019 translation; it is now widely taught in Anglophone courses on totalitarian dystopia, often paired with Atwood and Orwell.

## translation history
- **The Memory Police**: translated to English by **Stephen Snyder**, Pantheon (US) / Harvill Secker (UK), **2019** — 25 years after original publication. Shortlisted for the International Booker Prize (2020) and National Book Award (Translated Literature, 2019).
- **The Housekeeper and the Professor**: tr. Stephen Snyder, Picador, 2009.
- **Hotel Iris**: tr. Stephen Snyder, Picador, 2010.
- **The Diving Pool** (novella collection): tr. Stephen Snyder, Picador, 2008.
- French: widely translated by Rose-Marie Makino-Fayolle (Actes Sud).
- German, Italian, Spanish, Korean, Chinese editions widely available.

## notable recognition
- Akutagawa Prize (1990, *Pregnancy Diary*).
- Tanizaki Prize (2003).
- Shirley Jackson Award shortlist.
- International Booker Prize shortlist (2020, *The Memory Police*).
- National Book Award for Translated Literature shortlist (2019).

## brief bio
Ogawa Yōko was born in Okayama, studied at Waseda University, and won the Akutagawa Prize for "Pregnancy Diary" (1990). Her fiction is quiet, methodical, and often deeply unsettling; classification between literary fiction and SF is genuinely difficult for her and she is rarely marketed as a genre writer in Japan. The late-career English arrival of *The Memory Police* in 2019 — a novel that anticipates 2010s/20s discussions about erasure, memory, and authoritarianism — reshaped her international reputation. She lives in Ashiya, Hyōgo Prefecture.
