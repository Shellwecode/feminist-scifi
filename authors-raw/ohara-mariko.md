# Ōhara Mariko

## name
Ōhara Mariko / 大原まり子

## primary writing language
Japanese

## country / region
Japan (East Asian)

## years active
1980–present

## monumental works
- **『ハイブリッド・チャイルド』 *Haiburiddo Chairudo* (*Hybrid Child*) (1990)**
    - form: novel
    - A shape-shifting "sample" organism and a child refuse a cybernetic empire. Nihon SF Taishō winner; English translation by Jodie Beck (University of Minnesota Press, 2018).
- **『戦争を演じた神々たち』 *Sensō o Enjita Kamigami-tachi* (*The Gods Who Played at War*) (1998)**
    - form: novel
    - Mythic-SF of generational trauma and cosmic-scale conflict.
- **「アクアプラネット」 ("Aqua Planet")** (1990s short story)
    - form: short story
    - Space opera short; frequently anthologised in Japan.
- **『メンタル・フィメール』 *Mental Female* (1988)**
    - form: short-story collection
    - Early feminist cyberpunk collection; establishes the "cyberpunk vs. feminism" register of her work.

## themes
cyberpunk, AI/machine-consciousness, gender, embodiment, family/kinship, biology

## influences
- Alfred Bester (cited in interviews as a core model for her SF imagination).
- Philip K. Dick, J. G. Ballard — acknowledged by Ōhara in her nonfiction.
- James Tiptree Jr.; Ōhara translated Tiptree's work into Japanese in collaboration with Kotani Mari.

## influenced
- Together with Kotani Mari, Ōhara shaped the Japanese feminist-SF critical formation of the 1990s.
- Frequent mentor and anthologist; edited anthologies with Kotani.

## translation history
- **Hybrid Child**: translated to English by Jodie Beck, University of Minnesota Press, 2018 — the first full Ōhara novel in English. Minnesota's "Parallel Futures" series.
- Short stories in English: "Girl" in *Speculative Japan* anthologies (Kurodahan Press, various volumes, 2000s–2010s; translators vary including Alfred Birnbaum).
- French: selected stories in *La Nouvelle SF japonaise*.
- Korean: selected stories translated; translator records partial.

## notable recognition
- Nihon SF Taishō (1990, *Hybrid Child*).
- Seiun Award multiple times (Japan's SF fan award, equivalent of the Hugo).

## brief bio
Ōhara Mariko debuted in the early 1980s in *SF Magajin* and became one of the defining voices of late-1980s/1990s Japanese feminist SF. Her fiction imports cyberpunk textures into questions of motherhood, AI personhood, and bodily mutability. With critic Kotani Mari she co-organised the Japanese feminist-SF "WisCon-adjacent" discourse of the 1990s. *Hybrid Child* (1990), finally translated into English in 2018, remains her most internationally visible novel. She lives in Tokyo.
