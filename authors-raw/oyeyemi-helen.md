# Helen Oyeyemi

## name
Helen Olajumoke Oyeyemi

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
Nigeria / United Kingdom (African diaspora)

## years active
2005–present

## monumental works
- **The Icarus Girl** (2005)
    - form: novel
    - An English-Nigerian girl meets a spirit companion in Nigeria. Debut, written while Oyeyemi was still at secondary school.
- **White Is for Witching** (2009)
    - form: novel
    - A haunted Dover guesthouse; generations of white women eating their daughters. Somerset Maugham Award.
- **Mr Fox** (2011)
    - form: novel
    - A novelist and his muse in conversation across stories that keep killing women; meta-Bluebeard. Hurston/Wright Legacy Award.
- **Peaces** (2021)
    - form: novel
    - A honeymoon aboard a mysterious, possibly-haunted luxury train.

## themes
weird/horror, gender, family/kinship, race, religion/myth

## influences
- Yoruba folklore (Oyeyemi has cited *The Palm-Wine Drinkard*, the abiku tradition).
- European fairy tale traditions (Bluebeard, Grimm) — openly reworked.
- Emily Dickinson, the Brontës — acknowledged.

## influenced
- A recognizable "literary fairy tale" current in 2010s–20s English-language fiction (Carmen Maria Machado, Kelly Link, Sofia Samatar) inherits Oyeyemi's fractured-tale form; citations appear in interviews and acknowledgements.

## translation history
- Widely translated. **The Icarus Girl**: French, German, Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, Polish.
- **Mr Fox**: French (*Mister Fox*, Galaade, tr. Guillaume Villeneuve); German (Penguin / Arche); Japanese (tr. details partial).
- **White Is for Witching**: French, German, Spanish, Italian, Japanese.
- Translator attribution well-documented in publisher catalogs.

## notable recognition
- Named one of *Granta*'s Best Young British Novelists (2013).
- Somerset Maugham Award (2010).
- Hurston/Wright Legacy Award (2012).
- PEN/Open Book Award.

## brief bio
Helen Oyeyemi was born in Ibadan, Nigeria, and raised in London. She wrote her debut *The Icarus Girl* during her A-level year and published it at 20. Her work is genre-slippery, often read as literary fiction with speculative or weird elements — in this dataset she is included because her fiction is structured around fantastic premises (hauntings, shape-shifting, folkloric premises) and is regularly taught alongside Black diaspora speculative writers. She has lived in Prague, Paris, Berlin, and New York. Her writing moves with cosmopolitan fluency across fairy-tale and folkloric sources.
