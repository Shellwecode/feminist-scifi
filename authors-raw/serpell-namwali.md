# Namwali Serpell

## name
Namwali Serpell

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
Zambia / United States (African diaspora)

## years active
2010–present

## monumental works
- **The Old Drift** (2019)
    - form: novel
    - Multi-generational Zambian novel spanning colonial settlement to a drone-saturated near-future Lusaka; three families entangled across more than a century. Arthur C. Clarke Award (2020). Anisfield-Wolf Book Award.
- **The Furrows: An Elegy** (2022)
    - form: novel
    - Grief-novel with speculative doubling: a young woman is haunted by reappearances of her lost brother.
- **Stranger Faces** (2020)
    - form: essay collection
    - Criticism; includes speculative threads.

## themes
post-apocalypse, race, colonialism, power, family/kinship, ecology, time

## influences
- David Foster Wallace (Serpell wrote her doctoral dissertation at Harvard on Wallace, Tim O'Brien, and uncertainty).
- Toni Morrison — acknowledged in Serpell's essays.
- Zambian oral traditions and colonial-history documentary sources.

## influenced
- Cited by Anglophone African literary critics as a leading contemporary African SF/literary novelist; the Clarke Award result helped position *The Old Drift* as a key work of the African speculative-literary turn.

## translation history
- **The Old Drift**: French (*Les Enfants du Zambèze*, tr. Sika Fakambi, Laffont, 2021); Italian (Einaudi); German (*Der alte Fluss*, Suhrkamp), Portuguese, Dutch, Spanish.
- Widely translated for a debut novel; translator attribution well-documented.

## notable recognition
- Arthur C. Clarke Award (2020, *The Old Drift*).
- Anisfield-Wolf Book Award (2020).
- Windham-Campbell Prize (2020).
- Caine Prize for African Writing (2015, "The Sack").
- Named one of *Granta*'s Best of Young American Novelists (2017).

## brief bio
Namwali Serpell was born in Zambia in 1980 and moved to the US as a child. She holds a PhD from Harvard and teaches literature at Harvard; she was previously on the faculty at UC Berkeley. *The Old Drift* — a 400-year Zambian novel combining colonial history, biotechnology, and a near-future drone insurgency — won the Arthur C. Clarke Award for science fiction in 2020, unusual for a literary-marketed novel. Her critical work on uncertainty in fiction is substantial and has been collected in *Seven Modes of Uncertainty* (2014) and *Stranger Faces* (2020).
