# Nisi Shawl

## name
Nisi Shawl

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (African diaspora)

## years active
1989–present

## monumental works
- **Everfair** (2016)
    - form: novel
    - Alternate-history steampunk: the Congo Free State is bought out by British socialists and African-American expatriates to create a free nation that repels King Leopold. Nebula shortlist.
- **Kinning** (2024)
    - form: novel
    - Sequel to *Everfair*, carrying the alternate-history forward into the 1920s.
- **Writing the Other: A Practical Approach** (2005, with Cynthia Ward)
    - form: craft book
    - Widely used pedagogical text on writing across racial/cultural difference.
- **"Deep End" (story)** and other stories in *Filter House* (2008)
    - form: short-story collection
    - James Tiptree Jr. Award (2009) for *Filter House*.

## themes
race, colonialism, power, gender, post-apocalypse, utopia

## influences
- Samuel R. Delany — Shawl has cited Delany in multiple interviews and essays.
- Octavia E. Butler — direct acknowledgement; Shawl was a Clarion West student (1992) when Butler was teaching.
- Ursula K. Le Guin — acknowledged.

## influenced
- Co-founder of the Carl Brandon Society (1999), which established the Octavia E. Butler Scholarship at Clarion/Clarion West and the Parallax/Kindred Awards for speculative fiction by/about people of colour.
- *Writing the Other* has trained two decades of SFF writers in craft approaches to cross-cultural representation.

## translation history
- *Everfair*: partial European translation coverage; specifics are sparser than her award footprint would suggest.
- **Flag**: *Writing the Other* circulates widely in English-language writing communities but translations are limited.

## notable recognition
- James Tiptree Jr. Award (2009, *Filter House*).
- Kate Wilhelm Solstice Award (2020, for service to the SF community).
- World Fantasy Award for Life Achievement (2023).
- Locus Award, Nebula shortlists.

## brief bio
Nisi Shawl was raised in Kalamazoo, Michigan, attended Clarion West in 1992, and has lived in Seattle since. Her work as a fiction writer has been matched by decades of institutional labour: co-founder of the Carl Brandon Society, editor of the *Bloodchildren* anthology honouring Octavia Butler scholarship recipients, long-running review editor at *The Seattle Times* and *Cascadia Subduction Zone*. The 2023 World Fantasy Award for Life Achievement reflects both her fiction and this broader field-shaping work. She is disabled and has written publicly about disability and chronic illness in SFF.
