# Mary Shelley

## name
Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley (née Godwin)

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United Kingdom (Anglophone)

## years active
1797–1851

## monumental works
- **Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus** (1818; revised 1831)
    - form: novel
    - Generally held to be the first work of science fiction in the modern sense: a scientist animates a being from dead matter and is destroyed by the consequences.
- **The Last Man** (1826)
    - form: novel
    - End-of-the-world narrative set in a 21st-century plague pandemic; an early example of apocalyptic SF.

## themes
embodiment, biology, dystopia, post-apocalypse, gender, religion/myth

## influences
- Her father William Godwin and mother Mary Wollstonecraft (radical political thought; Wollstonecraft's feminism shapes Frankenstein's critique of creation without care).
- Milton's *Paradise Lost*, Coleridge, Erasmus Darwin's scientific poetry, and galvanism — all cited in Shelley's 1831 introduction to *Frankenstein*.

## influenced
- Named explicitly by later SF writers and critics as the genre's progenitor. Brian Aldiss argues in *Billion Year Spree* (1973) that SF begins with Shelley — a canonical framing that many women SF writers (Russ, Le Guin, Haraway) have echoed or contested.
- Donna Haraway's cyborg theory and Octavia Butler's Lilith's Brood both engage *Frankenstein* as a foundational text on made bodies and responsibility.

## translation history
- **Frankenstein**: translated into essentially every major world language, often many times over. Notable early translation: French, by Jules Saladin (1821). German translations from the 19th century onward; multiple modern Japanese, Chinese, Russian, Spanish, Arabic versions. ISFDB and the Mary Shelley Chronology list dozens.
- **The Last Man**: less widely translated; French and Spanish editions exist but it remained out of print for long stretches before its 20th-century rediscovery.

## notable recognition
- Canonical status as a founder of the science fiction genre.
- *Frankenstein* continuously in print since 1818; foundational text in gothic, horror, and SF traditions.

## brief bio
Mary Shelley was the daughter of political philosopher William Godwin and feminist writer Mary Wollstonecraft. She began *Frankenstein* at 18 during the 1816 "year without a summer" at Lake Geneva, prompted by a ghost-story challenge from Byron. After the drownings of her husband Percy and several of her children, she supported herself and her surviving son through writing — novels, biographies, and travel literature. *The Last Man*, published in 1826, is now recognised as an important early apocalyptic novel. Her status as the originator of science fiction was consolidated in the 20th century, particularly by Aldiss's argument in *Billion Year Spree*.
