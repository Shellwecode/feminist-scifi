# Johanna Sinisalo

## name
Johanna Sinisalo

## primary writing language
Finnish

## country / region
Finland (European non-English)

## years active
1985–present

## monumental works
- **Ennen päivänlaskua ei voi** (*Not Before Sundown* / US: *Troll: A Love Story*) (2000)
    - form: novel
    - A young man in Tampere finds a juvenile troll in an alley and takes it home; Finnish folklore's *peikko* is reconceived as a wild species living at the edges of modernity.
- **Auringon ydin** (*The Core of the Sun*) (2013)
    - form: novel
    - Alternate-reality Finland — a "eusistocratic" state where women have been selectively bred for docility and capsaicin is the major recreational drug.
- **Enkelten verta** (*Blood of Angels*) (2011)
    - form: novel
    - Apiarist whose son committed eco-terrorism observes colony-collapse disorder and a spiritual rupture.
- **Sankarit** (*Heroes*) (2003)
    - form: novel
    - Kalevala retold through contemporary mass media.

## themes
ecology, gender, religion/myth, biology, weird/horror, dystopia

## influences
- The Kalevala and Finnish folk tradition (explicit in *Not Before Sundown* and *Sankarit*).
- Tove Jansson — cited.
- Anglo-American feminist SF (Atwood, Le Guin) — acknowledged.

## influenced
- Central figure of the "Finnish Weird" (*suomikumma*) movement — along with Leena Krohn, Pasi Ilmari Jääskeläinen, Maria Turtschaninoff. The term and the movement's self-curation come from Sinisalo's essay work.
- Her Finlandia Prize win (2000) for a genre-clearly-speculative novel was historic — the first time the Finlandia went to a work often classed as SF/fantasy.

## translation history

- **The Core of the Sun**: English tr. **Lola Rogers**, Grove Atlantic, 2016.

## notable recognition
- Finlandia Prize (2000, *Ennen päivänlaskua ei voi*).
- James Tiptree Jr. Award (2004, *Not Before Sundown*).
- Prometheus Award nomination.
- Atorox Award (Finnish SF, multiple).

## brief bio
Johanna Sinisalo was born in Sodankylä, Lapland in 1958. She worked in advertising in Tampere while building a reputation in Finnish-language SF short fiction in the 1980s and 1990s. *Not Before Sundown* (2000) — her debut novel — won the Finlandia Prize, the first clearly speculative work to do so. Her articulation of a "Finnish Weird" aesthetic has been influential in the 2010s Nordic speculative-fiction formation. She is also a screenwriter and essayist; she lives in Tampere.
