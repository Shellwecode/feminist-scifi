# Suzuki Izumi

## name
Suzuki Izumi / 鈴木いづみ (sometimes 鈴木いず美)

## primary writing language
Japanese

## country / region
Japan (East Asian)

## years active
1969–1986

## monumental works
- **「女と女の世の中」 ("Women and Women")** (1977)
    - form: short story
    - An all-female society where men are kept in a reservation; female reproduction is managed artificially.
- **「魔女見習い」 ("Trial Witch")** (1975)
    - form: short story
    - Domestic surrealism: a bored housewife is granted shape-changing power.
- **『恋には至らず』 (c. 1983)**
    - form: short-story collection
    - Late-career collection; includes "Forgotten," "You May Dream," and other frequently reprinted stories.
- **Terminal Boredom** (English-language collection, 2021; original stories 1970s–80s)
    - form: short-story collection (English assembly)
    - Selection translated by Polly Barton, Sam Bett, David Boyd, Daniel Joseph, Aiko Masubuchi, Helen O'Horan; published by Verso (UK) and Verso (US) 2021.

## themes
gender, dystopia, embodiment, biology, family/kinship, weird/horror

## influences
- Counterculture of 1970s Japan; J. G. Ballard, Philip K. Dick (often cited by Japanese SF scholars as reference points for Suzuki — e.g., in Dani Cavallaro's *Japanese Science Fiction*, 2010, and in translator Polly Barton's introductory notes).
- The "New Wave" of Japanese SF centred on *SF Magajin* (SF Magazine, Hayakawa).

## influenced
- A cult figure during her lifetime; her reputation in Anglophone SF is entirely a 2020s phenomenon, driven by the *Terminal Boredom* (2021) and *Hit Parade of Tears* (2023) Verso translations.
- Cited by Japanese feminist SF critics as a precursor to Ōhara Mariko. Direct self-reported influence chains are sparse in English.

## translation history
- **Terminal Boredom: Stories** (2021): translated by Polly Barton, Sam Bett, David Boyd, Daniel Joseph, Aiko Masubuchi, and Helen O'Horan. Published by Verso.
- **Hit Parade of Tears: Stories** (2023): translated by Sam Bett, David Boyd, Helen O'Horan, and Daniel Joseph. Published by Verso / Verso UK.
- Prior to 2021 her work was essentially unavailable in English.
- French translation: selected stories in anthologies; some in *La Nouvelle SF japonaise*.
- Korean: selected stories translated; details partial.

## notable recognition
- Cult status within Japan; public visibility as much through her acting and modelling (she appeared in ATG-label films by Wakamatsu Kōji) as through her fiction during her lifetime.
- Posthumous English-language canonisation after the 2021 translation.

## brief bio
Suzuki Izumi worked as a bar hostess, nude model, and actor in the late 1960s Tokyo counterculture before she began writing. She married the free-jazz saxophonist Abe Kaoru in 1973; Abe died of a drug overdose in 1978, after which Suzuki raised their daughter alone while continuing to write. Her work is claustrophobic, genderqueer, often set in domestic interiors collapsing into surreal or SF premises. She died by suicide in Tokyo in 1986, aged 36. The English translations beginning in 2021 have made her a rediscovered figure in Anglophone feminist SF.
