# Tang Fei

## name
Tang Fei / 糖匪 (pen name)

## primary writing language
Chinese (Mandarin)

## country / region
China (East Asian)

## years active
mid-2000s–present

## monumental works
- **「黄色故事」 ("Call Girl") (2013, English tr. 2015)**
    - form: short story
    - A schoolgirl's secret work as a "call girl" who provides narrative experience rather than sex; liminal dream-SF. English tr. Ken Liu.
- **「伤口」 ("The Path to Freedom")** — various other short stories in Chinese SF magazines.
- **"Pepe" (short story, English tr. John Chu, *Clarkesworld* 2017)**
    - form: short story
- **"The Three Secrets of the Magic Forest"** — fantasy/SF.

## themes
weird/horror, gender, power, embodiment, first-contact

## influences
- Jorge Luis Borges, Italo Calvino — cited by Tang Fei in interviews as formative models.
- The Chinese literary "slipstream" tradition; her work is closer to *Asimov's*-style literary SF than to Liu Cixin-style hard SF.

## influenced
- Part of the 2010s "new wave" Chinese-SF cohort (Xia Jia, Hao Jingfang, Chen Qiufan, Gu Shi); mutual influence within this group is documented in anthology introductions but rarely spelled out as direct influence.

## translation history
- "Call Girl": English tr. **Ken Liu**, *Apex Magazine*, June 2013; also reprinted in *Invisible Planets* (Tor, 2016).
- "Pepe": English tr. **John Chu**, *Clarkesworld* #133, October 2017.
- Several stories in *Clarkesworld* Chinese translation project and in *Broken Stars* (2019, ed. Ken Liu).
- French: *Invisible Planets* and *Broken Stars* in Gwennaël Gaffric's translations (Actes Sud).
- Italian, Spanish anthologies.

## notable recognition
- Yinhe (Galaxy) Award nomination.
- Selected for *Invisible Planets* (2016) — one of the works that introduced contemporary Chinese SF to Anglophone readers.

## brief bio
Tang Fei is a Shanghai-based writer whose work sits closer to the literary and fantastic edge of Chinese SF than to hard-SF genre conventions. Her short stories inhabit dreamscapes, liminal urban spaces, and genre-fluid settings. Because she uses a pen name and does not appear frequently in English-language press, her biographical detail in English sources is thin — this is noted as a gap. She has written for *Science Fiction World* and other mainstream Chinese SF magazines and has been a regular presence in *Clarkesworld*'s translation programme.
