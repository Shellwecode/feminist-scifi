# James Tiptree Jr. (Alice B. Sheldon)

## name
Alice Bradley Sheldon, writing as James Tiptree Jr. and (less often) Raccoona Sheldon

## primary writing language
English

## country / region
United States (Anglophone)

## years active
1967–1987

## monumental works
- **"The Women Men Don't See"** (1973)
    - form: novelette
    - Two women choose to leave Earth with aliens rather than remain in patriarchal society.
- **"Houston, Houston, Do You Read?"** (1976)
    - form: novella
    - Three male astronauts time-slip into a future where men have died out; the society of women decides what to do with them.
- **"The Screwfly Solution"** (1977, as Raccoona Sheldon)
    - form: short story
    - A chemical manipulation of male sexual aggression turns femicide into extinction.
- **"Love Is the Plan the Plan Is Death"** (1973)
    - form: short story
    - Alien mating biology told from inside the creature's psyche.

## themes
gender, biology, first-contact, dystopia, embodiment, ecology

## influences
- Early reading of pulp SF; Sheldon's CIA/military background shaped the cold hard-SF surface of her stories (documented in Julie Phillips' biography).
- C. L. Moore's "No Woman Born" (direct acknowledgement — Phillips 2006).

## influenced
- Le Guin, Russ, and Sheldon corresponded extensively as "Tiptree"; the revelation that Tiptree was a woman (1976–77) shaped the feminist-SF discourse directly.
- The James Tiptree Jr. Award (est. 1991; renamed the Otherwise Award in 2019) institutionalised the feminist-SF tradition Tiptree helped crystallise.
- Direct influence claimed by Nicola Griffith, Kelly Link, Karen Joy Fowler, and others in introductions to reprints.

## translation history
- Collections translated into French (*Par-delà les murs du monde*, various translators, Denoël), German (*Doktor Ain*, Heyne; tr. Jürgen Langowski and others), Japanese (Hayakawa; tr. 伊藤典夫 Ito Norio on several stories), Russian anthologies.
- "Houston, Houston, Do You Read?" anthologised in many languages; translator records partial.

## notable recognition
- Two Hugo Awards, three Nebula Awards.
- Pilgrim Award for SF scholarship posthumously (indirectly, via award-naming).
- The Tiptree/Otherwise Award (est. 1991) honours SF exploring gender.

## brief bio
Alice B. Sheldon was raised in Chicago and spent parts of her childhood on her mother's ethnographic expeditions in colonial Africa. She served as a US Army Air Forces photointelligence officer in WWII, later worked for the CIA, and earned a PhD in experimental psychology. She began publishing as James Tiptree Jr. in 1967 to keep her academic identity separate; the pseudonym was unmasked in 1977 after the death of her mother, when a Chicago obituary linked the names. Her late-career work turned darker; she killed her infirm husband and then herself in 1987. Her letters with Russ, Le Guin, and Sheldon herself are a central document of 1970s feminist SF.
