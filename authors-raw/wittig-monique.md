# Monique Wittig

## name
Monique Wittig

## primary writing language
French

## country / region
France (European non-English)

## years active
1964–2003

## monumental works
- **Les Guérillères** (1969)
    - form: novel
    - Women wage a mythic war against patriarchal order; a prose-poem collage with grammatical insurrection (the pronoun *elles* as universal subject). Foundational text of radical feminist SF.
- **Le Corps lesbien** (*The Lesbian Body*) (1973)
    - form: novel / prose-poetry
    - Dismembered, re-membered lesbian embodiment; writing the body against patriarchal grammar.
- **L'Opoponax** (1964)
    - form: novel
    - Debut novel; Prix Médicis winner. Not strictly SF but formally central to her speculative project.
- **Virgile, non** (*Across the Acheron*) (1985)
    - form: novel
    - Paris recast as a Dantean lesbian hell and paradise.

## themes
gender, utopia, language, power, embodiment, religion/myth

## influences
- Simone de Beauvoir — cited; Wittig's essays in *The Straight Mind* extend and critique Beauvoir's positions on the category "woman."
- Gertrude Stein, Djuna Barnes — acknowledged formal influences.
- Marxist materialism (Wittig's essay "One Is Not Born a Woman" is dedicated to Simone de Beauvoir and engages directly with materialist feminism).

## influenced
- Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* (1990) engages Wittig extensively and credits her as a precursor to performative gender theory.
- Donna Haraway's "A Cyborg Manifesto" cites Wittig alongside Russ and Butler as a feminist-SF predecessor.
- Nicole Brossard and the Québécois feminist-SF tradition.
- Joanna Russ referred to *Les Guérillères* in her essays on feminist utopia; the text is a recognized ancestor of *The Female Man*.

## translation history
- **Les Guérillères**: English tr. **David Le Vay**, Peter Owen (UK) / Viking (US), 1971.
- **The Lesbian Body**: English tr. David Le Vay, Morrow, 1975.
- **Across the Acheron**: English tr. David Le Vay & Margaret Crosland, Peter Owen, 1987.
- Translated into German (Suhrkamp), Spanish (Pre-Textos), Italian, Portuguese, Dutch, Swedish, Japanese, Korean.

## notable recognition
- Prix Médicis (1964, *L'Opoponax*).
- Posthumous canonisation in queer-theory and feminist-SF curricula.
- *Les Guérillères* is routinely listed among key feminist-SF texts of the 20th century.

## brief bio
Monique Wittig grew up in Alsace, moved to Paris in 1950, and was a founding member of the *Mouvement de libération des femmes* (MLF). Her 1964 debut won the Prix Médicis; *Les Guérillères* in 1969 shifted her into radical feminist-SF territory that her later essays (*The Straight Mind*, 1992) theorised. Her position — that "lesbians are not women" because the category "woman" is a product of heterosexual social relations — put her in productive conflict with French feminism of the era. She emigrated to the US in 1976 and taught at UC Berkeley and the University of Arizona. She died of a heart attack in Tucson in 2003.
