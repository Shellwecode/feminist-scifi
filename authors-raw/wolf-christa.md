# Christa Wolf

## name
Christa Wolf (née Ihlenfeld)

## primary writing language
German

## country / region
East Germany (GDR) / Germany (European non-English)

## years active
1961–2010

## monumental works
- **Kassandra** (*Cassandra*) (1983)
    - form: novel
    - The Trojan War retold from Cassandra's prison-cell monologue; accompanying *Frankfurt Lectures* (*Voraussetzungen einer Erzählung*) theorise a feminist mythic SF. Clark Award shortlist-adjacent and Buchmesse Friedenspreis.
- **Störfall: Nachrichten eines Tages** (*Accident: A Day's News*) (1987)
    - form: novel
    - A woman in an East German village lives through the day of the Chernobyl disaster while her brother undergoes brain surgery; nuclear/ecological SF.
- **Kein Ort. Nirgends** (*No Place on Earth*) (1979)
    - form: novella
    - Imagined meeting of Heinrich von Kleist and Karoline von Günderrode — borderline speculative.
- **Medea: Stimmen** (*Medea: A Modern Retelling*) (1996)
    - form: novel
    - Medea re-voiced from Colchian exile; speculative-mythic.

## themes
war, religion/myth, ecology, gender, power, post-apocalypse

## influences
- Anna Seghers — literary mentor, documented correspondence (*Das Jahrhundert Anna Seghers*, 1989).
- Ingeborg Bachmann — literary dialogue, especially around *Kassandra*.
- Classical Greek material (Wolf's engagement with Homer and Euripides is methodical and essayed).

## influenced
- Her position as one of the GDR's most visible literary figures — simultaneously party-member and critic — made her a reference point for late-Cold-War politically engaged SF and mythic writing.
- *Kassandra* has been widely taught alongside Le Guin and Atwood in feminist-mythic-SF curricula.
- Ursula K. Le Guin's 2001 *Virginia Quarterly Review* essay on *Cassandra* endorsed its place in feminist SF.

## translation history
- **Cassandra**: English tr. **Jan van Heurck**, Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1984. French (*Cassandre*, tr. Alain Lance & Renate Lance-Otterbein, Aufbau/Alinéa, 1984); Italian (*Cassandra*, tr. Anita Raja, e/o); Spanish, Dutch, Polish, Japanese, Korean.
- **Accident**: English tr. Heike Schwarzbauer & Rick Takvorian, Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1989.
- **No Place on Earth**: English tr. Jan van Heurck, Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1982.
- **Medea**: English tr. John Cullen, Nan A. Talese/Doubleday, 1998.
- Very widely translated; Wolf is among the most translated GDR authors.

## notable recognition
- Büchner Prize (1980).
- Thomas Mann Prize (2010).
- Friedenspreis des Deutschen Buchhandels (2002).
- Nominated multiple times for the Nobel Prize (public speculation).

## brief bio
Christa Wolf was one of the most important writers of the GDR and of reunited Germany. She joined the SED in 1949, held critical positions within and against East German cultural politics, and in 1993 the disclosure that she had briefly cooperated with the Stasi in the 1950s (as an "informal collaborator," 1959–62) triggered major controversy. Her speculative and mythic novels — *Cassandra*, *Accident*, *Medea* — used classical and contemporary catastrophe to examine war, gender, and political complicity. She died in Berlin in 2011.
