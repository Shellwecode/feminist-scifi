Opening

This museum begins with what got overlooked.

Not with a canon, not with a definition of feminist science fiction, not with a list of who counts. With three reading experiences — a child's, a student's, an adult's — and the small private moment in each one where presence comes into focus. Where a writer who had been there all along becomes visible because the room finally has light in the right corner.

The argument, if there is one, is that this is what the tradition does. Feminist SF makes presence visible where it had been overlooked. The form of this site is meant to do the same thing. You are about to walk through the act, not the survey.

Begin with Zhang Xiaofeng.

V1 · 张晓风 · 1968

I was eight when I first read 张晓风. 《我在》 was the one that opened my world. There was a period of time I was obsessed with writing the character 在 (zài). It means being here and present. Reading《我在》, the piece built on a single word, I saw the way her sentences moved and connected the small dignity of a child's presence to the bigger world around her.

I read her as 散文家, or an essayist. The school curriculum had filed her there. So had every anthology I'd ever pulled off a shelf.

Today I learned she also wrote 《潘渡娜》 in 1968. Pandora, in the Taiwanese transliteration; the mainland would later render it 潘多拉. The story, published in 中国时报 on September 12 of that year, is now recognized in Taiwanese SF circles as the first piece of Chinese-language science fiction. 1968. A year before Le Guin's The Left Hand of Darkness. Years before almost anything English-language readers think of as the start of feminist SF.

She wrote it in a room that hadn't been built. The terms 科幻小说 and 科幻文学 didn't crystallize as stable categories in Taiwan until around 1980. When 《潘渡娜》 appeared, "Taiwanese SF" was not yet a thing the field could see. Two other writers — 张系国 and 黄海 — published their first SF stories that same year. Both later became 鼻祖 of Taiwan SF: founding figures, biographies built around the fact. The third writer was filed as 散文家 and stayed there.

So which is it: was the genre invisible to her, or was she invisible to the genre?

V2 · 夏笳 / Ken Liu · 2012

During my first year of college, I took a writing class on Weird Fiction. I read Xia Jia's 《百鬼夜行街》, A Hundred Ghosts Parade Tonight, first in Ken Liu's translation. It was the first time I'd read a Chinese writer in English before reading her in Chinese.

At the time, Ken Liu's name felt more visible because he had translated the well-known Three Body Problem series into English. I learned about Xia Jia and her stories through him. This was an inverted reading experience: I met the translator before I met the author, even though all three of us move between Chinese and English.

Reading 《百鬼夜行街》in English made me ponder the human-robot spectrum and the meaning of being human. But the Chinese version, with its extremely poetic language, added another register that reads more like fantasy. “假作真时真亦假” pulls me into the dreamy world 《红楼梦》fabricates.

Looking back, how much of what I thought 《百鬼夜行街》 was about came from Xia Jia, and how much from Ken Liu's hand?

V3 · Porridge SF

After my Weird Fiction semester, I read an interview where Xia Jia called her own work “porridge SF.” Not hard SF. Something she had to invent a name for.

Little me, reading in Chinese first, would have called 《百鬼夜行街》 玄幻 (fantasy), not 科幻 (SF). The genre line had already been drawn before I knew to look for it. A man writing speculative fiction at scale gets read as 科幻. A woman writing the same kind of speculative fiction at scale gets read as 玄幻 or 仙侠, and excluded from the SF canon.

科幻 carries the weight of conquered space and won wars. The rest goes elsewhere.
So who draws the genre line? And who gets filed by it?

Closing

V3 ends with two questions: who draws the genre line, and who gets filed by it. The museum does not answer these. It hands them back, with what it knows about where the line was drawn and where the filings landed.

What does Hong Kong feminist science fiction look like?

It doesn't, and that is the answer. The dominant Sinophone Hong Kong SF tradition is one man's fifty-year newspaper serial — 倪匡's 衛斯理 — and the genre's commercial oxygen was absorbed into it for decades. The market was small and Cantonese-vernacular and favoured serial and film over novel-form genre fiction, and there was no Hong Kong equivalent to 《科幻世界》 to anchor a canon. The HK women writers who do exist — 西西, 韓麗珠, 黃碧雲 — are speculative-literary, not genre-SF. Treat the gap as historical, not as a research failure.

Where are the women on 晋江?

Tens of thousands. The platform hosts massive volumes of women's speculative writing. The classification system files them as 言情, 仙侠, 玄幻, 穿越 — almost never as 科幻. Print-prize circuits ignore the corpus. A man writing speculative fiction at scale gets read as 科幻. A woman writing the same kind of speculative fiction at scale gets read as 玄幻 or 仙侠, and excluded from the SF canon by definition. The genre line cuts against women writers more than men. The line was drawn before any individual writer arrived at it.

Who are 杨潇, 杨枫, 姬少亭, 程婧波?

Vice-editor of 《科学文艺》 in the 1980s, who organised the first 银河奖 and kept the magazine alive through the SF crackdown. Founder of 八光分文化, the Chengdu independent press that has become the most important non-magazine publisher of women-authored SF. Founder of 未来事务管理局, which runs the dominant short-fiction venue outside 《科幻世界》's print pipeline. Editor of 《她：中国女性科幻作家经典作品集》, the 33-author 2023 anthology — the first source-language canon-formation volume for Chinese women's SF. The “writer” file flattens the labour that built the venues. Without the venues, most of the writing in this museum would not have been published.

And the next decade?

Conditional. The post-2018 surge depends on a small number of named translator careers continuing — Ken Liu for Chinese, others for other traditions you have not yet entered in this museum. None of these are guaranteed. The closing line is not “more is coming.” It is: more is possible, if the conditions hold.

This is where the museum ends. With the rooms it could not build yet, named, so you know they are missing.
