Opening

This museum begins with what got overlooked.

Not with a canon, not with a definition of feminist science fiction, not with a list of who counts. With three reading experiences — a child's, a student's, an adult's — and the small private moment in each one where presence comes into focus. Where a writer who had been there all along becomes visible because the room finally has light in the right corner.

The argument, if there is one, is that this is what the tradition does. Feminist SF makes presence visible where it had been overlooked. The form of this site is meant to do the same thing. You are about to walk through the act, not the survey.

Begin with Zhang Xiaofeng.

V1 · 张晓风 · 1968

I was eight when I first read 张晓风. 《我在》 was the one that opened my world. There was a period of time I was obsessed with writing the character 在 (zài). It means being here and present. Reading《我在》, the piece built on a single word, I saw the way her sentences moved and connected the small dignity of a child's presence to the bigger world around her.

V1 · 张晓风 · 1968

I read her as 散文家, or an essayist. The school curriculum had filed her there. So had every anthology I'd ever pulled off a shelf.

Today I learned she also wrote 《潘渡娜》 in 1968. Pandora, in the Taiwanese transliteration; the mainland would later render it 潘多拉. The story, published in 中国时报 on September 12 of that year, is now recognized in Taiwanese SF circles as the first piece of Chinese-language science fiction. 1968. A year before Le Guin's The Left Hand of Darkness. Years before almost anything English-language readers think of as the start of feminist SF.

She wrote it in a room that hadn't been built. The terms 科幻小说 and 科幻文学 didn't crystallize as stable categories in Taiwan until around 1980. When 《潘渡娜》 appeared, "Taiwanese SF" was not yet a thing the field could see. Two other writers — 张系国 and 黄海 — published their first SF stories that same year. Both later became 鼻祖 of Taiwan SF: founding figures, biographies built around the fact. The third writer was filed as 散文家 and stayed there.

V1 · 张晓风 · 1968

So which is it: was the genre invisible to her, or was she invisible to the genre?

Bridge A

Stories filed elsewhere

Five modes · Eight regions · Eleven writers

The category that turns a story into a "first" doesn't always exist when the story is written. Sometimes it crystallises decades later, and the writer who arrived early gets re-filed — or doesn't get filed at all. Pandora is one case. The pattern repeats.

Mode 1 — Already canonised as something else
Brazil · 1960 · 62 years

Dinah Silveira de Queiroz

Already a bestselling novelist and ABL São Paulo seat-holder when she published Eles Herdarão a Terra (1960) and Comba Malina (1969) — speculative-fiction story collections. One of her stories appeared in the foundational Brazilian SF anthology in 1961, alongside the all-male first-wave generation. She died in 1982 still filed as literary novelist. Editora Instante's 2022 Dinah Fantástica was the first time Brazilian publishing categorised her as a pioneer of national SF.

The writer was already inside one canon, so the second canon never had to admit her.

Cameroon–France · 2019

Léonora Miano

Rouge impératrice — explicit Africanfuturist SF set in 2124 in a unified Katiopa African super-state — was filed by French publishing as a literary novel. Miano was already canonised in France as a literary novelist. Anglophone SF circuits never received the file at all.

Mode 2 — Pseudonym held
United Kingdom · 1937 · 48 years

Katharine Burdekin

Swastika Night, published as Murray Constantine, imagined seven hundred years of Nazi rule with women kept as livestock. It predated Nineteen Eighty-Four by twelve years. Burdekin died in 1963 with the pseudonym intact. Daphne Patai re-attributed the Murray Constantine novels to Burdekin in 1985, working through publisher records.

Russia · 1997

Maria Galina

Her first SF novel was published under the pseudonym Maxim Golitsyn. She has since become one of the most internationally translated Russian women SF writers under her own name; the pseudonym debut shaped her Russian reception by a decade.

France · mid-20th century

Julia Verlanger

Published SF as Gilles Thomas, a male pen-name. The Prix Julia Verlanger — France's annual SF/fantasy novel prize — was named for her posthumously, under the male pseudonym, in 1996.

The genre filed the pseudonym, not the woman. In Verlanger's case the pseudonym is the prize.

Mode 3 — Prize-without-genre
Austria · 1963 · ~20 years

Marlen Haushofer

Die Wand won the Austrian State Prize for Literature in 1963 and the Arthur Schnitzler Prize in 1968. She died at 49 in 1970, filed as literary novelist. German feminist presses rediscovered the novel as foundational woman-alone-after-the-end-of-the-world SF in the 1980s. First English translation arrived in 1990, twenty-seven years after publication.

Sweden · 1940 · 85 years

Karin Boye

Kallocain — an injectable-truth-serum totalitarian dystopia — was published as SF/dystopia in 1940 by a major Swedish poet. It predates Orwell's Nineteen Eighty-Four by nine years and Atwood's Handmaid by forty-five. The Anglophone SF tradition treats Boye as poet-only when it treats her at all. In 2025, Kallocain was included in Sveriges kulturkanon — Sweden's state cultural canon — formalising what the source-language critical record had said all along.

Prizes filed the work as literature in its own time. Genre recognition arrived through feminist publishing decades later, or through state cultural-canon decades later still.

Mode 4 — State suppression
Argentina · 1968 · 11 years

Magdalena Mouján Otaño

A mathematician and Basque-Argentine writer, Mouján Otaño won first prize at the 1968 Mar del Plata SF convention for Gu ta gutarrak — a Basque-time-travel story in which Basques use a time machine to escape Spanish history. When Nueva Dimensión tried to publish it in 1970, the Spanish Franco-era Tribunal de Orden Público ordered the issue withdrawn. The fiscal especial declared the work "atentaba contra la unidad de España" — "attacked the unity of Spain." In 1979 the magazine republished the story as redress, in issue 114 — exactly one hundred issues after the suppression.

The state read the story as politics and removed it. The genre file inherited the absence.

Mode 5 — Venue-policing
Métis–Cree–Nordic · 1990 · 22 years

Misha (Misha Nogha)

Red Spider, White Web (Wordcraft of Oregon, 1990) was the earliest full-length cyberpunk SF novel by an Indigenous woman, published the same year as Pat Cadigan's Synners. Won the 1990 ReaderCon Award; finalist for the Arthur C. Clarke Award. The mainstream cyberpunk canon-formation infrastructure of the early 1990s — Locus, Foundation, the major SF studies textbooks — systematically did not place Misha in the cyberpunk canon. Grace Dillon's Walking the Clouds (2012) made the retrospective case for her centrality.

The venue of publication put the work outside the gates the canon-formation infrastructure was watching. The filing was complete; the watchers were facing the wrong direction.

Five modes, eight regions, one structural fact: a story is not a "first" until someone names the file. Genre is one filter. Language is another. Pseudonym is a third. Prize-without-the-right-category is a fourth. State censorship is a fifth. Indigenous-press venue is a sixth.

V2 · 夏笳 / Ken Liu · 2012

During my first year of college, I took a writing class on Weird Fiction. I read Xia Jia's 《百鬼夜行街》, A Hundred Ghosts Parade Tonight, first in Ken Liu's translation. It was the first time I'd read a Chinese writer in English before reading her in Chinese.

At the time, Ken Liu's name felt more visible because he had translated the well-known Three Body Problem series into English. I learned about Xia Jia and her stories through him. This was an inverted reading experience: I met the translator before I met the author, even though all three of us move between Chinese and English.

V2 · 夏笳 / Ken Liu · 2012

Reading 《百鬼夜行街》in English made me ponder the human-robot spectrum and the meaning of being human. But the Chinese version, with its extremely poetic language, added another register that reads more like fantasy. "假作真时真亦假" pulls me into the dreamy world 《红楼梦》fabricates.

V2 · 夏笳 / Ken Liu · 2012

Looking back, how much of what I thought 《百鬼夜行街》 was about came from Xia Jia, and how much from Ken Liu's hand?

Bridge B

Whose hand?

Four modes of gatekeeping

The shape of any non-English literature in English is the shape of a few careers — but those careers don't all do the same job. One translator's choices author the canon. Sometimes the translator is the author. Sometimes the gate is the publisher. Sometimes the gate is the editor.

Scroll to read the cases. Arrow keys skip ahead to V3.

Hook — Ken Liu, Chinese → English (2012–2020)
Chinese → English · 2012–2020

Ken Liu

Of the four contemporary Chinese women in this museum's roster — Hao Jingfang, Xia Jia, Chi Hui, Tang Fei — all four entered English through Ken Liu. The first English appearance of each came through his hand. The anthologies Invisible Planets (2016) and Broken Stars (2019), which he both edited and primarily translated, are the volumes in which Anglophone readers met contemporary Chinese SF as a body of work.

A poet's "假作真时真亦假" can become philosophy in one rendering and dream in another. The choice is an authoring act.

Mode 1 — Single-translator-builds-tradition
Korean → English · 2021–present

Anton Hur

The four Korean women SF writers most internationally visible right now — Bora Chung, Djuna, Kim Bo-Young, Kim Choyeop — all entered the Anglophone market through one translator over four years. Cursed Bunny on the International Booker shortlist in 2022 is the moment Korean weird-SF became a category English readers had a name for. Hur is not alone (Sophie Bowman and Sung Ryu also carry the post-2019 pipeline), but the doorway is recognisably his.

Spanish → English · 2017–present

Megan McDowell

McDowell is Samanta Schweblin's English career; every English Schweblin has come through her hand. Seven Empty Houses won the National Book Award for Translated Literature in 2022. The "Latin American weird boom" English readers have seen since 2017 is shaped substantially by which Spanish-language writers McDowell has had the time and contract to take on.

In East Asia and Spanish-Latin America, single translators build whole traditions' doorways. The pattern does not generalise.

Mode 2 — Author-as-translator
Spanish → English · 2003

Ursula K. Le Guin → Angélica Gorodischer

Le Guin learned Spanish to translate Gorodischer's Kalpa Imperial — the first American English translation, twenty years after the Spanish original. Subsequent Gorodischer translations exist in the doorway Le Guin opened. Bora Chung translates Lem and the Strugatskys into Korean. Karin Tidbeck self-translates in both directions. Élisabeth Vonarburg translated Le Guin into French.

When an author translates, the gate is held open by someone already inside the receiving canon.

Mode 3 — Editor-as-gateway
US Black diaspora · 2000–present

Sheree Renée Thomas

Editor of Dark Matter (2000) and Dark Matter: Reading the Bones (2004), both World Fantasy Award winners. Editor of The Magazine of Fantasy & Science Fiction since 2021 — the first Black woman editor in F&SF's history. Co-editor of Africa Risen (2022). Dark Matter institutionalised Afrofuturism analogously to Grace Dillon's Walking the Clouds for Indigenous Futurisms.

Nigeria · 2014–present

Chinelo Onwualu

Co-founded Omenana — Africa's primary online speculative-fiction magazine — in 2014 with Chiagozie Fred Nwonwu. Co-edited it 2014–2019, publishing and shaping the careers of Tade Thompson, Wole Talabi, Suyi Davies Okungbowa, Eugen Bacon, Dare Segun Falowo, and many of the writers who became the post-2010 African SFF cohort. She did not translate them; she opened the venue that meant they could be read at all.

Where the writers already write in English, the gate is the publication venue. The "hand" is an editor's, not a translator's.

Mode 4 — Publisher-as-gateway
Indigenous Turtle Island

The presses themselves

When the writers already write in English, the gate is the press. Inhabit Media (Inuit-owned, Iqaluit, est. 2006) publishes Aviaq Johnston and the Qitsualik-Tinsleys. Theytus Books (En'owkin Centre, est. 1980) — Canada's oldest Indigenous-owned press — published Mitêwâcimowina, the first Indigenous SF anthology in Canada. Arsenal Pulp Press published Love after the End. Wordcraft of Oregon published Misha's Red Spider, White Web in 1990.

The gate is the existence of a press that will publish the work as Indigenous SF rather than as magical realism, YA, or literary fiction. The "hand" is institutional.

The hand at the gate isn't always a translator's. Sometimes it's an editor's. Sometimes it's a publisher's. Sometimes the author's own. The translator chooses what reaches English. The genre line, in the source language, chooses what counts as SF. Whose hand is on the gate depends on what the gate is made of. Both are filings. Both are author-acts.

V3 · Porridge SF

After my Weird Fiction semester, I read an interview where Xia Jia called her own work "porridge SF." Not hard SF. Something she had to invent a name for.

Little me, reading in Chinese first, would have called 《百鬼夜行街》 玄幻 (fantasy), not 科幻 (SF). The genre line had already been drawn before I knew to look for it. A man writing speculative fiction at scale gets read as 科幻. A woman writing the same kind of speculative fiction at scale gets read as 玄幻 or 仙侠, and excluded from the SF canon.

V3 · Porridge SF

科幻 carries the weight of conquered space and won wars. The rest goes elsewhere.

So who draws the genre line? And who gets filed by it?

Closing — Absence as Answer

V3 ends with two questions: who draws the genre line, and who gets filed by it. The museum does not answer these. It hands them back, with what it knows about where the line was drawn and where the filings landed.

Closing · 1 of 4

What does Hong Kong feminist science fiction look like?

It doesn't, and that is the answer. The dominant Sinophone Hong Kong SF tradition is one man's fifty-year newspaper serial — 倪匡's 衛斯理 — and the genre's commercial oxygen was absorbed into it for decades. The market was small and Cantonese-vernacular and favoured serial and film over novel-form genre fiction, and there was no Hong Kong equivalent to 《科幻世界》 to anchor a canon. The HK women writers who do exist — 西西, 韓麗珠, 黃碧雲 — are speculative-literary, not genre-SF. Treat the gap as historical, not as a research failure.

Closing · 2 of 4

Where are the women on 晋江?

Tens of thousands. The platform hosts massive volumes of women's speculative writing. The classification system files them as 言情, 仙侠, 玄幻, 穿越 — almost never as 科幻. Print-prize circuits ignore the corpus. A man writing speculative fiction at scale gets read as 科幻. A woman writing the same kind of speculative fiction at scale gets read as 玄幻 or 仙侠, and excluded from the SF canon by definition. The genre line cuts against women writers more than men. The line was drawn before any individual writer arrived at it.

Closing · 3 of 4

Who are 杨潇, 杨枫, 姬少亭, 程婧波?

Vice-editor of 《科学文艺》 in the 1980s, who organised the first 银河奖 and kept the magazine alive through the SF crackdown. Founder of 八光分文化, the Chengdu independent press that has become the most important non-magazine publisher of women-authored SF. Founder of 未来事务管理局, which runs the dominant short-fiction venue outside 《科幻世界》's print pipeline. Editor of 《她：中国女性科幻作家经典作品集》, the 33-author 2023 anthology — the first source-language canon-formation volume for Chinese women's SF. The "writer" file flattens the labour that built the venues. Without the venues, most of the writing in this museum would not have been published.

Closing · 4 of 4

And the next decade?

Conditional. The post-2018 surge depends on a small number of named translator careers continuing — Ken Liu for Chinese, others for other traditions you have not yet entered in this museum. None of these are guaranteed. The closing line is not "more is coming." It is: more is possible, if the conditions hold.

This is where the museum ends. With the rooms it could not build yet, named, so you know they are missing.
