# Bridge A — Stories filed elsewhere

*Between V1 (Zhang Xiaofeng) and V2 (Xia Jia / Ken Liu). Theme: the modes of filing failure. Hook: 《潘渡娜》. Five typed modes with parallels from Brazil, the UK, Austria, Sweden, France, Russia, Argentina, and Indigenous Turtle Island.*

---

The category that turns a story into a "first" doesn't always exist when the story is written. Sometimes it crystallises decades later, and the writer who arrived early gets re-filed — or doesn't get filed at all. *Pandora* is one case. The pattern repeats across continents and centuries, and it doesn't repeat in only one shape. There are at least five distinct modes by which a woman SF writer's work gets filed elsewhere. The cross-recon evidence makes the typology visible.

---

## Hook — 張曉風 / Zhang Xiaofeng (Taiwan, 1968 → still arriving)

> "The Sinophone SF chronology, when read in source language, has a woman at the foundational point."

— from the project's own China reconnaissance, after the recon team finally went looking. Read in English, the chronology does not have her.

**1968.** *Pandora* appears in 中國時報. The category 科幻 is not yet a stable Chinese term. There is no shelf to put the story on.

**1968.** 張系國 and 黃海 publish their first SF stories the same year. They are filed nowhere in particular yet either.

**c. 1980.** 科幻文學 crystallizes as a category in Taiwan. The 1968 cohort can now be filed retroactively. 張系國 and 黃海 are filed as founders — 鼻祖. 張曉風 is filed as 散文家 and stays there.

**2019.** 鄭政恆's survey 《華文科幻小說史》 in 《明報月刊》 names *Pandora* as the originary text of Sinophone women's SF. Fifty-one years after publication, in Hong Kong, in a literary monthly.

**2022.** 吳岩's 《20世紀中國科幻小說史》, the canonical academic history of twentieth-century Chinese SF, is published. Internal recon notes the volume "may underrepresent the 1980s women cohort" — and 張曉風 sits a generation earlier.

**Today.** The English Wikipedia article for 張曉風 leads with her Christian-influenced essays. Standard English SF reference works name her infrequently or not at all.

*The filing failure: the genre crystallised after the story arrived, and when it crystallised it filed her elsewhere. The recon now confirms this same pattern in **eight of ten regions surveyed**.*

## The five modes

### Mode 1 — Already canonised as something else

**Dinah Silveira de Queiroz (Brazil, 1960 → 2022).** *Floradas na Serra* (1939) had made her a bestseller; *A Muralha* (1954) had been adapted for Brazilian television; she was an ABL São Paulo seat-holder. Then she published *Eles Herdarão a Terra* (1960) and *Comba Malina* (1969) — speculative-fiction story collections — and one of her stories appeared in *Antologia Brasileira de Ficção Científica* (Editora GRD, 1961), the foundational Brazilian SF anthology, alongside the all-male first-wave generation. She died in 1982 still filed as literary novelist. Editora Instante's 2022 *Dinah Fantástica*, with critical apparatus by Rita Lenira de Freitas Bittencourt and Ana Rüsche, marked the first time Brazilian publishing categorised her as a pioneer of national SF. Sixty-two years.

**Léonora Miano (Cameroon–France, 2019).** *Rouge impératrice* — explicit Africanfuturist SF set in 2124 in a unified Katiopa African super-state — was filed by French publishing as a literary novel. Miano was already canonised in France as a literary novelist. Anglophone SF circuits never received the file at all.

*The filing failure: the writer was already inside one canon, so the second canon never had to admit her.*

---

### Mode 2 — Pseudonym held

**Katharine Burdekin (United Kingdom, 1937 → 1985).** *Swastika Night*, published as Murray Constantine, imagined seven hundred years of Nazi rule with women kept as livestock. It predated *Nineteen Eighty-Four* by twelve years. Burdekin died in 1963 with the pseudonym intact. Daphne Patai re-attributed the Murray Constantine novels to Burdekin in 1985, working through publisher records. Forty-eight years.

**Maria Galina (Russia, 1997).** Her first SF novel was published under the pseudonym **Maxim Golitsyn**. She has since become one of the most internationally translated Russian women SF writers under her own name; the pseudonym debut shaped her Russian reception by a decade.

**Julia Verlanger (France, mid-20th c.).** Published SF as **Gilles Thomas**, a male pen-name. The Prix Julia Verlanger — France's annual SF/fantasy novel prize — was named for her *posthumously, under the male pseudonym*, in 1996.

*The filing failure: the genre filed the pseudonym, not the woman. In Verlanger's case the pseudonym is the prize.*

---

### Mode 3 — Prize-without-genre

**Marlen Haushofer (Austria, 1963 → 1980s).** *Die Wand* won the Austrian State Prize for Literature in 1963; the Arthur Schnitzler Prize in 1968. She died at 49 in 1970, filed as literary novelist. German feminist presses rediscovered the novel as foundational woman-alone-after-the-end-of-the-world SF in the 1980s. First English translation in 1990, twenty-seven years after publication. Lag: roughly two decades to feminist recategorisation; another to genre canon.

**Karin Boye (Sweden, 1940 → 2025).** *Kallocain* — an injectable-truth-serum totalitarian dystopia — was published as SF/dystopia in 1940 by a major Swedish poet. It predates Orwell's *Nineteen Eighty-Four* by nine years and Atwood's *Handmaid* by forty-five. It has been continuously canonised as Swedish dystopia since publication. The Anglophone SF tradition treats Boye as poet-only when it treats her at all. In 2025, *Kallocain* was included in *Sveriges kulturkanon* — Sweden's state cultural canon — formalising what the source-language critical record had said all along: this is a foundational SF novel. Eighty-five years.

*The filing failure: prizes filed the work as literature in its own time. Genre recognition arrived through feminist publishing decades later, or through state cultural-canon decades later still.*

---

### Mode 4 — State suppression

**Magdalena Mouján Otaño (Argentina, 1968 → 1979).** A mathematician (operations research, nuclear physics; Basque-Argentine descent), Mouján Otaño won first prize at the **Segunda Convención de Ciencia Ficción de la República Argentina, Mar del Plata, July 1968** for *Gu ta gutarrak* — a Basque-time-travel story in which Basques use a time machine to escape Spanish history.

When *Nueva Dimensión* tried to publish the story in issue 14 (1970), the Spanish Franco-era **Tribunal de Orden Público** ordered the issue withdrawn from circulation. The fiscal especial declared the work *"atentaba contra la unidad de España"* — "attacked the unity of Spain." Article two of the Press Law.

In July–August 1979, *Nueva Dimensión* republished the story as a redress to its author, in issue 114 — exactly one hundred issues after the suppression. The story was filed, in the official record, as a threat to the state.

*The filing failure: the state read the story as politics and removed it. The genre file inherited the absence.*

---

### Mode 5 — Venue-policing

**Misha (Misha Nogha) — Métis–Cree–Nordic, 1990.** *Red Spider, White Web* (Wordcraft of Oregon, 1990) was the earliest full-length cyberpunk SF novel by an Indigenous woman. Published the same year as Pat Cadigan's *Synners* and four years before Melissa Scott's *Trouble and Her Friends*. Won the 1990 ReaderCon Award; finalist for the Arthur C. Clarke Award. John Shirley has said Misha belongs on any list of seminal cyberpunk writers.

It was published by Wordcraft of Oregon — a small Indigenous-adjacent press — and reviewed in Indigenous-press / Indigenous-academic venues. The mainstream cyberpunk canon-formation infrastructure of the early 1990s — *Locus* reviews, *Foundation*, the major SF studies textbooks — systematically did not place Misha in the cyberpunk canon. Grace Dillon's *Walking the Clouds* (University of Arizona Press, 2012) made the retrospective case for her centrality. Twenty-two years.

*The filing failure: the venue of publication put the work outside the gates the canon-formation infrastructure was watching. The filing was complete; the watchers were facing the wrong direction.*

---

Five modes, eight regions, and one structural fact: a story is not a "first" until someone names the file. Genre is one filter. Language is another. Pseudonym is a third. Prize-without-the-right-category is a fourth. State censorship is a fifth. Indigenous-press venue is a sixth. Sometimes a writer arrives in English before she arrives in her own canon. Sometimes she arrives in her own canon decades after she arrived in print. Sometimes she arrives in her state's cultural canon eighty-five years after she arrived on the page.
