# Bridge A — themed variants

Four variants extending Bridge A ("Stories filed elsewhere"). Each connects to a specific point in the parent bridge, makes a different argument from the same or adjacent material, and can be triggered later as a sub-room or detour.

Each brief lists: connection point, argument, evidence base (research files), recommended format, scope, and open cautions. None of these has been written out as a finished bridge — they are design briefs.

---

## Variant A.1 — The Anglophone summary error

### Connects to

Bridge A's hook frames Zhang Xiaofeng's invisibility as a *genre crystallisation* problem. This variant reframes filing failure one layer further out: even when researchers go looking, they sometimes import errors from English-language overviews. The cataloguing layer fails the way the canon layer fails — for the same reason, in different mode.

### Argument

Phase-1 catalogues built from English secondary sources inherit whatever the English overviews got wrong. The new cross-recon synthesis observes a **~30–40% error rate** on Anglophone-derived names across the regional recons. The mis-filings cluster on names borrowed without source-language verification. The "filing elsewhere" problem reaches the dataset itself.

### Evidence in research files

`recon/synthesis.md` §2 ("The Anglophone-summary-error pattern") catalogues the corrections explicitly across 7 of 10 regions. Specific cases:

- **Sonia Sant'Anna (Brazil)** — gaps.md lists her as pre-2000 SF; Portuguese-language sources confirm she writes historical fiction. (`recon/brazil-recon.md` corrections block.)
- **Nora Naji (Morocco/France)** — gaps.md lists as Maghrebi SF; does not exist as a Maghrebi SF writer. Actual Maghreb anchor is Safia Ketou. (`recon/mena-recon.md`.)
- **Sumathi Mehta (South Asia)** — does not surface in source-language search; probable confusion with Tashan Mehta. (`recon/south-asia-recon.md`.)
- **Cristina Lasaitis attributed *A Mão que Pune – 1890***  — actually by Octavio Aragão (male). (`recon/brazil-recon.md` corrections block.)
- **The Chen Qiufan / Han Song mis-attribution** — the 2018 《光明日报》 essay 《科幻中的女性主义书写》 was attributed to 韩松 in prior recon; the correct author is 陈楸帆. (`recon/china-recon.md` corrections block, lines 5–7.)
- **The Mouján Otaño suppression** — gaps.md framed it as Argentine state action; the censorship was actually Spanish Franco-era Tribunal de Orden Público. (`recon/latam-spanish-recon.md` corrections.)

### Recommended format

**ABSENCE-DIALOGUE** with a meta-twist: the dialogue is between the dataset and its own sources. Each exchange names a claim the dataset inherited and the source-language correction. Three or four exchanges. Could pair with a small ROSTER of the corrected names.

### Scope

Compact. ~250–350 words. Better as a sidebar / detour than as a full bridge. Visually positioned near Bridge A's hook so the reader registers that even *this museum's own framing* has had to be corrected.

### Open cautions

The variant exposes the dataset's own provenance honestly. That's the spine doing its work, but it requires a curatorial decision about how confessional to be. A defensive register would undercut the gesture; a too-self-flagellating register would be off-putting. Calibrate.

## Variant A.2 — Magical-realism absorption

### Connects to

Bridge A's Mode 1 (already canonised as something else) covers cases where SF work was filed as *literary*. This variant carves out a more specific sub-mode: SF work absorbed into the *magical-realism* category in particular, which is its own well-funded canonical home with its own gatekeepers — and which sits next door to feminist SF without ever quite admitting it.

### Argument

"Magical realism" became a critical category large enough and prestigious enough to absorb work that elsewhere would have been filed as SF. The absorption is gendered and regional: Latin American women writers, African women writers, Indigenous women writers, and certain literary-prestige European writers get the magical-realism file even when their work has clear speculative or science-fictional structure. The category is a generous home, but it isn't the SF canon.

### Evidence in research files

- **Alexis Wright** (Waanyi, Aboriginal Australian) — *The Swan Book* (2013), classified by mainstream press as "magical realism"; her own framing emphasises Aboriginal "all-times" narrative structure closer to Native Slipstream. (`recon/indigenous-recon.md` line 363.)
- **Eden Robinson** (Haisla/Heiltsuk) — *Monkey Beach* (2000) read as magical-realist; the *Trickster* trilogy (2017–2021) explicitly speculative-Indigenous urban-mythology SF. (`recon/indigenous-recon.md`.)
- **Léonora Miano** — *Rouge impératrice* (2019), Africanfuturist SF set in 2124, filed in France as literary novel. (`recon/africa-recon.md`.)
- **Daína Chaviano** — Cuban; *El hombre, la hembra y el hambre* (1998) classified magical-realist despite speculative framing; *Fábulas de una abuela extraterrestre* (1988) unambiguously SF but no full English edition. (`authors-raw/chaviano-daina.md`.)
- **Helen Oyeyemi** — *White Is for Witching* and others routinely read as literary fairy-tale; speculative-weird filing inconsistent. (`authors-raw/oyeyemi-helen.md`.)
- **Tatiana Tolstaya** — *The Slynx* (2000) read as post-Soviet literary dystopia; SF reading underweighted in literary reception. (`authors-raw/tolstaya-tatiana.md`.)
- The cross-recon synthesis names this directly for African work: "Speculative elements either marginalised or absorbed into 'magical realist' framing." (`recon/africa-recon.md` line 278.)

### Recommended format

**CLASSIFICATION** parallel to Bridge A's hook, but the filing chain shows movement *between two adjacent canonical categories* (literary–magical-realism–speculative-weird–SF) rather than between *outside* and *inside* canon. Could include a short typographic visualisation of the four categories and the writers who slide across them. ~400 words.

### Scope

Medium. Could be a full sub-bridge of its own; works as both. The strongest case for treating it as a sub-bridge is that magical realism has its own large global canon and the slide-into-SF question is genuinely live in literary criticism.

### Open cautions

This variant risks reading as a critique of magical realism, which it shouldn't be. Magical realism is a real and valuable canonical home. The argument is about what *isn't* in the SF file because it's in the magical-realism file — which is a comment on the SF file's narrowness, not on magical realism's expansiveness. Frame carefully.

## Variant A.3 — The recanonisers

### Connects to

Every filing-failure case in Bridge A's typology has a *recanoniser* — the person, press, or institution that did the work of recategorising. The bridge mentions them in passing (Daphne Patai for Burdekin, Ana Rüsche for DSdQ, Grace Dillon for Misha) but doesn't name them as a group. This variant takes the parent bridge's whole roster and turns the spotlight on the labour underneath each correction.

### Argument

Filing failure gets corrected, when it gets corrected, by a small set of mostly-women critics, editors, scholars, and publishing houses who do the unpaid or underpaid labour of recanonising. The work is largely invisible because it's filed as criticism or editorial labour rather than as authorship. The recanonisers form a tradition of their own, and the museum's whole spine — making presence visible where it had been overlooked — is the recanoniser's gesture at meta-level.

### Evidence in research files

For each of Bridge A's named cases:

- **Daphne Patai → Katharine Burdekin** (1985, Feminist Press introduction). (`authors-raw/burdekin-katharine.md`.)
- **Ann J. Lane → Charlotte Perkins Gilman** (1979, Pantheon edition of *Herland*, introduction). (`authors-raw/gilman-charlotte-perkins.md`.)
- **Ana Rüsche + Rita Lenira de Freitas Bittencourt → Dinah Silveira de Queiroz** (2022, Editora Instante's *Dinah Fantástica*, critical apparatus). (`recon/brazil-recon.md`; `synthesis.md` §1d-1e.)
- **German feminist presses (collective) → Marlen Haushofer** (1980s rediscovery wave). (`authors-raw/haushofer-marlen.md`.)
- **Sveriges kulturkanon committee → Karin Boye** (2025 state cultural canon inclusion of *Kallocain*). (`recon/europe-recon.md`.)
- **Grace L. Dillon → Misha + the entire Indigenous Futurisms field** (2003 coinage, 2012 *Walking the Clouds* University of Arizona Press). (`recon/indigenous-recon.md` line 19+.)
- **鄭政恆 (Cheng Cheng-hang) → 張曉風** (2019 《華文科幻小說史》 in 《明報月刊》). (`recon/china-recon.md` line 59 + line 182.)
- **程婧波 (Cheng Jingbo) → 33-author Chinese women's SF cohort** (2023 《她》 anthology). (`recon/china-recon.md` line 20.)
- **Lady Sybylla + Aline Valek → Brazilian feminist SF cohort** (2013 *Universo Desconstruído*, the first feminist Brazilian SF anthology). (`recon/brazil-recon.md`.)
- **Sheree Renée Thomas → African-diaspora SF canon** (2000/2004 *Dark Matter*; 2022 *Africa Risen*). (`recon/africa-recon.md`.)
- **Trishna Basak → Bangladeshi women's SF** (2023 *Pramila Kalome Kalpabigyan* anthology). (`recon/synthesis.md` §1.)

### Recommended format

**ROSTER** — 8–12 entries, each with: recanoniser name + region/language + the writer or cohort recovered + the year and venue of recovery. Single line per entry, scannable. Could be paired with a short opening paragraph naming the labour-as-tradition, and a closing line that hands back to the parent bridge.

### Scope

Compact-to-medium. ~300–500 words depending on how many entries. Strong case for medium — this is one of the museum's most under-told patterns and deserves visible weight.

### Open cautions

Two of the recanonisers in the candidate roster are male (Cheng Cheng-hang, occasionally Bittencourt's collaborators) and at least one is an institution rather than a person (Sveriges kulturkanon committee). The variant should not silently exclude them or recategorise them; the labour is what matters. Also: include a one-line acknowledgement that this museum *is* a recanonisation event and is using the labour the variant names. The self-implication is the spine.

## Variant A.4 — The first-woman-to-win pattern

### Connects to

Bridge A's hook turns on a *first* (Zhang Xiaofeng as the foundational point of Sinophone women's SF). The bridge's modes catalogue ways that first-ness goes unrecognised. This variant flips the lens to the cases where first-ness *was* recognised — through prizes — and asks what the prize record tells us about how the canon's gates open.

### Argument

The genre's awards open one woman at a time. The "first woman to win X" data-point recurs across the dataset as a structural fact: McCaffrey first Hugo for fiction (1968); Norton first SFWA Grand Master (1984); Hopkinson first Black woman Damon Knight Grand Master (2021); Roanhorse first Indigenous Astounding Award (2018); Padmanabhan first Indian woman Onassis Prize (1997); Beukes first African Clarke (2011); Hao Jingfang first Chinese woman Hugo (2016); Damián Miravete first Latin American Tiptree (2018). Each "first" is a gate opening — and the half-century gap between McCaffrey's 1968 Hugo and Hao Jingfang's 2016 Hugo is the gate's measured slowness.

### Evidence in research files

The "first" data-points are scattered across the dataset's per-author files, surfaced in `narrative/synthesis.md` §6. Specific entries:

- McCaffrey first Hugo for fiction by a woman (1968 — `authors-raw/mccaffrey-anne.md`).
- Norton first SFWA Grand Master (1984 — `authors-raw/norton-andre.md`).
- Padmanabhan first Indian woman Onassis Prize (1997 — `authors-raw/padmanabhan-manjula.md`).
- Beukes first African Clarke Award (2011 — `authors-raw/beukes-lauren.md`).
- Hao Jingfang first Chinese woman Hugo (2016 — `authors-raw/hao-jingfang.md`).
- Roanhorse first Indigenous Astounding Award (2018 — `authors-raw/roanhorse-rebecca.md`).
- Damián Miravete first Latin American Tiptree (2018 — `authors-raw/damian-miravete-gabriela.md`).
- Hopkinson first Black woman Damon Knight Grand Master (2021 — `authors-raw/hopkinson-nalo.md`).

The new cross-recon synthesis flags "gendered prize-record asymmetry" as a freshly emerged pattern (per `recon/synthesis.md` opening paragraph).

### Recommended format

**LAG-BAR** as the primary visual: a horizontal time-strip from c.1965 to today with dated marks for each "first," and ~80 words of frame text naming the half-century slowness. Possibly accompanied by a short ROSTER giving the eight cases above with the prize and year.

### Scope

Compact. ~200–300 words plus the lag-bar. Could become a stand-alone exhibit panel rather than a full bridge.

### Open cautions

The "first woman to win X" framing is itself a complicated trope — it can read as celebratory (great, she got the prize) or as critical (look how late the prize arrived). The variant should hold the second register, not the first. Also: some "firsts" are firsts-by-default rather than firsts-by-recognition (the prize categories changed; the visibility threshold dropped). Mark each case with the relevant condition.
