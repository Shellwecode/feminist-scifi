# Bridge B — Whose hand?

*Between V2 (Xia Jia / Ken Liu) and V3 (Recanonization). Theme: gateways to English are shaped differently in different traditions. Hook: Ken Liu. Four modes of gatekeeping, with parallels from Korea, Spanish-Latin America, the Anglo-American author-as-translator tradition, continental Africa and the African diaspora, and Indigenous Turtle Island.*

---

The shape of any non-English literature in English is the shape of a few careers — but those careers don't all do the same job. One translator's choices author the canon. Sometimes the translator is the author. Sometimes there isn't a translator, because the writer already writes in English; in that case the gate is the publisher. Sometimes there is no single hand at all, because an editor has done the gating long before any translator gets near the text. Ken Liu is the case V2 makes intimate. The pattern is wider, and it is not one pattern. There are at least four.

---

Of the four contemporary Chinese women in this museum's roster — Hao Jingfang, Xia Jia, Chi Hui, Tang Fei — all four entered English through Ken Liu. The first English appearance of each came through his hand, and the two anthologies *Invisible Planets* (2016) and *Broken Stars* (2019), which he both edited and primarily translated, are the volumes in which Anglophone readers met contemporary Chinese SF as a body of work. A poet's "假作真时真亦假" can become philosophy in one rendering and dream in another. The choice is an authoring act.

This is one mode of gatekeeping. A single translator builds a tradition's English doorway in a half-decade. The mode appears strongest in two regions.

## Mode 1 — Single-translator-builds-tradition

### Anton Hur, Korean → English (2021–present)

- **2021** — Bora Chung 정보라 *Cursed Bunny* — Honford Star (UK) / Algonquin (US, 2022). International Booker Prize shortlist 2022; National Book Award (Translated Literature) shortlist 2022.
- **2021** — Kim Bo-Young 김보영 *The Prophet of Corruption* (《저 이승의 선지자》). The work that anchored her Anglophone reception, fifteen years after her Korean debut.
- **2023** — Djuna 듀나 *Counterweight* — Pantheon. Djuna's first full novel in English, after a thirty-one-year domestic career under deliberate pseudonymity.
- **2024** — Bora Chung *Your Utopia* — Algonquin.
- **2024** — Kim Choyeop 김초엽 *If We Can't Go at the Speed of Light* — HarperVoyager. The collection that had sold 300,000+ copies in Korean since 2019.

The four Korean women SF writers most internationally visible right now — Bora Chung, Djuna, Kim Bo-Young, Kim Choyeop — all entered the Anglophone market through one translator over four years. Hur is not alone in the work; Sophie Bowman and Sung Ryu also carry the post-2019 pipeline (the Korea recon notes the pipeline is "dominated by women translators with explicit feminist-SF curation politics"). But the Booker shortlist for *Cursed Bunny* in 2022 is the moment Korean weird-SF became a category English readers had a name for, and that arrived through Hur.

### Megan McDowell, Spanish → English (2017–present)

- **2017** — Samanta Schweblin *Fever Dream* — Riverhead. Man Booker International shortlist.
- **2019** — Schweblin *Mouthful of Birds* — Riverhead.
- **2020** — Schweblin *Little Eyes* — Riverhead. Booker International shortlist.
- **2022** — Schweblin *Seven Empty Houses* — Riverhead. National Book Award for Translated Literature, **winner**.

McDowell is Schweblin's English career; every English Schweblin has come through her hand. Her broader Spanish-language list (Mariana Enríquez, Alejandro Zambra, others outside this museum) makes her the dominant single translator for Latin American literary fiction in English in the 2010s–20s. The "Latin American weird boom" English readers have seen since 2017 is shaped substantially by which Spanish-language writers McDowell has had the time and contract to take on. *Tender Is the Flesh* by Agustina Bazterrica reached English the same year as *Little Eyes* — through Sarah Moses, Riverhead's other Spanish translator, not through McDowell. Two translators, two careers; the doorway is narrow.

*The pattern: in East Asia and Spanish-Latin America, single translators build whole traditions' doorways. The pattern does not generalise.*

## Mode 2 — Author-as-translator

### Ursula K. Le Guin → Angélica Gorodischer, Spanish → English (2003)

- **2003** — Angélica Gorodischer *Kalpa Imperial* — Small Beer Press. The first American English translation of Gorodischer's work, twenty years after the Spanish original. Le Guin's introduction.

Le Guin learned Spanish to do this. She used her own SF-canonical platform to bring an Argentine writer into English. Subsequent Gorodischer translations exist in the doorway Le Guin opened: Amalia Gladhart's *Trafalgar* (Small Beer Press, 2013), Sue Burke's *Prodigios* (Aqueduct Press, 2015). Three translators across twelve years, each carrying a different Gorodischer book.

The author-as-translator mode is rare but has its own genealogy. **Bora Chung** translates Lem and the Strugatskys *into* Korean as part of her writing practice — the gate held open in the other direction, by a Slavic-studies PhD who is also a Booker-shortlisted novelist. **Karin Tidbeck** writes in both Swedish and English and self-translates in both directions, collapsing the gate into a single hand. **Élisabeth Vonarburg** translated Le Guin into French. **Vandana Singh** edits and anthologises South Asian SFF in English.

*The pattern: when an author translates, the gate is held open by someone already inside the receiving canon. Le Guin's introduction made *Kalpa Imperial* legible to readers who had never heard of Argentine SF.*

## Mode 3 — Editor-as-gateway

In continental Africa and the African diaspora, the gatekeeper is more often an editor than a translator — partly because much African SF is written in English already, partly because the field's institutional history is anthology-driven. The gate is the venue, not the translation.

### Sheree Renée Thomas, US Black diaspora → African-diaspora SF canon-formation

- **2000** — *Dark Matter: A Century of Speculative Fiction from the African Diaspora* (ed.). World Fantasy Award; Bram Stoker Award.
- **2004** — *Dark Matter: Reading the Bones* (ed.). World Fantasy Award (second).
- **2021–present** — Editor, *The Magazine of Fantasy & Science Fiction*. The first Black woman editor in F&SF's history.
- **2022** — *Africa Risen: A New Era of Speculative Fiction* (co-edited with Oghenechovwe Donald Ekpeki and Zelda Knight). Locus Award.

The Africa recon flags Thomas as "the highest historical value of any single diaspora addition; the dataset's failure to include her parallels its earlier failure to include Misha for Indigenous Futurisms." *Dark Matter* (2000, 2004) institutionalised Afrofuturism analogously to Grace Dillon's *Walking the Clouds* for Indigenous Futurisms — an editor-built canon.

### Chinelo Onwualu, Nigeria → continental African SF venues

- **2014** — co-founds ***Omenana*** with Chiagozie Fred Nwonwu. Africa's primary online speculative-fiction magazine.
- **2014–2019** — co-edits *Omenana*. Publishes and shapes the careers of Tade Thompson, Wole Talabi, Suyi Davies Okungbowa, Eugen Bacon, Dare Segun Falowo, and many of the writers who become the post-2010 African SFF cohort.
- **2019** — departure essay "So Long and Thanks for All the Stories," *Omenana*, 9 May 2019.

Onwualu's career-shape is editor-as-gateway in its purest form. She did not translate the writers *Omenana* published; she opened the venue that meant they could be read at all. *Sofia Samatar* did similar work as co-editor of *Jalada 02: Afrofuture(s)* (2015), four years before Okorafor's "Africanfuturism Defined" coined the movement's name.

*The pattern: where the writers already write in English, the gate is the publication venue. The "hand" is an editor's, not a translator's.*

## Mode 4 — Publisher-as-gateway (the inverted case)

In Indigenous Turtle Island, the pattern inverts further. Indigenous SF writers — Cherie Dimaline, Darcie Little Badger, Tanya Tagaq, Aviaq Johnston, Eden Robinson, Daniel Heath Justice, Joshua Whitehead — predominantly self-write in English. The colonial language is the language of publication. There is no translator at the gate, and often no editor at a single venue. There is, instead, a small set of Indigenous-led or Indigenous-aligned presses whose existence determines whether a manuscript becomes a book.

- **Inhabit Media** (Iqaluit, Nunavut, est. 2006). Inuit-owned, Inuktitut-and-English bilingual. Publishes Aviaq Johnston (*Those Who Run in the Sky*, 2017; *Those Who Dwell Below*, 2019) and the Qitsualik-Tinsleys (*Ajjiit: Dark Dreams of the Ancient Arctic*, 2011). Without Inhabit Media there is no Inuit-press SF stratum.
- **Theytus Books** (Penticton, BC, En'owkin Centre, est. 1980). Canada's oldest Indigenous-owned press; publishes the *Mitêwâcimowina* anthology (Neal McLeod, ed., 2016) — the first Indigenous SF anthology published in Canada.
- **Arsenal Pulp Press** (Vancouver). Publishes *Love after the End: An Anthology of Two-Spirit and Indigiqueer Speculative Fiction* (Joshua Whitehead, ed., 2020), Lambda Literary Award winner.
- **Wordcraft of Oregon** (small press). Published Misha's *Red Spider, White Web* in 1990.

The gate, here, is the existence of the press. The Indigenous-Futurisms infrastructure that Grace Dillon coined in 2003 and anchored in *Walking the Clouds* (University of Arizona Press, 2012) presupposes these venues. The Indigenous recon names this directly: "publisher-as-influence, not strictly an author-to-author edge but a documented field-shaping presence."

*The pattern: when the writers already write in the receiving language, there is no translator's hand to author the canon. The gate is the existence of a press that will publish the work as Indigenous SF rather than as magical realism, YA, or literary fiction. The "hand" is institutional.*

---

The hand at the gate isn't always a translator's. Sometimes it's an editor's. Sometimes it's a publisher's. Sometimes the author's own. In two regions the single-translator model dominates and one career can carry a tradition into English in five years. In two others the model doesn't apply at all — because the editorial venue is the gate, or because the writers already write in the receiving language and the press is the gate. The translator chooses what reaches English. The genre line, in the source language, chooses what counts as SF. Whose hand is on the gate depends on what the gate is made of. Both are filings. Both are author-acts.
