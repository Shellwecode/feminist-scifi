# Bridge B — themed variants

Four variants extending Bridge B ("Whose hand?"). Each connects to a specific point in the parent bridge, makes a different argument from the same or adjacent material, and can be triggered later as a sub-room or detour.

Each brief lists: connection point, argument, evidence base (research files), recommended format, scope, and open cautions.

---

## Variant B.1 — Anthology-as-canon-formation

### Connects to

Bridge B's Mode 3 (editor-as-gateway) names *Dark Matter*, *Africa Risen*, *Omenana*, *Jalada 02* as African / diaspora canon-shaping anthologies. This variant generalises the move: anthology editing is canon-formation in its own right, and across the recons the **anthology event** has emerged as a structural pattern recurring in 6+ regions. The variant reroutes the editor-as-gateway claim into a single visible series.

### Argument

A women-or-2SQ-edited anthology framed explicitly as the cohort's first comprehensive showing has become the recurring institutional gesture by which feminist SF gets its source-language canon. The events cluster post-2010 and arrive in different traditions at different decades. Reading the anthology series as a series makes the global pattern legible — and shows that "the canon" is built not by individual genius but by a sequence of editorial gestures, each one woman or one collective deciding what counts.

### Evidence in research files

Drawn directly from the new top-level cross-recon synthesis (`recon/synthesis.md` §1, "The canon-formation event pattern"):

- **Indigenous (Turtle Island), 2012** — *Walking the Clouds: An Anthology of Indigenous Science Fiction*, ed. Grace L. Dillon (U Arizona Press). The foundational text of Indigenous Futurisms. (`recon/indigenous-recon.md` line 19+.)
- **Brazil, 2013** — *Universo Desconstruído*, ed. Lady Sybylla + Aline Valek. "*A primeira coletânea do gênero no Brasil*." (`recon/brazil-recon.md`.)
- **Indigenous 2SQ, 2020** — *Love after the End: An Anthology of Two-Spirit and Indigiqueer Speculative Fiction*, ed. Joshua Whitehead (Arsenal Pulp Press). Lambda Literary Award. (`recon/indigenous-recon.md`.)
- **Brazil (recanonisation), 2022** — *Dinah Fantástica* (Editora Instante), apparatus by Ana Rüsche + Rita Lenira de Freitas Bittencourt. (`recon/brazil-recon.md`.)
- **China, 2023** — *《她：中国女性科幻作家经典作品集》*, ed. 程婧波. 33 women authors, three generations. The first source-language canon-formation volume specifically for Chinese women's SF. (`recon/china-recon.md` line 20.)
- **Bangladesh, 2023** — *Pramila Kalome Kalpabigyan*, ed. Trishna Basak. (`recon/synthesis.md` §1.)
- **Africa-diaspora, 2000/2004** — *Dark Matter* (vols 1 + 2), ed. Sheree Renée Thomas. (`recon/africa-recon.md`.)
- **Africa-diaspora, 2022** — *Africa Risen*, co-ed. Sheree Renée Thomas + Oghenechovwe Donald Ekpeki + Zelda Knight. Locus Award. (`recon/africa-recon.md`.)
- **East African, 2015** — *Jalada 02: Afrofuture(s)*, co-ed. Sofia Samatar (predates Okorafor's 2019 Africanfuturism coinage by four years). (`recon/africa-recon.md` line 54.)
- **Africanfuturism (anthology), 2020** — Wole Talabi ed., *Africanfuturism: An Anthology*. (`recon/africa-recon.md` line 35.)
- **Foundational-essay diptych, 2018+2019 (variant form)** — Mohale Mashigo, "Afrofuturism is not for Africans living in Africa" (2018) + Nnedi Okorafor, "Africanfuturism Defined" (2019). Two essays jointly establish a self-naming movement-political position; structurally homologous to Dillon's 2003 Indigenous Futurisms coinage that anchored *Walking the Clouds* nine years later. (`recon/synthesis.md` §1; `recon/africa-recon.md`.)

### Recommended format

**LAG-BAR** as primary visualisation: a chronological strip from 2000 to today with each anthology event marked, sub-grouped by region. Each mark labelled with editor + title + region + year. Plus ~150 words of frame naming the pattern.

OR **ROSTER**: 10–12 entries each with editor + title + region + year + one-line claim about what the anthology did. ~400 words.

The new cross-recon synthesis explicitly identifies three sub-types of canon-formation event (anthology, foundational-essay diptych, institutional/critical apparatus) — so the visualisation could colour-code or symbol-mark by sub-type to make the typology visible too.

### Scope

Medium-large. This is one of the museum's strongest cross-regional patterns. Could be a full sub-bridge or a stand-alone exhibit hall. The "next decade" question — which traditions haven't had their canon-formation event yet (MENA hasn't; Spanish-language Latin America hasn't, per `recon/synthesis.md` §1) — is its own forward-looking material.

### Open cautions

Spanish-language Latin America **breaks the pattern** — no women-edited feminist-SF anthology has emerged across the Hispanic Americas, and the new synthesis flags this as a finding, not an oversight. (Closest analogue: 2025 Ecuadorian *La irrealidad y nada más que la irrealidad*, which is fantastic-literature-broad rather than feminist-SF-specific.) MENA also has no equivalent event. The variant must include the breaks honestly — they're part of what the pattern means.

## Variant B.2 — The translator collective

### Connects to

Bridge B's Mode 1 (single-translator-builds-tradition) emphasises individuals: Liu, Hur, McDowell. But the most extreme single recent translation event in the dataset — the 2021 Suzuki Izumi *Terminal Boredom* arrival — was done by **a six-translator collective**. And in the Korean case, Hur is one of three or four named women translators with shared feminist-SF curation politics. This variant carves out a sub-mode: collaborative gatekeeping.

### Argument

Some traditions enter English not through one career but through a small group that splits the labour. The collective form solves a single-translator's bandwidth limit and distributes the curation decisions. The form has its own politics — collective gatekeeping is harder to capture or institutionalise than individual gatekeeping, but it is also faster and broader.

### Evidence in research files

- **The Verso *Terminal Boredom* collective (2021) — Polly Barton, Sam Bett, David Boyd, Daniel Joseph, Aiko Masubuchi, Helen O'Horan**, plus the same group reconfigured (Bett, Boyd, O'Horan, Joseph) for *Hit Parade of Tears* (2023). Six translators co-translated Suzuki Izumi into English after a 40-year lag. (`authors-raw/suzuki-izumi.md`.) The collective form is what made the 40-year backlog tractable.
- **Korean translators with shared feminist-SF politics** — Anton Hur + Sophie Bowman + Sung Ryu + Joungmin Lee Comfort + Sora Kim-Russell. The Korea recon notes: "Translation pipeline is dominated by women translators with explicit feminist-SF curation politics, which is itself a methodological note — the corpus reaching Anglophone reception is filtered by translator preferences." (`recon/korea-recon.md` line 88.)
  - Anton Hur — Bora Chung (Cursed Bunny 2021, Your Utopia 2024), Kim Bo-Young (*The Prophet of Corruption* 2021), Djuna (*Counterweight* 2023), Kim Choyeop (*If We Can't Go at the Speed of Light* 2024).
  - Sophie Bowman + Sung Ryu — Kim Bo-Young's *I'm Waiting for You* (Harper Voyager 2021).
  - Joungmin Lee Comfort + Sora Kim-Russell — Kim Bo-Young's *On the Origin of Species and Other Stories* (Kaya Press 2021).
- **The Storycom / *Clarkesworld* Chinese translation programme** — Ken Liu primary but also Andy Dudak, Carmen Yiling Yan, John Chu and others. (`authors-raw/chi-hui.md`, `authors-raw/tang-fei.md`.) Collaborative pipeline running parallel to Liu's individual career.
- **The *Speculative Japan* anthology series (Kurodahan Press)** — multiple translators, multiple volumes 2000s–2010s. (`authors-raw/ohara-mariko.md`.) Collective curation of Japanese SF in English over a longer arc.

### Recommended format

**TRANSLATOR** format adapted for plurality: each collective gets a panel showing the named participants + the works they collectively delivered + the year(s). Could pair with a short paragraph naming the trade-offs (bandwidth solved; coherence harder; politics distributed).

### Scope

Medium. ~400–500 words. Strong companion to Bridge B's Mode 1 — explicitly takes the *not-Liu-not-Hur-not-McDowell* path through translator gatekeeping.

### Open cautions

Be careful about romanticising the collective form as inherently more progressive than the individual. The single-translator and the collective are responses to different structural constraints (publisher contract structure, source-text length, available translator labour). The form is shaped by what's possible, not by ideology alone. Also: the Korean case is gendered all the way down (women translators of women writers); the Suzuki collective is mixed-gender. The variant should not collapse these into one claim.

## Variant B.3 — Critic-as-gatekeeper

### Connects to

Bridge B's typology covers translator, author-translator, editor, publisher. Missing from the typology — and yet running underneath each existing mode — is the **critic** who names a tradition into existence. Grace Dillon coining "Indigenous Futurisms" (2003) created the framework that *Walking the Clouds* (2012) anchored. Bridge B references the recon for this directly. The variant makes the critic-as-gatekeeper its own mode.

### Argument

A critic's neologism, framework, or canonising essay can do the same work as a translator's bibliography or an editor's anthology — define a tradition, retroactively recover its members, and make it institutionally legible. The labour is even more invisible than translation labour because criticism doesn't sell as books. The critic-gatekeepers cluster in academic positions, but they shape what gets read by far wider audiences than their journals.

### Evidence in research files

- **Grace L. Dillon (Anishinaabe, Portland State)** — coined "Indigenous Futurisms" in 2003, anchored the field with *Walking the Clouds* (U Arizona Press, 2012). The single most consequential framework-coinage in the recon. (`recon/indigenous-recon.md` line 19+.)
- **小谷真理 / Kotani Mari (Japan)** — *女性状無意識 (Joseijō Muishiki / Techno-Gynesis)*, 1994; co-founded ジェンダーSF研究会 (Gender SF Research Society) in 2000; established the センス・オブ・ジェンダー賞 (Sense of Gender Award) in 2001. Did the canon-formation work in Japan ~20 years before the anthology-event pattern arrived elsewhere — through critical apparatus rather than anthology. Translated Tiptree into Japanese with Ohara Mariko. (`recon/synthesis.md` §1; `authors-raw/ohara-mariko.md`.)
- **Daphne Patai (US)** — re-attributed the Murray Constantine novels to Burdekin in 1985, working through publisher records; wrote the introduction to the Feminist Press reissue. (`authors-raw/burdekin-katharine.md`.)
- **Ana Rüsche (Brazil)** — doctoral thesis on Le Guin / Atwood (FFLCH-USP, 2015); critical apparatus in the 2022 *Dinah Fantástica* reissue; her own academic-creative *Quimeras do Agora* (Bandeirola, 2025). Writer-critic-recanoniser hybrid. (`recon/brazil-recon.md`.)
- **宋明炜 / Song Mingwei (Wellesley)** — "the closest thing to a Sinophone Donna Haraway-ish reader" per the China recon; *《看的恐惧：中国科幻诗学》* reads cyborg/posthuman/non-binary gender as structural concern. (`recon/china-recon.md` line 41.)
- **M. Elizabeth Ginway (U Florida)** — *Ficção Científica Brasileira: Mitos Culturais e Nacionalidade no País do Futuro* (PUC-Rio 2005). The most-cited academic monograph on Brazilian SF; commentary on Ruth Bueno + Dinah Silveira de Queiroz; closest thing to an explicit feminist-inflected senior critic of Brazilian SF. (`recon/brazil-recon.md`.)
- **Roberto de Sousa Causo (Brazil)** — gatekeeper-historian of Brazilian SF; PhD USP 2013; *Ficção Científica, Fantasia e Horror no Brasil 1875 a 1950*. Married to Finisia Fideli — flagged by the recon as a methodological caveat. (`recon/brazil-recon.md`.)
- **Ann J. Lane (US)** — 1979 Pantheon edition of *Herland* with introduction; the recanoniser of Charlotte Perkins Gilman. (`authors-raw/gilman-charlotte-perkins.md`.)
- **鄭政恆 / Cheng Cheng-hang (Hong Kong)** — *《華文科幻小說史》*, 明報月刊 2019-07. Most authoritative source-language Sinophone overview. Named *《潘渡娜》* as foundational. (`recon/china-recon.md` line 59 + 182.)
- **Mohale Mashigo (South Africa)** — 2018 essay "Afrofuturism is not for Africans living in Africa" — opened the conceptual door for Africanfuturism. (`recon/synthesis.md` §1.)
- **Nnedi Okorafor** — 2019 blog essay "Africanfuturism Defined" — coined the term. Closes the diptych with Mashigo. (`authors-raw/okorafor-nnedi.md`.)

### Recommended format

**ROSTER** with strong narrative frame. ~10–12 critics with one-line claims about each. Each entry: critic + region + the framework/essay/event they produced + the writer or cohort their work made legible. Could be paired with one short ESSAY (~250 words) framing the critic-as-gatekeeper claim.

### Scope

Medium. ~500–700 words. Strong sub-bridge candidate — the critic-as-gatekeeper claim is genuinely under-told and overlaps with both Bridge A (Variant A.3, the recanonisers — they share several entries) and Bridge B's existing typology.

### Open cautions

There is overlap with Variant A.3 (the recanonisers) — Patai, Rüsche, Lane, Dillon appear in both. Either decide that A.3 and B.3 are paired sub-rooms (one in each parent's set) or merge them. The cleanest division: A.3 stays focused on recanonisation *of individual writers* (Patai → Burdekin); B.3 stays focused on framework-creation *that shapes whole traditions* (Dillon → Indigenous Futurisms). The Cheng Cheng-hang case sits on the line and could go either way.

Also: Causo's marriage to Fideli is the methodological note the recon makes load-bearing. Including him as a critic-gatekeeper is honest if the museum also names the conflict-of-record.

## Variant B.4 — Self-translation as gate-collapse

### Connects to

Bridge B's Mode 2 (author-as-translator) names Le Guin → Gorodischer as the canonical case, and mentions Tidbeck's bilingual self-translation in passing. This variant makes the self-translator its own mode: the gate collapses when a single hand writes on both sides of it. Rare but structurally distinct from the other modes.

### Argument

Self-translation eliminates the gatekeeper because the writer *is* the gatekeeper. There is no second authoring act to negotiate; the register choices, the rendering decisions, the introduction to a new readership — all done by the same person. The self-translator's bibliography reveals what an author would write in each language *if there were no intermediary at all.* The mode is rare partly because it requires bilingual fluency at literary register, partly because it is structurally redundant with other gatekeeping modes (publishers don't need both versions).

### Evidence in research files

- **Karin Tidbeck (Sweden)** — writes and self-translates between Swedish and English in both directions. *Jagannath* (Cheeky Frawg, 2012) was self-translated from Swedish; *Amatka* (Vintage, 2017) self-translated from the 2012 Swedish edition; *The Memory Theater* (2021) was written in English directly and self-translated *into* Swedish subsequently. The most thorough self-translation case in the dataset. (`authors-raw/tidbeck-karin.md`.)
- **Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain (Bengal, 1905)** — wrote *Sultana's Dream* directly in English for *The Indian Ladies' Magazine* (Madras), then translated it into Bengali herself as *Sultanar Swapna* (1908). Predates Tidbeck by a century. The *first* foundational feminist SF text in Anglophone reception was self-translated by its author. (`authors-raw/hossain-rokeya.md`.)
- **Yoko Tawada (Japanese-German)** — writes in both Japanese and German; not strictly self-translating between her two languages but operating bilingually at literary register. The Japan recon flags her as cross-category and "speculative reading is plausible and not contested but is not the field-internal anchor." (`recon/japan-recon.md`; flagged in `narrative/synthesis.md`.)
- **Vandana Singh (India / US)** — writes in English directly while drawing on Hindi-Urdu literary tradition, Sanskrit and Indian scientific material. Not self-translation in the strict sense, but a related collapse: the source-language inflection is in the English original. (`authors-raw/singh-vandana.md`.)

Adjacent (mentioned for contrast, not full cases):

- **Ursula K. Le Guin** translated her *own* version of the *Tao Te Ching* (2011) — author-as-translator of someone else's text, not self-translation, but worth noting as the same author's translation labour being invisibilised within her writer reputation. (`authors-raw/le-guin-ursula.md`.)
- **Bora Chung** translates Lem and the Strugatskys *into* Korean — the translator hat is a separate role, not self-translation. (`authors-raw/chung-bora.md`.)

### Recommended format

**ESSAY** (~300–400 words) on the gate-collapse claim, anchored on the Tidbeck and Hossain cases as paired bookends. Could include a small typographic note showing the bidirectionality of Tidbeck's translation arrows. The shortness and rarity of the mode argue for ESSAY rather than ROSTER — the form should match the smallness of the catalogue.

### Scope

Compact. ~300–500 words. Probably the smallest of the variants. Best deployed as a short detour off Bridge B's Mode 2, not as a full sub-bridge.

### Open cautions

The self-translation case is small in absolute number. Stretching it to include Tawada or Singh would dilute the strict definition (they write in two languages but don't self-translate texts already written in one). The variant is strongest when held tight to Tidbeck + Hossain — two writers separated by a century who collapsed the same gate by the same gesture. The smallness is the point; don't pad it.
