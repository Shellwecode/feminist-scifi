# Synthesis — first pass, surfacing only

A reading-partner's notes on what's actually in the 76 author files plus the two recons and the two meta-files. Bullets, not prose. Where I'm uncertain I say so. Where the data pushes back on the spine I flag it.

---

## 1. Patterns in the data

### 1a. Temporal patterns

**The shape isn't a steady accumulation. It's three pulses with a long gap between two and three:**

- **Pulse 1: scattered solitaries (1818–1965).** Shelley 1818, Gilman 1892/1915, Hossain 1905, Mitchison 1923+/1962, Burdekin 1937 (lost as Murray Constantine until 1985), Moore 1933, Brackett 1940, Norton 1934, Haushofer 1963, Kurahashi 1960. Plus from recon: Dinah Silveira de Queiroz 1960/69, Zhang Xiaofeng 1968 (Taiwan). These are *individuals*, not a movement; many lost-then-rediscovered.
- **Pulse 2: 1970s feminist-SF crystallization (1968–c.1985).** Le Guin LHoD 1969, Russ "When It Changed" 1972 / *Female Man* 1975, Tiptree's major novellas 1973–77, McCaffrey/McIntyre/Wilhelm/Lessing-Canopus, Atwood Handmaid 1985, Wittig *Les Guérillères* 1969 in French, Carter *Passion of New Eve* 1977. Suzuki Izumi 1975–77 in Japan. Gorodischer 1973–83 in Argentina. Vonarburg debut 1981. Chaviano debut 1979. Zhang Jing 1986 (recon, China). **What makes this a "crystallization" rather than another scattering: the Anglo-American cohort is in active correspondence with itself.** Russ–Tiptree–Le Guin–Sheldon letters; Wilhelm/Knight Clarion 1968; McIntyre Clarion West 1971. Mutual self-recognition is what distinguishes Pulse 2 from Pulse 1.
- **Pulse 3: the translation surge (c.2015–present).** Distinct from Pulse 2 in that the *writing* is older than the *English arrival*. See lag table below. The pulse is a reception-event, not a writing-event.

**The lag distribution is the single most striking number in the dataset:**

| Author | Original | English | Lag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Suzuki Izumi *Terminal Boredom* (collection) | 1970s–80s | 2021 | **40+ yrs** |
| Ohara Mariko *Hybrid Child* | 1990 | 2018 | 28 yrs |
| Ogawa *Memory Police* | 1994 | 2019 | 25 yrs |
| Wittig *Les Guérillères* | 1969 | 1971 | 2 yrs (anomaly) |
| Wolf *Cassandra* | 1983 | 1984 | 1 yr |
| Gorodischer *Kalpa Imperial* | 1983 | 2003 (Le Guin) | 20 yrs |
| Tolstaya *Slynx* | 2000 | 2003 | 3 yrs |
| Hao Jingfang "Folding Beijing" | 2012 | 2015 (Ken Liu) | 3 yrs |
| Bora Chung *Cursed Bunny* | 2017 | 2021 | 4 yrs |
| Bazterrica *Tender Is the Flesh* | 2017 | 2020 | 3 yrs |
| Kim Choyeop *If We Can't Go…* | 2019 | 2024 | 5 yrs |
| Djuna *Counterweight* | 2021 | 2023 | 2 yrs |
| Dinah SdQ (recon) | 1960/69 | not yet (recanonised in PT 2022) | 60+ yrs and counting |

The lag is shrinking — but unevenly, and only for *new* work. The historical backlog is 40-year delays that are now collapsing all at once. Suzuki, Ohara, Ogawa arriving in English 2018–21 isn't the field expanding; it's the field's prior decades catching up.

### 1b. Translator clusters — the bottleneck IS the structure

This is the loudest pattern in the per-author files and probably the most under-told. A *handful of named individuals* determine what reaches English readers from each non-English region:

- **Chinese → English: Ken Liu** (Hao Jingfang, Xia Jia, Chi Hui, Tang Fei + the *Invisible Planets* 2016 / *Broken Stars* 2019 anthologies). Without Ken Liu, contemporary Chinese women's SF is essentially absent in English. Single point of failure.
- **Korean → English: Anton Hur** (Bora Chung *Cursed Bunny* + *Your Utopia*; Kim Choyeop; Djuna *Counterweight*). Plus Sophie Bowman + Sung Ryu for Kim Bo-Young.
- **Spanish (Latin American) → English: Megan McDowell** (all of Schweblin's English work) and Sarah Moses (both Bazterrica novels). For Mexican/Argentine short fiction: Adrian Demopulos, Emma Törzs at magazine scale.
- **Japanese → English: Stephen Snyder** (all four major Ogawa works — single translator, sustained relationship); the *Verso* Suzuki collective (Polly Barton, Sam Bett, David Boyd, Daniel Joseph, Aiko Masubuchi, Helen O'Horan); Jodie Beck (Ohara *Hybrid Child*, U Minnesota Parallel Futures series).
- **Polish → English: Jennifer Croft + Antonia Lloyd-Jones** split Tokarczuk between them.
- **Russian → English: Jamey Gambrell** translated nearly all Tolstaya's major work.
- **Arabic → English: Elisabeth Jaquette** (Abdel Aziz *The Queue*, PEN-shortlisted) + Jonathan Wright (*Here Is a Body*).
- **Hebrew → English: Dalya Bilu** (both Hareven novels in English).
- **French (Wittig) → English: David Le Vay**, the same translator across her three SF-relevant novels.
- **French (Vonarburg) → English: Jane Brierley** — Vonarburg's complete English oeuvre.
- **German (Wolf) → English: Jan van Heurck + others.** German (Haushofer): Shaun Whiteside.
- **Finnish → English: Lola Rogers + Herbert Lomas** (Sinisalo).
- **Self-translation: Tidbeck** writes/translates herself between Swedish and English. Unique in dataset.
- **Author-as-translator (across language pairs):** Le Guin → Gorodischer (English from Spanish); Bora Chung → Lem and Strugatsky (Korean from Polish/Russian); Vonarburg → Le Guin (French from English); Ohara + Kotani Mari → Tiptree (Japanese from English); Singh as anthology editor of South Asian SF.

**Pattern claim:** what reaches English is not a market response to demand; it is what 8–10 named translators have *chosen* to invest years of unpaid or low-paid labour in. The shape of the canon-as-received is the shape of these careers.

### 1c. Regional clusters and the asymmetry the dataset already knows about

- Anglophone 29 / East Asian 12 (4+4+4) / Latin American 8 / African+diaspora 9 / European non-English 8 / South Asian 5 / MENA + other 5. **38% of the dataset is Anglophone.** Research-log says this was deliberate — to anchor influence edges. Honest.
- East Asian is the *most balanced* internally (4 Japan + 4 China + 4 Korea); Latin American leans Argentine (4 of 8); African splits diaspora-heavy (Hopkinson, Due, Shawl, Okorafor in US/Canada/UK; Beukes, Kahiu, Onwualu, Serpell partial diaspora; only Beukes lives continentally).
- Multi-region authors not handled cleanly: Hopkinson (Jamaica/Canada/US), Chaviano (Cuba/US since 1991), Suzuki (Tokyo but globally received only post-2021), Serpell (Zambia/US), Tidbeck (Sweden, but bilingual production), Ohara (Tokyo, globally read), Singh (India/US), Oyeyemi (Nigeria/UK/everywhere). The dataset's single-region field is doing violence here.
- South Asian is functionally India + Pakistan; Bangladesh has Hossain (1905, pre-partition) and nothing modern; Sri Lanka absent.
- MENA cluster dates: all four authors (Abdel Aziz, Al-Maria, Sansour, Hareven) post-2008. **No pre-2010 MENA representation at all.**

### 1d. Thematic clusters that span the corpus (these are the strongest "global conversation" evidence)

Six themes have enough cross-regional, cross-temporal density to anchor a museum room:

1. **Single-sex worlds** (men eradicated / men kept apart / women segregated). Probably the *most consistent* thematic continuity across 100 years and every region:
   - Hossain *Sultana's Dream* 1905 (Bengali) — Ladyland with men in *mardana*.
   - Burdekin *Swastika Night* 1937 — women as livestock.
   - Russ "When It Changed" 1972 / *Female Man* 1975 — Whileaway.
   - Tiptree "Houston, Houston" 1976 / "Screwfly Solution" 1977.
   - Suzuki "女と女の世の中" 1977 (Japanese) — same year as Tiptree.
   - Carter *Passion of New Eve* 1977.
   - Mitchison *Solution Three* 1975.
   - Kurahashi *Amanon-koku Ōkanki* 1986 — *untranslated to English*.
   - Atwood *Handmaid* 1985.
   - Vonarburg *In the Mothers' Land* 1992.
   - Griffith *Ammonite* 1993.
   - Padmanabhan *Escape* 2008.
   - Beukes *Afterland* 2020.
   - Shah *Before She Sleeps* 2018.
   - **Every continent represented. Every decade since the 1970s. The data wants this room.**

2. **Reproductive coercion / forced reproduction.** Atwood, Bazterrica, Butler (*Bloodchild*, *Xenogenesis*), Russ *We Who Are About To*, Mitchison *Solution Three*, Kurahashi, Burdekin, Padmanabhan, Shah, Lessing's late work. Cross-regional, cross-temporal.

3. **Cyborg bodies, embodiment, disability as theme.** Anchored by Moore "No Woman Born" 1944 (cyborg-dancer), now extending: Cadigan (cyberpunk), Ohara *Hybrid Child*, Tidbeck *Amatka*, Kim Choyeop (deaf protagonist + author), Griffith (MS, written into work), Bujold (Miles's disability central), McIntyre *Superluminal*, McCaffrey *Ship Who Sang*, Butler (chronic illness threads), Cherryh *Cyteen* (cloning). gaps.md flags `disability` as a missing controlled-vocabulary tag — this is correct; the texts are there.

4. **Translation/linguistic ontology as plot device.** Leckie pronoun politics (Radchaai unmarked "she"); Wittig grammatical insurrection (universal *elles*); Tidbeck *Amatka* (objects unmade by being un-labelled); Le Guin (translated Lao Tzu and Gorodischer); Roanhorse (Diné cosmology as living grammar); Hopkinson (Caribbean Creole as SFnal medium); Tolstaya *Slynx* (archaic Russian register); Cherryh *Foreigner* (translator-protagonist 20+ vols); Bora Chung's parallel translator-career. **gaps.md flags this as a missing tag — it's there in the texts and it's distinct enough from `language`-as-diegetic to deserve its own room.**

5. **Climate / ecological collapse.** Butler *Parable*, Jemisin *Broken Earth*, Bazterrica *Unworthy*, Sinisalo *Blood of Angels*, Singh *Of Wind and Fire*, Beukes *Afterland*, Vonarburg *Silent City*, Mitchison's late work, Roanhorse *Trail of Lightning* (post-climate Dinétah), Chambers solarpunk Monk & Robot, Kahiu *Pumzi* (water scarcity), Sansour *In Vitro* (post-eco bunker beneath Bethlehem). Truly cross-regional.

6. **Folklore-becoming-SF.** Carter, Lanagan, Tidbeck (Swedish vittror), Sinisalo (Kalevala), Wolf (Cassandra/Medea), Hopkinson (Caribbean cosmology), Roanhorse (Diné), Okorafor (Africanfuturism), Xia Jia (zhiguai / chuanqi ghost mode), Chabria (Andal devotional poetry), Oyeyemi (abiku tradition), Hossain (proto-utopian Muslim Bengali). Every continent.

(Other tags worth tracking but slightly thinner: state surveillance/bureaucratic dystopia, time/memory, first-contact xenobiology, cyberpunk.)

### 1e. The influence graph has two suns and many moons

The documented-only edges that the dataset contains converge on two super-nodes:

- **Octavia Butler** as the central influence-node for the Black/African-diaspora chain: explicitly cited by Hopkinson, Due, Shawl, Okorafor, Jemisin, Onwualu, Roanhorse, Beukes, Sansour, Al-Maria, Kahiu (planning to film *Parable*). Plus Imarisha + brown's *Octavia's Brood*.
- **Le Guin** is the central influence-node for women's SF *globally*: Russ, Atwood (their public exchange about "speculative fiction"), Jones, Griffith, Vonarburg (translated Le Guin into French), Singh, Sinisalo, Chaviano, Mira de Echeverría, Kim Bo-Young, Kim Choyeop, Hao Jingfang, Xia Jia (PhD work on Le Guin), Damián Miravete, Sansour, Chambers, Jemisin. Plus Le Guin's own *outward* labour: she translated Gorodischer's *Kalpa Imperial*, learned Spanish to do so, and her introduction frames the project; she essayed on Wolf's *Cassandra* in *Virginia Quarterly Review* 2001.

Smaller dense subnetworks worth surfacing:

- **Clarion teaching network**: Wilhelm + Knight 1968 → Butler (1970), Robinson, McIntyre, Sterling, later Chiang. Kress regular instructor. Butler → Shawl (Clarion West 1992).
- **Carl Brandon Society**: Shawl co-founded 1999; Octavia E. Butler Scholarship; Parallax/Kindred Awards.
- **Korean SF chain**: Djuna → Kim Bo-Young → Kim Choyeop. *Readymade Bodhisattva* 2019 (Park & Park, Kaya Press) is the documenting anthology. Add Bora Chung's translator-axis → Lem/Strugatsky Korean editions.
- **Chinese SF infrastructure** (recon): Yang Xiao (1980s editor), Yang Feng → Bāguāngfēn / Eight Light Minutes Press, Cheng Jingbo → *她* / *She* anthology 2023 (33 women authors, three generations) — and a load-bearing un-recorded edge: **Qian Lifang → Liu Cixin** (Liu has reportedly credited *Tianyi* 2004 as the precedent that gave him courage for *Three-Body*; recon flags this as awaiting verification of verbatim source).
- **Brazilian re-canonisation chain** (recon): Dinah Silveira de Queiroz (1960/69 SF) → Ana Rüsche (2022 critical apparatus in *Dinah Fantástica* reissue). Plus the **Universo Desconstruído 2013 anthology** (Lady Sybylla + Aline Valek) as the first explicitly feminist Brazilian SF anthology — entirely missing from the dataset's current Brazilian entry.
- **Editorial / field-builder women** as their own undertold pattern: Vonarburg ran *Solaris* for ~20 years; Wilhelm co-founded Clarion; McIntyre co-founded Clarion West; Onwualu co-founded *Omenana* 2014; Hopkinson edited *So Long Been Dreaming* 2004; Singh co-edited *Breaking the Bow* 2012; Shawl co-founded Carl Brandon Society; Cheng Jingbo edited *She* 2023; Yang Feng founded Bāguāngfēn; Jana Bianchi founded *Mafagafo* 2018; Lady Sybylla co-organised *Universo Desconstruído* 2013; Xia Jia is now an academic shaping Chinese-SF reception. **The "writer" tag flattens labour that built the venues.**

The graph shape is honest about what's documented but probably under-counts everything else. gaps.md is explicit that the strict-citation rule under-counts real edges (peer cohorts, undocumented woman-to-woman chains).

## 2. Candidate zones for the museum (3–5 alternative organisations)

I'll lay out five and weigh them. None is obviously right; each makes a different argument from the same data.

### Option A — Time-first (chronological halls)

Four halls:
1. **Solitary Visionaries (1818–1965).** Shelley, Gilman, Hossain, Mitchison, Burdekin, Moore, Brackett, Norton, Haushofer, Kurahashi-debut, Lessing-pre-Canopus, Carter-early. + DSdQ, Zhang Xiaofeng (recon).
2. **The 1970s Crystallization (1965–1985).** Le Guin, Russ, Tiptree, Atwood-debut, McCaffrey, McIntyre, Wilhelm, Wittig, Carter-major, Suzuki, Gorodischer, Vonarburg-debut, Chaviano-debut, Fideli, Lessing-Canopus, Zhang Jing (recon). + Burdekin's *rediscovery* in 1985 belongs here as an event, not in Hall 1.
3. **Genre Diversification (1985–2010).** Butler-major, Cherryh, Bujold, Cadigan, Jones, Griffith, Willis, Kress, Lee, Hopkinson, Due, Padmanabhan, Singh-debut, Wolf, Tokarczuk-early, Tolstaya, Sinisalo, Hao-debut, Xia Jia-debut, Djuna-debut.
4. **The Translation Wave (2010–present).** Korean wave, Latin American wave, Suzuki-translation, Ohara-translation, Ogawa-translation, MENA-arrivals (Abdel Aziz, Al-Maria, Sansour), Africanfuturism's English-market consolidation (Okorafor, Jemisin, Serpell, Beukes), Brazilian recanonisation. Recent Indian voices (Singh, Shah). Rebecca Roanhorse 2017 onward.

- **What this argues:** the receiver's chronology — when work *reached English readers* — is itself the story. The lag becomes the museum's spine.
- **What it costs:** privileges the Anglophone reception perspective; risks reproducing Anglo-centrism even as it tries to challenge it. The chronology *of writing* gets blurred against the chronology *of arrival*. Suzuki belongs to 1970s Pulse 2 by writing-date but to Pulse 3 by reception-date. Treating those two facts as the same is dishonest; treating them differently within one chronological framework is hard.

### Option B — Geography-first (regional rooms)

Seven rooms, mirroring the dataset's regions (with internal chronologies). Anglophone subdivided by era to manage size. Cross-region influence chains shown as connecting wires/bridges between rooms.

- **What this argues:** each region is its own tradition, on its own terms. Contests Anglo-default by spatial equality — not equal *number* of authors per room, but equal *room*.
- **What it costs:** reproduces the dataset's own regional buckets, which the entries themselves often violate (Suzuki Tokyo–globally, Hopkinson Jamaica/Canada/US, Chaviano Cuba/US). Cross-regional themes (single-sex worlds across continents) become harder to see. The Anglophone room will overflow its allotted space simply by author count — solving this in the visualization will require a decision about whether to display *fewer* Anglophones per square metre.

### Option C — Translation-flow (the pipeline AS the museum)

Four to five halls:
- **Author-Translator Pairs.** Le Guin/Gorodischer, Tidbeck-self, Vonarburg/Brierley, Wolf/van Heurck, Tolstaya/Gambrell, Snyder/Ogawa, Hur/Chung, Croft–Lloyd-Jones/Tokarczuk, Ken Liu/Hao+Xia+Tang+Chi.
- **Pipeline Hubs.** Verso (Suzuki collective), Small Beer Press (Le Guin–Gorodischer–Gladhart), Pantheon (Hur), Saga Press (Liu), Kaya Press (Korean), Hoopoe-AUC (Arabic), Decrescenzo (French-Korean), Actes Sud (Gaffric Chinese-French), Honford Star, Tor.com.
- **Anthologies as Canon-Making Acts.** *Cosmos Latinos* 2003, *Invisible Planets* 2016, *Broken Stars* 2019, *Readymade Bodhisattva* 2019, *AfroSF* 2012, *So Long Been Dreaming* 2004, *Universo Desconstruído* 2013 (recon), *Spanish Women of Wonder* 2016, *Speculative Japan* (Kurodahan), *This Kind of Woman* 1982 Stanford, *她* 2023 (recon), *Sultana's Dream and Selections* 1988 Feminist Press.
- **Awards as Sorting Mechanisms.** Hugo, Nebula, Booker International, Tiptree/Otherwise, Clarke, Argos (Brazil), Galaxy/银河奖 (China), Premio Clarín, Prix Médicis, Sapir, Akutagawa, Nihon SF Taishō, Geffen.
- **Optional: Press / Magazine institutions** (*Mafagafo*, *Omenana*, *Solaris* Quebec, *Science Fiction World* Chengdu, *Strange Horizons*, *Clarkesworld*, *Samovar*).

- **What this argues:** infrastructure is the message. The 76 authors are positioned not as solitary geniuses but as nodes in a translation/publishing system. Performs the spine's "translation lag is closing" claim by literally showing the closing pipeline.
- **What it costs:** risks making the museum about translators and publishers rather than writers. Could feel like a publishing-industry case study. Less emotionally resonant for a casual visitor. Foregrounds infrastructure over voice.

### Option D — Thematic zones (cross-cutting, ignoring time/region)

Six rooms, one per the major themes from §1d:
1. **Single-Sex Worlds.** ~12 authors across 100 years and every continent.
2. **Reproductive Futures.** ~10 authors, similarly distributed.
3. **Cyborg Bodies & Disabled Futures.** Moore 1944 as anchor + ~10 contemporary.
4. **Translation as Politics.** Wittig, Leckie, Tidbeck, Le Guin (translator), Roanhorse, Hopkinson, Tolstaya, Cherryh, Bora Chung — small but coherent.
5. **Apocalypse, Ecology, Climate.** ~12 authors, includes filmic Kahiu/Sansour.
6. **Folklore-Becoming-SF.** ~12 authors, every continent.

- **What this argues:** the global conversation is *literal* — Suzuki and Russ wrote single-sex world stories the same year on opposite sides of the Pacific. Bazterrica and Atwood echo each other. Moore in 1944 is direct ancestor to Kim Choyeop in 2019. Themes are evidence the conversation was always happening; the translation lag obscured it.
- **What it costs:** loses the visible chronology that makes "translation lag is closing" legible. Many authors fit multiple themes, so the curation will be aggressive. Also: a thematic museum can feel timeless in a way that contradicts the spine's claim about *the next decade*.

### Option E — Reception-status (what English readers know, vs. what's just arrived, vs. what's blocked, vs. what's structurally absent)

Five zones, structured around what kind of *visibility* each entry has from an Anglophone reader's vantage. The museum performs the gesture by walking the visitor *out* of the familiar canon and into successively less-visible territory.

1. **The Familiar Canon.** Shelley, Le Guin, Atwood, Butler, Russ, Tiptree, Carter, Jemisin, Cherryh, Bujold, McCaffrey, Willis, Wilhelm, McIntyre, Norton, Brackett, Moore, Lessing, Wolf, Tokarczuk (Nobel/Booker raises visibility), Hopkinson, Okorafor — what an Anglophone feminist-SF reader of c.2010 already knew or could find easily.
2. **The Recently Arrived (post-2018 translations).** Suzuki *Terminal Boredom* 2021, Ogawa *Memory Police* 2019, Ohara *Hybrid Child* 2018, Bora Chung 2021, Kim Bo-Young 2021, Kim Choyeop 2024, Djuna 2023, Hao *Vagabonds* 2020, Bazterrica 2020/24, Schweblin (English from 2017), Abdel Aziz 2016/21, Sansour film-circulation 2010s, Al-Maria 2012/19, Roanhorse 2017+. All the dates from §1a's lag table.
3. **The Long-Established But Barely-English (or untranslated).** Kurahashi *Amanon* 1986 — *not in English*. Chaviano *Fábulas* 1988 — no full English edition. Chi Hui — no English novel. Mira de Echeverría — anthology-only. Vonarburg *Silent City* 1981 — partly out of print. Burdekin *End of This Day's Business* — sparse availability. Mitchison's translation history "sparse and under-documented" by her own entry. Padmanabhan *Escape*/*Island of Lost Girls* — translation thinner than play *Harvest*'s. Tang Fei — no collection in English. Plus the recon's tier-1 candidates: Zhang Jing, Hong Ling (Lucifer Hung, Taiwan queer-cyberpunk 1990s), Qian Lifang, Lu Ain-Zaila (Brazilian Afrofuturism), Cheng Jingbo, Wang Kanyu, Han Lai-chu (Hong Kong).
4. **The Editorial-Critical Field-Builders (under-credited because the dataset's category is "writer").** Onwualu/*Omenana*; Shawl/Carl Brandon; Wilhelm-McIntyre/Clarion+Clarion West; Hopkinson/*So Long Been Dreaming*; Singh/*Breaking the Bow*; Vonarburg/*Solaris*; Xia Jia (academic); Bora Chung (translator); + recon adds: Cheng Jingbo (*她*), Yang Feng (Bāguāngfēn), Yang Xiao (1980s 科幻世界), Jana Bianchi (*Mafagafo*), Lady Sybylla + Aline Valek (*Universo Desconstruído*), Ana Rüsche (Brazilian writer-critic-recanoniser).
5. **The Ghost Territories.** The structural absences treated as a room of their own. Pre-1980 PRC silence (real, political); 90-year South Asian gap 1905–1997 (sourcing blind spot); MENA pre-2010 (sourcing-thin and possibly real); Indigenous beyond Roanhorse (Dimaline, Little Badger, Erdrich); HK genre-SF women (*structural*, per recon); Northeast Brazilian SF (regional); Sri Lankan, Bangladeshi post-Hossain; web-fiction women on 晋江 / Wattpad as a *gendered classification* gap (a woman writing speculative fiction at scale gets read as 仙侠/玄幻 or romance, not 科幻); non-binary writers excluded by framing (Emezi, Solomon, Yang).

- **What this argues:** the museum performs the spine's argument *in the visitor's own walk*. Start where they already are; lead them progressively into territory they didn't know existed; end at the empty rooms that mark what's missing. The form mirrors the content.
- **What it costs:** the "Familiar Canon" zone risks being read as a celebration of the dominant canon rather than a critical setup — care needed in framing/labelling. Also: this is the most editorially aggressive of the five options; it requires the museum to *take a position* on what is and isn't visible, which is itself a curatorial act some visitors will dispute.

### A note on hybridisation

These options aren't mutually exclusive. The most narratively powerful synthesis is probably **Option E as the architecture, with Option D themes as cross-cutting filters** the visitor can apply. Time (Option A) and Translation-flow (Option C) become *visualisation modes* rather than zone organisations — overlaid onto the E architecture. Option B (geography) becomes a counter-default — a way of *re-reading* Zones 2–5 to show that "Recently Arrived" isn't actually one homogeneous moment but a collection of regional reception events.

This is my recommendation among the candidates, but it is one synthesis of several plausible ones. Hold off on committing.

## 3. Evidence density per candidate zone

Honest audit. Scale: dense / moderate / thin / gestural.

### For Option A (chronological halls)
- **Solitary Visionaries (pre-1965)**: moderate — anchored by 9 dataset entries plus 2 recon entries (DSdQ, Zhang Xiaofeng); but Anglo-heavy (7 of 11 with the recon adds). Hossain (1905) and Kurahashi (1960) carry almost the whole non-Anglo weight. **Vulnerable claim**: hard to argue "feminist SF was always global" from this evidence; safer to argue "feminist SF before the 1970s was geographically scattered and mostly invisible to itself."
- **1970s crystallization (1965–85)**: dense. The mutual-acknowledgement record is concrete (Russ–Tiptree–Le Guin–Sheldon letters, McIntyre–Le Guin friendship, Wilhelm Clarion). Plus Pulse-2 international entries: Wittig, Suzuki (writing!), Gorodischer, Vonarburg-debut, Chaviano-debut, Zhang Jing (recon), Lessing-Canopus.
- **1985–2010**: dense. Probably the largest hall by entry count.
- **2010–present**: dense in lag-data and translator attribution, but thin in *self-reported influence* (the new arrivals haven't yet had the decade of interviews and acknowledgements that older work has).

### For Option B (regional)
- Anglophone: dense (29 entries; over-saturated relative to others).
- East Asian: dense if recon's Chinese/Taiwan additions are folded in (currently 12 + 8–10 strong recon candidates).
- Latin American: moderate, with Argentine sub-cluster dense (Gorodischer, Schweblin, Bazterrica, Mira de Echeverría) and Brazilian thin (Fideli alone — but recon's Dinah SdQ + Lady Sybylla + Ana Rüsche + Lu Ain-Zaila etc. transform this).
- African + diaspora: dense for diaspora, moderate for continental, thin for non-diasporic women editorially recognised in source language.
- European non-English: moderate — one author per country gives breadth without depth. The "tradition" claim is harder; mostly individual exemplars.
- South Asian: thin (5 entries); the 90-year gap between Hossain and Padmanabhan is its own argument but creates a thin-looking room.
- MENA + other: thin (5 entries, all post-2008); cross-media inclusions (Al-Maria, Sansour) are the room's strongest material *because* of the absence of pre-2010 prose.

### For Option C (translation-flow)
- Author-Translator Pairs: dense. Every per-author file contains explicit translator + year for at least the major translation events.
- Pipeline Hubs (publishers, anthologies): dense. All anthology titles are named.
- Awards: moderate — awards are mentioned but not systematically cross-tabulated by year/region.
- Magazines / venues: moderate — *Clarkesworld*, *Asimov's*, *SF Magajin*, *Science Fiction World*, *Solaris*, *Omenana*, *Mafagafo*, *Strange Horizons* all named.

### For Option D (thematic)
- Single-sex worlds: dense (12+ named texts).
- Reproductive futures: dense (10+).
- Cyborg/disability: dense (10+).
- Translation as politics: moderate — strong texts but spread across regions; needs careful selection.
- Apocalypse/ecology: dense (12+).
- Folklore-becoming-SF: dense (12+).

### For Option E (reception-status)
- Familiar canon: dense.
- Recently arrived: dense — exact translation dates per entry.
- Long-established but barely-English: moderate — visible in the per-author entries' *gaps* as much as in their assertions; recon's tier-1 candidates substantially fill this out.
- Editorial field-builders: moderate — spread across entries; would need synthesis. Recon is essential here for the Brazilian and Chinese cases.
- Ghost territories: dense — gaps.md is the explicit catalog and recon adds substantive structure. **Probably the best-documented "absence" in the dataset**, paradoxically.

## 4. Ghost territories as their own pattern

The structural absences are not a footnote. They cluster into types, and the typology is itself part of the museum's argument.

### Type 1 — Historical absences (real, not sourcing)

- **Pre-1980 PRC women's SF.** Per recon: real, the political conditions of 1949–1976 excluded most women from the science-popularisation infrastructure that produced 1950s–60s SF. The first identifiable PRC woman SF writer is Zhang Jing, 1985–86. **Mark this as a historical fact**, not a research failure.
- **Hong Kong genre-SF women's tradition.** Per recon: structural. The Ni Kuang 卫斯理 monopoly absorbed the genre's commercial oxygen for decades; HK's small Cantonese-vernacular literary market favoured serial/newspaper/film over novel-form genre fiction; no HK equivalent of *Science Fiction World*. The HK women writers who exist (Xi Xi, Han Lai-chu, Wong Bik-wan) are *speculative-literary*, not genre-SF. **Treat the gap as real, not as a research failure.** This contradicts an implicit assumption in gaps.md that women's SF is everywhere if you look hard enough.

### Type 2 — Sourcing/access absences (probably more there than visible)

- **South Asian women's SF, 1905–1997.** Per gaps.md: a 90-year gap. Almost certainly a sourcing blind spot. Bengali/Marathi/Tamil/Urdu/Malayalam speculative fiction by women between WWI and the 1990s.
- **MENA pre-2010.** Salwa Bakr, Nora Naji, 1970s–2000s Egyptian/Syrian/Lebanese women's speculative writing.
- **Brazilian Segunda Onda women (c. 1985–2000).** Per recon: under-feminised in Brazilian SF historiography, but Causo's archives may surface more.
- **Pre-1995 Chinese mid-1990s cohort** (between Zhang Jing 1986 and Ling Chen / Zhao Haihong 1995–97). Per recon: visible in 银河奖 records, dark in English critical literature.

### Type 3 — Categorical / classificatory absences (the gap is the genre boundary)

Probably the most important type for the spine.

- **Brazilian recanonisation gap.** Per recon: Dinah Silveira de Queiroz wasn't lost; she was canonised as a *literary* novelist (ABL São Paulo, *A Muralha* TV-adapted) and *not categorised as SF* until the 2022 *Dinah Fantástica* reissue. **The gap is not "writers who weren't there" but "writers who were never SF-canonised because they were already canonised as something else."** This is a fundamentally different kind of gap than translation-blockage.
- **Chinese 仙侠 / 玄幻 / 科幻 gendered policing.** Per recon: 晋江 (Jinjiang) hosts massive volumes of women's speculative writing classified as 言情/仙侠/玄幻, almost never 科幻. Print-prize circuits ignore this corpus. **The genre line cuts against women writers more than men.** Tens of thousands of authors exist in this corpus and don't enter the SF canon by design.
- **Brazilian Wattpad / Catarse / KDP self-published women.** Per recon: parallel structure to the 晋江 case, less institutionalised but the same logic.
- **Cross-media exclusion.** The dataset is novel/short-fiction-biased. Filmic (Kahiu, Sansour, Padmanabhan's *Harvest* as drama, Al-Maria's video work) is included only as category extension. Where prose markets are thin (MENA, parts of Africa), the *living* speculative work is in film/installation/essay. Excluding this would over-represent Anglophone publishing conventions.
- **Non-binary writers.** Excluded by the dataset's framing on "women SF writers." Akwaeke Emezi, Rivers Solomon, Neon Yang, JY Yang. Tidbeck included with note. This is a *self-imposed* gap that the museum must address explicitly rather than silently.

### Type 4 — Influence-edge absences (real influence, undocumented edge)

Per gaps.md: the strict-citation rule under-counts real edges. Specifically:
- Peer influences within cohorts (1970s Japanese New Wave; 1990s Korean SF formation; 2010s Chinese new wave).
- Influences mediated through translation (which Anglo-American writers shaped 1990s Korean SF beyond Le Guin? Recon flags this is partially knowable but not yet recorded).
- Women-to-women chains where interviewers don't ask (Tanith Lee → later British SFF).
- **One specific load-bearing example from recon:** Qian Lifang's *Tianyi* (2004) → Liu Cixin's *Three-Body* (Liu reportedly credited *Tianyi* as the precedent that gave him courage). If the verbatim source can be confirmed, this is a *woman-to-Liu-Cixin* influence edge — a major reframing of what the Chinese SF establishment thinks its origins are.

### What the typology argues

The "ghost territories" room shouldn't be a single empty room. It should show *four kinds of emptiness* — each making a different argument about what the dataset can and can't say. Type 1 says "this is the world." Type 2 says "we haven't looked hard enough yet." Type 3 says "the categories themselves do the excluding." Type 4 says "the influence chains are denser than we can prove."

## 5. What the data resists

Where the spine doesn't quite fit, or where the data tells a different story than the brief implies. These are the places to pressure-test before committing to zones.

### 5a. The "global conversation" claim is weakest at the foundation

Pre-1965, the dataset's non-Anglo presence is essentially: Hossain (1905), Kurahashi (1960), Haushofer (1963 — German-language, but Austrian/European), Wittig (1969 — French). Plus recon's DSdQ (1960/69) and Zhang Xiaofeng (1968). That's it for "global" before the 1970s.

The honest version of the spine is **not** "feminist SF was always a global conversation that Anglophone readers couldn't hear." It's **"feminist SF was a globally scattered set of solitary efforts that became a self-aware Anglo-American conversation in the 1970s and only in the 2010s–20s started to be visibly global to Anglophone readers."** That's a different and arguably more honest claim. The spine as currently written risks asserting a more flattering history than the data supports.

### 5b. The "translation lag is closing" claim is conditional, not automatic

The lag is closing *because* a small number of named translators have invested years of unpaid or low-paid labour in specific authors. Anton Hur, Ken Liu, Megan McDowell, Stephen Snyder, Jodie Beck, Elisabeth Jaquette, Jamey Gambrell. If any of these careers ends, the corresponding pipeline slows or stops.

The "next decade will reshape what reaches English readers" claim depends on:
1. Translator pipelines continuing and expanding.
2. Anglo publishers continuing to acquire (Verso, Saga, Pantheon, Honford Star, Algonquin, Tor have all been buyers; their acquisition appetite is finite).
3. The post-2018 commercial validation (Booker shortlists for Schweblin, Bora Chung; Hugo for Hao; bestseller status for Bazterrica) sustaining itself.

None of these are guaranteed. The spine should make the claim defensibly: *if* the current translator-publisher infrastructure continues, *then* the next decade will reshape what English readers see. The unconditional claim is over-confident.

### 5c. The translation gap and the recanonisation gap are different gaps

Per the Brazilian case (recon): Dinah Silveira de Queiroz isn't a translation gap. Her work exists in Portuguese; she's a major Brazilian literary novelist. The gap is that she was never *categorised* as SF in her lifetime. The 2022 reissue is recanonisation, not translation.

This generalises. The Tokarczuk / Wolf / Lessing / Carter / Lispector / Oyeyemi / Schweblin pattern is: literary-prestige writers whose work *is* speculative but whose presence in the SF canon is recent or contested. Treating these the same as Suzuki Izumi (who wrote in Japanese genre venues but wasn't English-translated until decades later) collapses two different things.

The museum should distinguish: **(a) translation gap** (work exists in source language SF venues, hasn't reached English), **(b) recanonisation gap** (work exists in literary canon, hasn't been read as SF), **(c) historical absence** (work doesn't exist; political/institutional conditions prevented it), **(d) classificatory absence** (work exists in volume but the genre line excludes it — the 晋江 / Wattpad case). These are four distinct phenomena.

### 5d. The influence graph over-credits the documented ancestors

Le Guin and Butler are everywhere because they're real influences *and* because Anglophone interviewers ask about them. The undocumented edges — Djuna → Kim Bo-Young → Kim Choyeop is partially recorded but the broader 1990s reading environment that shaped Korean SF formation isn't; Kotani Mari → Ohara Mariko in Japan is partial; Gorodischer → Mira de Echeverría is sketched — are systematically under-recorded.

The visualisation will look like **two suns and many moons**. This is honest about the documentation but it's also potentially misleading about the field. **Consider visually distinguishing "documented edges" from "plausible-edges-acknowledged-as-uncertain"** as gaps.md proposes.

### 5e. The "form mirrors content" gesture has a self-implicating problem

The spine claims the museum "acts as" feminist SF — telling stories the canon didn't tell. But the museum is being built by an Anglophone-trained dataset assembled by someone working in English, with research-log entries that explicitly acknowledge English-source dependency. Even with the recons, the museum is still *primarily* an Anglophone-vantage account of where the global field is.

This is not a fatal problem — the museum can be honest about its vantage. But the spine as currently written claims a meta-level gesture that the dataset can only partially perform. The Brazilian and Chinese recons do important work toward escaping this; their findings should be visibly integrated rather than treated as supplementary. The recon-corrected facts (DSdQ, Zhang Jing, Hong Ling, Lu Ain-Zaila, Cheng Jingbo, Han Lai-chu, the 1957/1982 Brazilian periodisation, the GRC→CLFC typo, the Chen Qiufan misattribution-correction) are the museum's strongest claim to *not* being only an Anglophone synthesis.

### 5f. Some categorical decisions are doing more work than the entries acknowledge

- **The "primary medium" question.** Including Kahiu, Al-Maria, Sansour as cross-media is the right call — but their inclusion makes Padmanabhan's *Harvest* (drama) look anomalously categorised. If drama counts, why not include Carolina Maria de Jesus's late-1950s Brazil context or Suzanne Césaire's surrealist essays? Drawing the medium line consistently is harder than the dataset's case-by-case approach.
- **The "speculative-literary" / "core SF" line.** Carter, Tokarczuk, Wolf, Lessing, Oyeyemi, Schweblin, Lanagan, Hareven, Serpell, Chabria, Tolstaya — all are arguably literary-with-speculative rather than SF-in-the-genre-sense. The dataset includes them, which is defensible, but the museum should be explicit that *the line itself is the politics.* The Chinese recon's argument — that the genre line is gendered policing — applies in muted form to the Anglophone field too.
- **The "single region" field doesn't fit ~10 authors.** Hopkinson, Suzuki (in reception), Chaviano, Serpell, Tidbeck, Singh, Oyeyemi, Bora Chung (translator-axis), Vonarburg (France/Quebec), Cherryh (US but classics-anchored). gaps.md proposes primary+secondary region; the museum may need to commit to that.

### 5g. The 1970s "crystallization" itself is mostly an Anglo-American event

What makes 1970s Pulse 2 a *crystallization* and not just another *scattering* is that the Anglo-American writers were in correspondence with each other and built institutions (Clarion, the Tiptree letters, WisCon would come later). The non-Anglo Pulse 2 writers — Suzuki in Japan, Wittig in France, Gorodischer in Argentina, Vonarburg in Quebec, Chaviano in Cuba — were largely *not* in correspondence with the Anglo cohort or with each other. They were having parallel solitary breakthroughs that look retrospectively like a global movement but at the time were not connected.

This matters for the spine. If "the conversation was always global," the data partially refutes it. If "the conversation became visibly global only when translated decades later," the data supports it. The spine should commit to the latter.

## 6. Cross-cutting observations the museum should probably absorb

A few things that don't fit cleanly into 1–5 but seem load-bearing:

- **The dataset's strongest evidence is the lag table** (§1a) and the **translator-cluster table** (§1b). These two tables make the spine's argument more concretely than any thematic claim. Consider one or both as a dedicated installation.
- **The documented influence edges are dense around Le Guin and Butler and sparse elsewhere.** This shape is partly real, partly an artifact of which interview questions get asked. The visualisation should be honest about this.
- **The recons (China, Brazil) are doing heavier work than their two files suggest.** They contain corrections to gaps.md (Sonia Sant'Anna miscategorisation, GRC→CLFC, Chen Qiufan vs Han Song attribution, the 1957/1982 Brazilian periodisation), substantive new authors (DSdQ, Zhang Jing, Hong Ling, Lu Ain-Zaila, Cheng Jingbo, Han Lai-chu), structural arguments about the gendered genre boundary (晋江 / Wattpad), and a load-bearing potential influence edge (Qian Lifang → Liu Cixin). The Phase-2 dataset will be substantively different from the Phase-1 dataset once these are integrated. The narrative spec should anticipate which of these the museum can plausibly include in its first version.
- **Field-builder women are the dataset's most under-told story.** The "writer" field flattens decades of editorial, pedagogical, infrastructural labour. Vonarburg/*Solaris*, Wilhelm-McIntyre/Clarion+ClarionWest, Onwualu/*Omenana*, Hopkinson/*So Long Been Dreaming*, Cheng Jingbo/*她*, Yang Feng/Bāguāngfēn, Jana Bianchi/*Mafagafo*, Lady Sybylla+Aline Valek/*Universo Desconstruído*, Bora Chung as translator, Singh as anthologist, Shawl/Carl Brandon. **Without this labour, most of the post-1990 entries would not have been published.** The museum has a strong opportunity to *make this labour visible* in a way the genre's standard self-narration doesn't.
- **"The first woman to win X" is a recurring data-point in the entries** (McCaffrey 1968 first Hugo for fiction by a woman; Norton first SFWA Grand Master 1984; Hopkinson first Black woman Damon Knight Grand Master 2021; Roanhorse first Indigenous Astounding Award 2018; Padmanabhan first Indian woman Onassis Prize 1997; Beukes first African Clarke Award 2011; Hao Jingfang first Chinese woman Hugo 2016; Damián Miravete first Latin American Tiptree 2018; Onwualu/Nommo Awards). This isn't a coincidence — it's a structural pattern about how prizes function as visibility-gates. Worth making visible.
