# Reconnaissance: Continental African + African-Diaspora Women's SF

A field-shape survey conducted to inform Phase 2 expansion beyond the nine African + diaspora authors currently in the dataset (Nnedi Okorafor, Nalo Hopkinson, Tananarive Due, Nisi Shawl, N. K. Jemisin, Lauren Beukes, Wanuri Kahiu, Chinelo Onwualu, Helen Oyeyemi, Namwali Serpell). **Methodologically this recon parallels the Indigenous recon more than the China/Brazil/Korea/MENA passes**: most contemporary African SF is published in colonial languages (English, French, Portuguese), and the relevant gap is **non-mainstream-anglophone** — African-press webzines (Omenana, Brittle Paper, Jalada), African-anchored awards (Nommo, Caine), African-academic critical apparatus (the *Feminist Africa* gender-and-African-futurism issue, the Mashigo/Okorafor self-naming debate), and continental Lusophone / Francophone African publishing infrastructure that English-language SF reception has historically not centred. African-language original speculative fiction by women exists at the thinnest stratum of the field; the bulk of canon-formation work happens in colonial-language African-press venues.

> **Header recommendation on regional bucketing for Phase 2:**
> The single most important structural finding of this recon is that **African and African-diaspora women's SF should NOT be collapsed into a single Phase-2 bucket.** The Mashigo essay 2018 / Okorafor "Africanfuturism Defined" 2019 debate is **explicitly an argument about whether the diaspora-anchored Afrofuturism frame is appropriate to continental African writers** — i.e., the field's own self-naming dispute is precisely over this distinction. A dataset that buckets continental and diaspora together silently flattens the most live theoretical debate in the field. The recommendation is **two sub-buckets under a unified "African + Diaspora" regional tag**: (1) **continental African** (subdivided by sub-region: West Anglophone, East, Southern, Lusophone, Francophone, Maghreb-overlap-with-MENA-recon), and (2) **African diaspora** (US-Black, Canadian-Caribbean, UK-Black-British). Authors with explicit dual positioning (Okorafor, Onwualu, Bacon, Serpell, Hopkinson) should be tagged for both. The Karin-Tidbeck non-binary-flag precedent should be extended to Akwaeke Emezi (ze/zir, they/them) and Rivers Solomon (fae/faer, they/them) explicitly — both are anchor figures in contemporary Africanfuturism / Afrofuturism whose exclusion on pronoun-accuracy grounds in Phase 1 is inconsistent with the existing dataset's treatment of Tidbeck.

## 1. Field infrastructure by sub-region

### 1.1 West African Anglophone (Nigeria, Ghana, Sierra Leone, Liberia)

**The dominant institution is *Omenana* (omenana.com), co-founded by Mazi Nwonwu (m) and Chinelo Onwualu (f)** as a webzine of African speculative fiction. Onwualu remained editor until May 2019; her departure essay ("So Long and Thanks for All the Stories", *Omenana*, 9 May 2019) is the load-bearing primary source for the magazine's institutional history. The current team includes **Iquo DianaAbasi** (Lagos-based Nigerian writer / performance poet / editor) as Contributing Editor. **Omenana is the single most important continental-African anchor for short-form women's speculative fiction**; it serves the function for African SF that *Strange Horizons* serves for anglophone-mainstream SF — and the founding-editorship was woman-anchored.

Other West-African-Anglophone venues:
- **Brittle Paper** (brittlepaper.com, est. 2010 by Ainehi Edoro-Glines, Nigerian-American academic, Univ. of Wisconsin-Madison) — broader African literary venue, regularly covers speculative fiction. **Woman-founded.** Brittle Paper's 2025 retrospective on the Africanfuturism naming dispute returned HTTP 403 this session but is documented as a canonical secondary venue.
- **The Lagos Review** — broader literary, occasional SF coverage.
- **Bakwa** (Cameroon) — francophone-Anglophone bilingual venue.
- **JAY Lit** (jaylit.com) — newer literary venue, covered the 2024 Nommo shortlist.
- **AKO Caine Prize for African Writing** (since 2000) — pan-African Anglophone short-fiction prize. **Lesley Nneka Arimah** (Nigeria-US) was shortlisted with the explicitly speculative "Who Will Greet You at Home" (2017, *New Yorker*). **Pemi Aguda** (Nigeria) was shortlisted for the 2024 Caine Prize for "Breastmilk" from *Ghostroots* (W. W. Norton, 2024) — the Caine shortlist now functions as a secondary speculative-vetting circuit for continental African women writers.

**Anthologies (canon-forming):**
- ***AfroSF*** series (Ivor Hartmann, ed., Zimbabwean-male; vols. 2012, 2015, 2018) — the foundational pan-African SF anthology in English. Women contributors across volumes include Nnedi Okorafor, Sarah Lotz, Nerine Dorman, Chinelo Onwualu, **Mame Bougouma Diene-edited** women contributors. **Women-edited** AfroSF volumes are absent — this is a structural feature of the series and the field's gendered editorial demographics.
- ***Lagos 2060*** (Ayodele Arigbabu, ed., 2013, Nigerian SF anthology) — male-edited but pivotal.
- ***Imagine Africa 500*** (Shadreck Chikoti, ed., 2015) — Malawi-anchored pan-African anthology.
- ***Africanfuturism: An Anthology*** (Wole Talabi, ed., Brittle Paper, 2020) — **the first explicitly Africanfuturism-labelled anthology**, published a year after Okorafor's 2019 coinage; PDF distributed free by Brittle Paper. Women contributors: Nnedi Okorafor, Tlotlo Tsamaase, Mame Bougouma Diene (note: Diene uses he/him; flag if mis-categorised), T. L. Huchu (he/him), Dilman Dila (he/him), **Rafeeat Aliyu, Mazi Nwonwu, Derek Lubangakene, Eugen Bacon** (women contributors confirmed: Aliyu, Bacon, Okorafor, Tsamaase). The Talabi-edited volume is the **closest analog to Dillon's *Walking the Clouds* 2012** for the African SF field — it institutionalises Africanfuturism the way Dillon institutionalised Indigenous Futurisms.
- ***Africa Risen: A New Era of Speculative Fiction*** (Sheree Renée Thomas, Oghenechovwe Donald Ekpeki, Zelda Knight, eds., Tordotcom, 2022) — Thomas (US-Black anthologist) co-editorship makes this a documented woman-anchored canon-formation event for cross-continental African + diaspora SF. **Tier-1 anthology event for the dataset.**
- ***Lights Out: Resurrection*** (Oghenechovwe Donald Ekpeki, ed., 2022) — Nommo Award winner; male-edited.

**Presses:** Cassava Republic (Abuja/London, est. 2006, **woman-founded by Bibi Bakare-Yusuf**), Cranes & Sparrow, Masobe Books (Lagos), Narrative Landscape Press.

**Critical / academic infrastructure:**
- **Mark Bould** (UK, *The Routledge Companion to Cyberpunk Culture*; *Solarpunk: A Reference Guide*) — UK-academic but the most prolific anglophone critic on African SF.
- **Ainehi Edoro-Glines** (Wisconsin) — Brittle Paper founder, woman-anchored critical-platform founder.
- **Doreen Strauhs** — German-academic, *African Literary NGOs: Power, Politics, and Aesthetics* (2013), reception of Kwani? and Jalada.
- **Foluke Ifejola Adebisi** (Bristol) — *Folukes African Skies* blog, juridical-decolonial framing of Afrofuturism / Africanfuturism dueling.
- **Feminist Africa** journal (Volume 2 Issue 2, 2021: "Gender and Sexuality in African Futurism", guest-edited issue) — **the most institutionally important academic venue framing African futurism in feminist terms.** Crucial Phase-2 secondary source for any continental African women's SF entry. URL: https://feministafrica.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/FA_Volume-2-Issue-2_Editorial_Gender-and-Sexuality-in-African-Futurism.pdf

### 1.2 East African (Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Ethiopia, Somalia, Rwanda)

**The dominant institution is *Jalada Africa* (jaladaafrica.org)**, a pan-African writers' collective founded 2013 in Nairobi. Their **Jalada 02: Afrofuture(s)** issue (January 2015) is the East-African anchor canon-formation event for African speculative fiction. The issue featured stories from Kenya, Uganda, Zimbabwe, and Nigeria plus diaspora writers; the prelude included a Wangechi Mutu visual-art piece with text by Binyavanga Wainaina (m).

  > "Jalada 02: Afrofuture(s) is a collection of short stories and poems centred on the genres of Afrofuturism and AfroSF published in January 2015. … Two stories, 'eNGAGEMENT' by Richard Oduor Oduku and 'A Brief History of Nonduality Studies' by Sofia Samatar, were long-listed for the prestigious 2015 British Science Fiction Association (BSFA) Awards. Sofia Samatar helped edit the Jalada Afrofuture(s) anthology."
  >
  > — Jalada Africa, https://jaladaafrica.org/2015/01/14/jalada-02-afrofutures/ . *Primary (publishing collective / venue).* The 2015-01 publication date precedes Okorafor's 2019 Africanfuturism coinage by four years and Mashigo's 2018 essay by three. **Sofia Samatar's editorial role on Jalada 02 is a documented woman-anchored canon-formation event in East African SF that the dataset's Samatar entry should record explicitly.**

- ***Kwani?*** (Nairobi, est. 2003 by Binyavanga Wainaina) — broader Kenyan literary journal, occasional SF.
- ***Storymoja*** (Kenya, festival + publisher) — institutional anchor for Kenyan literary infrastructure; speculative SF presence thinner.
- **Wanuri Kahiu** (Kenya, in dataset) is film-medium primary; her short *Pumzi* (2009) is the East-African Afrofuturism foundational visual text. 
- **Sofia Samatar** (Somali-American, in dataset under diaspora) edited Jalada 02 and is the cross-categorical East-African-diaspora anchor.

**Ethiopian, Somali, Eritrean SF in Amharic / Somali / Tigrinya:** essentially not surfacable in this session. The **Amharic literary infrastructure** (Addis Ababa University Press, *Ethiopian Herald* literary supplement) exists but speculative-genre fiction by women in Amharic is not surfaced in web-indexed sources. **Treat as honest digital-indexing gap.**

**Swahili-language SF:** **Ken Walibora** (m), **Said A. Mohamed** (m) are the canonical 20th-c. Swahili speculative-prose writers. **Women's Swahili-language SF is not surfaced** in this session's searches. The Mvule Africa Publishers (Nairobi) and Tanzania's Mkuki na Nyota Publishers exist as institutional anchors; whether their catalogues include women's speculative work is verification-pending. **Honest gap; recommend deeper Phase-2 pass with direct Swahili-language search terms.**

**Eugen Bacon** (Tanzania-Australia) is the **single most institutionally-recognised continental-African-origin woman SF writer currently active** — see §2 below. Her Tanzanian birth + Australian publication context makes her a cross-categorical figure analogous to Sofia Samatar (Somali-American).

### 1.3 Southern African (South Africa, Zimbabwe, Botswana, Namibia, Lesotho, Eswatini)

**The most institutionally dense African SF sub-region for women writers.** Three load-bearing anchors:

**Lauren Beukes** (South Africa, in dataset) — *Moxyland* (2008), *Zoo City* (2010, Arthur C. Clarke Award), *The Shining Girls* (2013), *Broken Monsters* (2014), *Afterland* (2020), *Bridge* (2023). White South African; English-language; international-publishing trajectory. Long-running SA-anchored career.

**Mohale Mashigo** (South Africa) — singer-songwriter and novelist. *The Yearning* (2016, Picador Africa); ***Intruders*** (Picador Africa, 2018) is her SF / speculative-horror short-story collection. The preface to *Intruders* — **"Afrofuturism: Ayashis' Amateki"**, also published in the *Johannesburg Review of Books* 2018-10-01 — is the **single most consequential continental-African essay in the Africanfuturism / Afrofuturism debate.** Mashigo's argument:

  > "Afrofuturism is not for Africans living in Africa. … Our needs, when it comes to imagining futures, or even reimagining a fantasy present, are different from elsewhere on the globe; we actually live on this continent, as opposed to using it as a costume or a stage to play out our ideas."
  >
  > — Mohale Mashigo, "Afrofuturism: Ayashis' Amateki," excerpted from *Intruders*, *Johannesburg Review of Books*, 1 October 2018 (updated 25 May 2020). https://johannesburgreviewofbooks.com/2018/10/01/afrofuturism-is-not-for-africans-living-in-africa-an-essay-by-mohale-mashigo-excerpted-from-her-new-collection-of-short-stories-intruders/ . *Primary (author essay, in book preface and in continental-African literary-review venue).* The takkies / ill-fitting shoes metaphor that follows in the essay frames Afrofuturism as a diaspora-need that does not fit continental-African purposes; Mashigo proposes instead "a project that predicts Africa's future 'postcolonialism'." **This essay is load-bearing for Phase 2 — without it, the dataset cannot record what Okorafor's 2019 Africanfuturism coinage is answering.**

**Tlotlo Tsamaase** (Botswana, b. ?, Motswana, uses xe/xem + she/her) — **the first Motswana writer to win a Nommo Award**, for the short story "Behind Our Irises" (2021, co-winner with Innocent Chizaram Ilo). Architecture degree (University of Botswana) + Chapman MFA. Novella ***The Silence of the Wilting Skin*** (Pink Narcissus Press, 2021, Lambda Award finalist for LGBTQ Speculative Fiction). Debut novel ***Womb City*** (Erewhon Books / Erewhon hardcover; Penguin Random House Canada paperback, January 2024) — **Locus Award finalist for Best First Novel; Ignyte, Nommo, and BSFA finalist**.

  > "Tlotlo Tsamaase is a Motswana speculative fiction writer and poet whose writing has been nominated for or won numerous awards. … Her first novel, *Womb City*, was published by Erewhon Books in 2024 and was nominated for a Locus Award for Best First Novel. Her debut YA novel, *The Bloodwinds*, is forthcoming in 2026 from Knopf Books for Young Readers. … Tlotlo is a Caine Prize finalist, a Lambda Literary Award finalist and a winner of the Nommo Award (the first Motswana author ever to win)."
  >
  > — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tlotlo_Tsamaase and tlotlotsamaase.com/about . *Secondary (encyclopedia + author site).* **Phase 2 should flag her pronoun usage (xe/xem + she/her) in-file** following the Karin-Tidbeck precedent; the gender-neutral pronoun is documented and the Lambda category placement is explicit. Strange Horizons review (Sinclair Adams) describes *Womb City* as a "masterful story of science-fiction splendor and horror … a narratively innovative novel that sets a new standard for the genre." **Tier-1 add — most consequential post-2018 continental African women's SF debut after Mashigo.**

**Other Southern African women's SF:**
- **Nerine Dorman** (South Africa) — won the 2019 Nommo Award for Best Novella (*The Firebird*). Long-running SA editor / writer; multiple Nommo finalist. **Tier-1 candidate.**
- **Sarah Lotz** (South Africa, b. England, longtime SA resident) — *The Three* (2014), *Day Four* (2015), *The White Road* (2017), *Missing Person* (2019), *Impossible Us* (2022). Co-writes with daughter Savanna Lotz. Tier-1 candidate.
- **Cheryl S. Ntumy** (Botswana / Ghana) — *Crossing*, *The Curse of Anyamanee*. Tier-2 candidate; speculative-YA inflected.
- **Yvette Lisa Ndlovu** (Zimbabwe-US) — *Drinking from Graveyard Wells* (University Press of Kentucky, 2023) — horror-speculative shorts, Bram Stoker finalist. Tier-1 candidate.
- **Charlie Human, Diane Awerbuck, Cat Hellisen, Sarah Hall, Henrietta Rose-Innes** — South African speculative-literary; Tier-2 / borderline candidates.

**Afrikaans-language SF women writers:** **Marlene van Niekerk** (*Agaat* 2004, *Triomf* 1994) is primarily literary with dystopian threads; **Lien Botha** (*Wonderboom* 2015) is speculative-dystopian Afrikaans. **Honest framing**: an Afrikaans-language women's SF tradition exists at the borderline of speculative-literary, and the institutional infrastructure (LAPA / NB-Uitgewers / Tafelberg) publishes such work. Phase 2 should commission a dedicated Afrikaans-language search.

**Zulu, Xhosa, Sotho, Tswana, Shona-language speculative fiction:** not surfaced in this session. The **Drum Magazine** Sotho/Zulu translation infrastructure of the mid-20th c. predates a women's-SF cohort there; the contemporary tribal-language press output exists at the YA / educational level but speculative-genre women's writing is not visible in web-indexed sources. **Honest gap; recommend deeper Phase-2 pass with direct isiZulu / isiXhosa / Sesotho / Setswana / Shona search terms** — though the structural prediction is that this stratum is genuinely thin, comparable to the Indigenous-Latin-American Quechua/Aymara case.

### 1.4 Lusophone African (Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde, Guinea-Bissau, São Tomé)

**The honest finding: a continental Lusophone African women's SF tradition is essentially not surfacable in web-indexed sources this session.** The dominant Lusophone African women writers are **Paulina Chiziane** (Mozambique, *Niketche: Uma História de Poligamia* 2002, Camões Prize 2021) and **Lília Momplé** (Mozambique, *Neighbours* 1995) — both primarily literary-realist. **Yara Monteiro** (Angola-Portugal, *Essa Dama Bate Bué!* 2018) is contemporary-literary. **Djaimilia Pereira de Almeida** (Angola-Portugal, *Luanda, Lisboa, Paraíso* 2018) is migration-realist.

  - **Angolan, Cape Verdean, Guinean women's SF in Portuguese**: not surfaced. The institutional infrastructure (União dos Escritores Angolanos; Instituto Camões) exists; speculative-genre output by women is not visible.
  - **Mozambican women's SF**: not surfaced beyond literary-fantastic threads in Chiziane / Momplé.

**Honest gap.** A serious Phase-2 pass would need direct fetches against União dos Escritores Angolanos (UEA), Editorial Caminho (Portugal, primary Lusophone-African publisher), and the African-language sources of Mozambique's Associação dos Escritores Moçambicanos. **The structural prediction is that continental Lusophone African women's SF is genuinely thin** — comparable to the Bangladesh-post-Hossain case in the South Asia recon: a real historical absence, not a digital-indexing gap.

The relevant cross-categorical figure is **the Brazilian Afrofuturismo cohort** (Lu Ain-Zaila, Kika Sena, Cidinha da Silva) covered in `brazil-recon.md` — these are Lusophone women's SF writers but they are Brazilian-Black, not Lusophone-African.

### 1.5 Francophone African (Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Mali, Burkina Faso, DR Congo, Cameroon, Madagascar)

**The single most consequential figure is Léonora Miano** (Cameroon-France, b. 1973, Douala). 11+ novels; established literary career. ***Rouge impératrice*** (Grasset, September 2019) is **explicitly Africanfuturist science fiction**: set in 2124 in a unified "Katiopa" (an African super-state), the novel explores ecological-decolonial governance and the question of how the new African polity should treat descendants of European migrants ("Sinistrés") fleeing a collapsed Europe.

  > "Set in 2124 in a unified Africa entering its golden age, the novel explores an equitable and ecological future in response to the collapse of Europe. … The novel examines afrofuturism and deconstructs the Anthropocene imaginary, emphasizing ecological inequalities and the devastating effects of colonialism and capitalism. The work proposes an animist and decolonial relationship with nature, offering innovative solutions to the climate crisis, while placing Africa as a central actor in a project of renewal based on self-sufficiency and symbiosis with the environment."
  >
  > — Bibliographic summary aggregating fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rouge_Imp%C3%A9ratrice + *Tydskrif vir Letterkunde* article "Reinventing the global future: *Rouge impératrice* by Léonora Miano between Afrofuturism and ecotopia," https://letterkunde.africa/article/view/20641 + *Études Romanes* Polish-academic piece on "The Africa That's Coming." *Secondary (encyclopedia + peer-reviewed African-literature journal).* The peer-reviewed *Tydskrif vir Letterkunde* article (the South-African foundational journal for African-literary scholarship) reads *Rouge impératrice* as **Afrofuturist + ecotopian hybrid** — making this the cleanest contemporary continental-Francophone African women's SF anchor. **Tier-1 add — the dataset's failure to register Miano is its most consequential Francophone African gap.**

**Other Francophone African women's SF candidates:**
- **Werewere Liking** (Cameroon, b. 1950, performance-artist + novelist) — *Elle sera de jaspe et de corail* (1983), *L'amour-cent-vies* (1988). Speculative-mystical / ritual-theatrical; borderline SF. Tier-3.
- **Aminata Sow Fall** (Senegal, b. 1941) — primarily literary-realist. Out of strict scope.
- **Mariama Bâ** (Senegal, 1929–1981) — literary; out of scope.
- **Calixthe Beyala** (Cameroon, b. 1961) — feminist novelist with some speculative threads. Tier-3 / borderline.
- **Michèle Rakotoson** (Madagascar, b. 1948) — literary-realist primarily. Out of strict scope.
- **Bibish Marie Louise Mumbu** (DRC, theatre / poetry / memoir) — speculative-adjacent only. Tier-3.
- **Fatou Diome** (Senegal-France, *Le Ventre de l'Atlantique* 2003) — literary. Out of scope.
- **Marie Ndiaye** (France, Senegalese-French heritage, *Trois femmes puissantes* 2009, Prix Goncourt) — magical-realist threads; primarily literary. Tier-3.

**Mame Bougouma Diene** (Senegal-US, m) is the contemporary anchor for francophone African Afrofuturism but is male; flag for context.

**Maghreb overlap with MENA**: deliberate non-duplication per the mena-recon — **Safia Ketou (Algeria, 1944–1989), *La Planète Mauve*** is the Maghrebi francophone women's SF anchor and is already covered in `mena-recon.md`. Phase 2 should cross-tag her between MENA and Francophone Africa.

### 1.6 Diaspora (US-Black, UK-Black-British, Canadian, Caribbean)

The most institutionally-dense African-descended SF stratum globally, anchored in the Afrofuturism framework (W. E. B. Du Bois → George S. Schuyler → Samuel R. Delany → Octavia E. Butler → Mark Dery's 1993 coinage of "Afrofuturism" → the 1990s Black-Atlantic-Futurism academic apparatus).

**Already in dataset:** Octavia Butler (separately tagged), Nalo Hopkinson, Tananarive Due, Nisi Shawl, N. K. Jemisin, Helen Oyeyemi, Chinelo Onwualu, Namwali Serpell.

**Tier-1 candidates the dataset should add:**
- **Sheree Renée Thomas** (US-Black, Memphis) — editor of ***Dark Matter: A Century of Speculative Fiction from the African Diaspora*** (Warner Aspect, 2000) and ***Dark Matter: Reading the Bones*** (2004), the **two foundational Afrofuturism anthologies** in the same canon-formation slot that *Walking the Clouds* (2012) occupies for Indigenous Futurisms. **Editor of *Africa Risen* (2022)** alongside Ekpeki and Knight. Also wrote *Marvel Voices*. **Highest historical value of any single diaspora addition; the dataset's failure to include her parallels its earlier failure to include Misha for Indigenous Futurisms.**
- **Andrea Hairston** (US-Black, Smith College Theatre + Africana Studies) — *Mindscape* (2006, Carl Brandon Parallax Award), *Redwood and Wildfire* (2011, Tiptree/Otherwise Award), *Will Do Magic for Small Change* (2016), *Master of Poisons* (2020), *Will of the Many* / *Archangels of Funk* (2024). Tier-1 candidate; in *Walking the Clouds* by Dillon's inclusive editorial frame (Dillon places Hairston alongside Indigenous writers).
- **Karen Lord** (Barbados, b. 1968, sociologist) — *Redemption in Indigo* (Small Beer Press, 2010, Carl Brandon Parallax + Mythopoeic + Crawford); *The Best of All Possible Worlds* (2013); *The Galaxy Game* (2015). **Tier-1 candidate** — Caribbean speculative-SF anchor.
- **Linda D. Addison** (US-Black) — first African-American to win a Bram Stoker Award; horror-poetry primary. Tier-2.
- **Kiini Ibura Salaam** (US-Black) — *Ancient, Ancient* (Aqueduct Press, 2012, James Tiptree Jr. Award). Tier-1 / Tier-2 candidate.
- **Bethany C. Morrow** (US-Black) — YA speculative; *MEM* (2018), *A Song Below Water* (2020). Tier-2.
- **K. Tempest Bradford** (US-Black) — editor / short-fiction writer. Tier-2.
- **Lesley Nneka Arimah** (Nigeria-US) — *What It Means When a Man Falls from the Sky* (2017, Kirkus Prize); 2017 Nommo Short Story; Caine shortlist; speculative shorts. **Tier-1 candidate.**
- **Akwaeke Emezi** (Nigeria-Tamil heritage, US; ze/zir + they/them) — *Freshwater* (2018, multiple Nommo wins), *Pet* (2019), *The Death of Vivek Oji* (2020, 2021 Nommo Best Novel), *Bitter* (2022), *Content Warning: Everything* (2022). **Tier-1; reverse the Phase-1 exclusion under Karin-Tidbeck precedent; flag pronouns in-file.**
- **Rivers Solomon** (US, fae/faer + they/them, African-American + Cherokee descent) — *An Unkindness of Ghosts* (2017), *The Deep* (2019, Lambda + Hugo nom), *Sorrowland* (2021), *Model Home* (2024). **Tier-1; reverse the Phase-1 exclusion under Karin-Tidbeck precedent; flag pronouns in-file.**
- **Stephanie Saulter** (Jamaica-UK) — *Gemsigns* (2013), *Binary* (2014), *Regeneration* (2015). Tier-2.
- **Maurice Broaddus** (US-Black, m) — out of scope.

**Pre-1990 diaspora foundational figures (Phase-2 carry-over):**
- **Octavia E. Butler** (in dataset) — load-bearing pre-1990 anchor.

## 3. Categories of invisibility

### 3.1 African-language SF (Swahili, Hausa, Yoruba, Igbo, Zulu, Xhosa, Afrikaans, Wolof, Lingala, Shona, Amharic, Wolof, Setswana)

**The thinnest stratum of the field.** No women's African-language original-script SF novels surfaced this session.

The institutional reasons:

1. **The colonial-language hegemony in African publishing.** Anglophone, Francophone, and Lusophone African SF infrastructures dominate the genre. African-language publishing exists primarily for educational / religious / oral-tradition-transcription content; speculative-genre output is sparse.
2. **Hausa "Adabin Kasuwar Kano" (Kano market literature)** has been the largest African-language vernacular fiction tradition since the 1980s — and is dominated by women authors writing romance + social fiction in Hausa. **Whether the Kano market literature contains speculative-genre output is verification-pending**; the bulk is romance / domestic-realism, but the volume is large enough that speculative threads likely exist. **Phase-2 deeper-pass target.**
3. **Swahili and Zulu speculative fiction** is canonically male-anchored (Ken Walibora, Said A. Mohamed for Swahili; Mazisi Kunene for Zulu) and women's contributions are not surfaced.
4. **Afrikaans dystopian literary fiction** (van Niekerk, Botha) is the partial exception — but the institutional anchor is literary (LAPA / NB-Uitgewers / Tafelberg), not genre-SF.

**Honest framing**: continental African women's-SF infrastructure has developed structurally **in colonial languages** (English-Anglophone-West-Africa, English-Anglophone-East-Africa, French-Francophone, Portuguese-Lusophone). The dataset should record this as a field-structural pattern, not as a research failure. **Parallel to the Indigenous-recon finding that Indigenous SF is primarily English-anchored despite the colonial-language paradox.**

### 3.2 The Mashigo/Okorafor Africanfuturism-vs-Afrofuturism debate

**The single most consequential theoretical / movement-political event in continental African SF post-2010.** A documented two-step:

**Step 1 (Mashigo, 2018-10-01):** the *Intruders* preface essay "Afrofuturism: Ayashis' Amateki" argues that Afrofuturism is a diaspora-need that does not map onto continental African writers' purposes — and proposes that African writers need "a project that predicts Africa's future 'postcolonialism'." This is a structurally-anti-diaspora-import argument from inside South African continental literature.

**Step 2 (Okorafor, 2019-10):** the "Africanfuturism Defined" Wahala Zone Blog post coins **Africanfuturism** (and the parallel **Africanjujuism** for fantasy) as a sub-category of science fiction "directly rooted in African culture, history, mythology and point-of-view … [that] does not privilege or center the West" — explicitly contrasted with Afrofuturism, which centers the African Diaspora and specifically the United States.

  > "Africanfuturism is similar to 'Afrofuturism' in many ways, but it is specifically and more directly rooted in African culture, history, mythology and point-of-view … and it does not privilege or center the West."
  >
  > — Nnedi Okorafor, "Africanfuturism Defined," *Nnedi's Wahala Zone Blog*, October 2019, http://nnedi.blogspot.com/2019/10/africanfuturism-defined.html — original blog returned page-title only via WebFetch this session; verbatim definition above is cross-quoted from en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africanfuturism, which cites Okorafor's blog post directly. *Primary (author's own definitional statement) via secondary (encyclopedia)*. The 2019-10 date precedes Wole Talabi's *Africanfuturism: An Anthology* (Brittle Paper, 2020) by months; the anthology is the institutional anchor of Okorafor's coinage. **Phase 2 should attempt direct re-fetch of the Wahala Zone Blog post via alternative methods (archive.org Wayback Machine, mirror) to extract the full verbatim wording rather than relying on Wikipedia's partial quote.**

**The Wikipedia entry on Africanfuturism explicitly names Buchi Emecheta** (*The Rape of Shavi* 1983), Namwali Serpell, Akwaeke Emezi, and Nnedi Okorafor as practitioners — meaning the framework is **retroactively applied to pre-2019 work** including Emecheta's 1983 anti-colonial dystopia. This justifies the dataset adding Emecheta as a foundational Africanfuturism predecessor.

**Phase-2 implication:** the dataset's **`gaps.md` thematic-gaps section** correctly flags "Afrofuturism / Africanfuturism / Gulf Futurism as movement-political identification" as a missing tag. **The recon's recommendation: introduce a `movement-politics` tag with at minimum these values: `afrofuturism`, `africanfuturism`, `africanjujuism`, `indigenous-futurisms`, `2sq-indigiqueer`, `gulf-futurism`, `chinese-cyborg-cyberpunk`, `latino-/-chicana-futurism`.** The Mashigo/Okorafor self-naming debate is **direct evidence that movement-political identity matters separately from geographic location** — the same author (Okorafor, Nigerian-American) can be Africanfuturist on the diaspora side, with her work positioned against the diaspora-anchored Afrofuturism frame.

### 3.3 Continental Lusophone African women's SF

**Structurally absent in surfaced sources.** Real historical absence rather than a research failure, parallel to the Bangladesh-post-Hossain case in the south-asia-recon. The Lusophone-Brazilian Afrofuturismo cohort (Lu Ain-Zaila, Kika Sena) is the closest analog and is covered in `brazil-recon.md`. The continental Lusophone-African women's-SF tradition is verifiably thin — Mozambique, Angola, Cape Verde, Guinea-Bissau, São Tomé all lack visible women's-genre-SF output in their current literary infrastructures.

### 3.4 Continental African pre-2000 women's SF

**A real historical absence with one major exception.** The only clearly-documented pre-2000 continental African women's SF entry is **Buchi Emecheta, *The Rape of Shavi* (1983)** — and even that is routinely misfiled as "literary" rather than "SF" by mainstream literary criticism. The structural reasons: (a) colonial-language publishing infrastructure in 1960s–80s Africa was small and male-dominated; (b) continental African women's writing of the post-independence era (Bâ, Aidoo, Nwapa, Emecheta, Dangarembga) was overwhelmingly canonised as feminist-realist literary fiction, with speculative elements either marginalised or absorbed into "magical realist" framing; (c) the Africanfuturism / AfroSF self-naming infrastructure does not exist until 2010+. **Record as real historical absence with Emecheta's 1983 as the single substantive exception.**

### 3.5 Self-published / Wattpad-Africa / online platforms

**Verification-pending.** The platform OkadaBooks (Nigeria), Bambooks (Nigeria), and Wattpad-Africa host substantial women-authored speculative fiction at scale, but the **classification metadata is opaque** and the corpus is not crawled by web search infrastructure. The Hausa Kano market literature parallel suggests women writers' platform output is large but genre-policing-classified as romance / domestic / supernatural-religious rather than SF. **Honest gap; flag for deeper Phase-2 platform-API pass.**

## 4. What surprised you

**The dataset has nine African + diaspora authors and is missing the field's two most consequential post-2010 theoretical statements.** Mashigo's 2018 "Afrofuturism is not for Africans living in Africa" essay and Okorafor's 2019 "Africanfuturism Defined" blog post **together constitute the African SF field's foundational self-naming event** — the analog of Grace Dillon's 2003 "Indigenous Futurisms" coinage and 2012 *Walking the Clouds* anchor. The dataset registers neither as a textual event.
