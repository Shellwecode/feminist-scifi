# Reconnaissance: Brazilian Women's SF (Portuguese-language sources)

A source-language survey of the field's shape — infrastructure, candidates, structural absences, sourcing reliability — conducted to inform Phase 2 expansion beyond the single Brazilian author currently in the dataset (Finisia Fideli, included as placeholder for the entire pre-2000 women's SF tradition). The memo follows the source priority rule: Portuguese-language sources first, English secondary only as confirmation. Original-language titles in the body (*Eles Herdarão a Terra*) with English gloss in parens on first mention.

> **Corrections from prior recon / dataset (logged during this session):**
> - **`gaps.md` lists "Sonia Sant'Anna" as a pre-2000 Brazilian women's SF author.** Source-language sources do not support this categorisation. Sonia Sant'Anna (b. São Paulo, sister of the late Sérgio Sant'Anna) is a writer of historical fiction; her 2021 collection *Rondó* (Editora Penalux) marks her return to the short story after a ten-year gap and is autobiographical-historical, not SF. No Portuguese-language source surfaced this session places her in any SF anthology, fanzine record, or in Causo's surveys. **Recommend removal from the Brazilian SF gap list, or reclassification as "speculative-adjacent literary, unverified."**
> - **A widely shared English-language summary (PublishNews / Brazilian Publishers digest) attributes the Argos-winning novel *A Mão que Pune – 1890* (2018) to Cristina Lasaitis.** This is wrong. The novel is by **Octavio Aragão** (male; Wikipedia-verified, Caligari publisher). Lasaitis is a real Brazilian-SF "third wave" author with her own corpus, but *A Mão que Pune* is not part of it. Any Phase-2 entry citing this attribution should be reviewed.
> - **`authors-raw/fideli-finisia.md` references "GRC (Grupo de Leitores de Ficção Científica)".** The actual organisation name is **CLFC (Clube de Leitores de Ficção Científica)**, founded 1985. "GRC" does not match any surfaced Brazilian SF institution. Likely a typo for CLFC; flag for correction at Phase-2 normalisation. The same file also dates the "Primeira Onda" as "c. 1960–79" — the canonical Wikipedia-PT periodisation, traceable through Causo / CLFC sources, dates **the Primeira Onda from 1957** (Editora GRD's foundational period centred on *Antologia Brasileira de Ficção Científica* 1961) **and the Segunda Onda from 1982** (the *Isaac Asimov Magazine BR* / Somnium / Causo cohort). Fideli's main publication arc places her in the Segunda Onda. Her 1978 "Os Deuses de Arkhalhag" actually predates the Segunda Onda's institutional start — making her a transitional figure rather than a clean First Wave or Second Wave member.
> - **The fideli-finisia.md sources field cites "https://sf-encyclopedia.com/entry/fideli_finisia"** — this URL needs verification; SFE entries on Brazilian women writers are inconsistently present. If absent, the entry currently rests primarily on Causo's books, which is acceptable but should be made explicit.

## 1. Field infrastructure

### 1.1 Magazines, fanzines, webzines

The Brazilian SF print ecology is much smaller than the Sinophone or Anglophone equivalents and has historically been organised around fanzines and short-lived magazines rather than a dominant flagship like 《科幻世界》 or *Asimov's*.

- ***Magazine de Ficção Científica*** (Brazilian edition of *F&SF*, ed. Jerônymo Monteiro), 1970–1971, 22 issues — the foundational pulp-era venue but folded fast.
- ***Isaac Asimov Magazine*** (Brazilian edition, Editora Record, 1990–1993, ~28 issues, ed. Ronaldo Sardenberg / coordenação Jorge Luiz Calife) — the single most important venue for the **Segunda Onda** (Second Wave). Finisia Fideli, Roberto de Sousa Causo, Jorge Luiz Calife, Bráulio Tavares, and a small cohort of women writers published here. Closure of *IAM-BR* in 1993 effectively ended the print-magazine era for Brazilian SF.
- ***Somnium*** (CLFC's official fanzine, 1985–present, monthly/irregular) — has been continuously published for 40+ years and is the single longest-lived SF venue in Brazil, print and now web. Won the Prêmio Nova for "Melhor Fanzine" 1987, 1989, 1991; "Melhor Publicação Semi-profissional" 1994. **The Segunda Onda effectively cohered through Somnium's pages** (per CLFC's own historical statement).
- Other fanzines: ***Megalon***, ***Hyperspace***, ***Boletim Antares***, ***Astronauta*** — most active 1985–1995, mostly print-only and badly digitised. Genuine archival holes exist here.
- **Webzines (post-2010):** ***Mafagafo Revista*** (2018–, ed. Jana P. Bianchi), ***Trasgo*** (Editora Trasgo's fiction site), ***Plástico Bolha*** (broader speculative). Mafagafo's submissions page **explicitly invites and prioritises** women, racialised, LGBTQIA+, disabled, religious-minority and immigrant authors — making it the closest Portuguese-language equivalent to *Strange Horizons*' editorial politics.

### 1.2 Awards

- **Prêmio Nova** (1988–c. 2003): community-voted, CLFC-administered. The historical predecessor to Argos.
- **Prêmio Argos** (since 2008–2009 in current form, run by **CLFC** — *Clube de Leitores de Ficção Científica*): the single most authoritative "fantastic literature" prize in Brazil. Categories: novel, short story, anthology/collection (and minor categories). **Notable women laureates:**
    - **Giulia Moon** — *Kaori e o Samurai Sem Braço* (2012), Argos for best fantasy novel.
    - **Lis Vilas Boas** — *Garras*, Argos 2025 for best novel (debut).
    - **Ursulla MacKenzie** — *Pista Norte*, Argos 2025 best short story.
    - **Juliane Vicente** — *Despertar na Aurora Sideral*, Argos 2024 best short story (1st).
    - Other women appear in second/third places and shortlists across recent years.
- **Prêmio Jerônymo Monteiro** (run since 1986, intermittently, by *Isaac Asimov Magazine* / Devir / CLFC depending on edition) — short-story prize. Finisia Fideli appears in early editions; women winners need a roster pull.
- **Prêmio Odisseia** (since 2017, ed. Editora Draco / Bandersnatch / community partners) — newer fantastic-literature prize, partly overlapping Argos.
- *No "Prêmio Hugo do Brasil" exists*; speculation in some sources confuses it with the Argos. Drop from research framing.

### 1.3 Anthologies (canon-forming)

- ***Antologia Brasileira de Ficção Científica*** (Editora GRD, 1961, ed. Gumercindo Rocha Dorea) — the canon-founding First Wave anthology. **Dinah Silveira de Queiroz appears here**, alongside the all-male GRD generation (André Carneiro, Jerônymo Monteiro, Levy Menezes, Rubens Teixeira Scavone, Dinarte Albuquerque). Her presence in this anthology is the single most important institutional fact for placing her in the SF canon.
- ***Phantástica Brasileira*** (1985, ed. — ?), Finisia Fideli's "*Exercícios de Silêncio*" (1988 in fideli-finisia.md but more likely 1985 anthology contents) appears here.
- ***Os Melhores Contos Brasileiros de Ficção Científica*** (Devir, ed. Roberto de Sousa Causo), multiple volumes — Causo-curated retrospective canon.
- ***Universo Desconstruído*** (Editora Draco / Dame Blanche, 2013, co-org. Lady Sybylla & Aline Valek) — **the first explicitly feminist Brazilian SF anthology**; sequel 2015/2016. This is the single most important woman-organised institutional intervention in Brazilian SF and substantively changes the field's gendered shape post-2013.

  > "Também em 2013, as escritoras Lady Sybylla e Aline Valek lançaram a coletânea *Universo Desconstruído — ficção científica feminista*, a primeira coletânea do gênero no Brasil."
  >
  > [translated from Portuguese: original / English: "Also in 2013, the writers Lady Sybylla and Aline Valek launched the collection *Universo Desconstruído — feminist science fiction*, the first anthology of the genre in Brazil."]
  > — pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fic%C3%A7%C3%A3o_cient%C3%ADfica_do_Brasil. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* The "first feminist SF anthology in Brazil" claim is corroborated across the *Lady Sybylla* and *Ficção científica do Brasil* Wikipedia entries. The same overview entry confirms the Brazilian SF Primeira Onda's institutional periodisation: "*O marco inicial do período é o ano de 1957*" ("The initial marker of the period is the year 1957"); and the Segunda Onda's: "*É chamada de Segunda Onda da Ficção Científica Brasileira, com início em 1982*" ("It is called the Second Wave of Brazilian Science Fiction, beginning in 1982"). **This 1957 / 1982 periodisation is the canonical Wikipedia-PT framing and should be the dataset's anchor for Brazilian SF generation tagging**, replacing the loose c. 1960–79 framing in the existing fideli-finisia.md.
- ***Dinah Fantástica: Contos de Ficção Científica Reunidos*** (Editora Instante, 2022, ed. Fabiana Yoshikawa, with critical apparatus by Rita Lenira de Freitas Bittencourt and **Ana Rüsche**) — a 2022 reissue gathering *Eles Herdarão a Terra* (1960) and *Comba Malina* (1969). This volume is the load-bearing event that re-canonised Dinah Silveira de Queiroz as an SF author rather than a literary novelist. Ana Rüsche's authorship of the critical apparatus is a documented woman-to-woman canon-formation link.

### 1.4 Presses

- **Editora GRD** (1957–): historic foundational SF press; closed/dormant.
- **Devir Livraria** (São Paulo, 1990s–): Causo's primary publisher; flagship Brazilian SF press for the 1990s–2010s.
- **Editora Aleph** (translations primarily; SF / fantasy classics into Portuguese).
- **Editora Draco** (2008–): genre-focused; published *Universo Desconstruído*. Closed c. 2018.
- **Mafagafo / Editora Patuá / Plutão Livros / Editora Skript / Editora Trasgo / Editora Bandeirola / Editora Caligari / Editora Penalux / Editora Instante**: contemporary small/independent presses publishing women SF authors. **Bandeirola** publishes Ana Rüsche's recent academic-creative *Quimeras do Agora* (2025); **Instante** is Dinah Silveira de Queiroz's posthumous SF reissue press.
- **Suma de Letras / Companhia das Letras / Record / Rocco**: large trade houses, occasional SF; mostly translations.

### 1.5 Academic and critical infrastructure

- **Roberto de Sousa Causo (b. 1965, PhD USP 2013)**: the gatekeeper-historian. Married to Finisia Fideli (since 1991) — a fact that should be flagged in the influence-edge architecture: most surfaced documentation of Fideli passes through her husband-historian, which is a methodological caveat, not a delegitimising one. Key works: ***Ficção Científica, Fantasia e Horror no Brasil: 1875 a 1950*** (Editora UFMG, 2003); critical introductions in numerous Devir anthologies.

  > "Casou-se com a também escritora de ficção científica Finisia Fideli em 1991, quando passou a viver em São Paulo, capital."
  >
  > [translated from Portuguese: original / English: "He married Finisia Fideli, also a science fiction writer, in 1991, when he moved to live in São Paulo, the capital."]
  > — pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roberto_de_Sousa_Causo. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* This 1991 marriage date should be added to *fideli-finisia.md*; it is documented in the open record and matters as a methodological caveat for documentation chain.
- **M. Elizabeth Ginway** (Univ. of Florida): ***Ficção Científica Brasileira: Mitos Culturais e Nacionalidade no País do Futuro*** (PUC-Rio, 2005; English edition *Brazilian Science Fiction*, Bucknell UP, 2004). Anglophone but writing in / translated into Portuguese; the most widely cited academic monograph on the field. Surveys 1960–2000. **Includes commentary on women writers including Ruth Bueno, Dinah Silveira de Queiroz**, and addresses race / gender in the genre's mythography. Ginway is the closest thing to an explicit feminist-inflected senior critic of Brazilian SF, though her frame is national-mythography first.
- **Bráulio Tavares**: writer-critic; long-running essays on Brazilian fantastic.
- **Ramiro Giroldo** (UFMS): younger academic; PhD on Brazilian SF / fantastic; publishes in *Abusões* (UERJ).
- **Ana Rüsche** (USP, post-doc): doctoral thesis "Utopia, feminismo e resignação em *The Left Hand of Darkness* (Le Guin) e *The Handmaid's Tale* (Atwood)" (FFLCH-USP). She is **a writer-critic hybrid** like Causo, but with explicit feminist-utopian theoretical commitments. Her presence in the *Dinah Fantástica* (2022) volume marks her as a canon-formation actor.
- **Revista Abusões** (UERJ): the single most important academic journal for Brazilian fantastic-literature studies. Hosts the 2018 paper "Cultura e política nos anos 2010: anseios e impasses na ficção científica de Aline Valek e Lady Sybylla" (Natascya Pereira Melo).
- **University programmes**: USP (FFLCH), UERJ (Abusões), UFMG, UFMS, UnB. Multiple ETD theses on Brazilian women's SF — at least Ana Rüsche's USP thesis (2015) and Natascya Pereira Melo's UnB thesis (2022) are open-web accessible.

### 1.6 Online / community infrastructure

- **CLFC (Clube de Leitores de Ficção Científica, 1985–)** at clfc.com.br — administers Argos and Somnium. **The single oldest and most continuous Brazilian SF institution, predating most contemporary websites and platforms.** Founded by Ivan Carlos Regina, Fritz Peter Bendinelli, Walter da Silva Machado.
- **Universo Galaxis** (universogalaxis.com.br) — Causo's blog and project hub.
- **Ficção Científica Brasileira** WordPress (ficcaocientificabrasileira.wordpress.com) — community blog, indexes Brazilian SF news with a Causo-aligned editorial line.
- **Trasgo / Mafagafo / Selo Multiversos / Ficções Humanas / Sci-fi Tropical / Mensagens do Hiperespaço / Curta Ficção podcast** — newer-generation web venues, post-2015.

### 2.2 Primeira Onda (c. 1957–1972): Dinah is the only certain woman

- **Dinah Silveira de Queiroz (1911–1982)** — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** Born São Paulo; major Brazilian literary author (*Floradas na Serra*, 1939, bestseller; *A Muralha*, 1954, adapted for TV; first woman elected to ABL São Paulo seat). Her two SF collections: ***Eles Herdarão a Terra*** (1960; "They Shall Inherit the Earth") and ***Comba Malina*** (1969). Re-edited as the single volume *Dinah Fantástica* (Editora Instante, 2022) with prefácio by Rita Lenira de Freitas Bittencourt and posfácio by **Ana Rüsche**.

  > "Dinah foi também uma pioneira da ficção científica brasileira."
  >
  > [translated from Portuguese: original / English: "Dinah was also a pioneer of Brazilian science fiction."]
  > — Editora Instante, *Dinah Fantástica* product page, https://editorainstante.com.br/produtos/dinah-fantastica/. *Secondary (publisher).* The same page frames her work as "penetrar nas camadas autoritárias e mergulhar em outros imaginários possíveis" (to penetrate authoritarian layers and dive into other possible imaginaries) — quoting Ana Rüsche's posfácio. The pioneer framing is publisher-paratext but is corroborated by the Editora Instante substack, which states "*A autora, ainda, é considerada uma das pioneiras da ficção científica nacional por ter escrito os contos de Eles herdarão a terra (1960) e Comba Malina (1969)*" ([translated from Portuguese: original / English: "The author, moreover, is considered one of the pioneers of national science fiction for having written the stories of *Eles herdarão a terra* (1960) and *Comba Malina* (1969)."]) — *Editora Instante substack, Quem foi Dinah Silveira de Queiroz?*, https://suminstante.substack.com/p/quem-foi-dinah-silveira-de-queiroz. *Secondary (publisher-essayistic).*

  **Substantively complicates `gaps.md`'s framing**: Brazilian women's SF has a foundational figure embedded in the GRD First Wave, contemporary with André Carneiro and Jerônymo Monteiro.

### 2.3 Late Primeira Onda / proto-Segunda (1970s)

- **Finisia Fideli (already in dataset)** — **Tier-1, well-documented but framing needs correction.** Active from late 1970s. Recon places her in the **Segunda Onda** (Second Wave, c. 1985–2000) rather than the Primeira; she belongs to the *Isaac Asimov Magazine BR* / *Somnium* cohort. The 1978 short story "Os Deuses de Arkhalhag" precedes the Second Wave but her main publication arc is 1985–2000s. Married to Causo since 1991.
- **Maria Cristina Brito** — **Suspected.** Mentioned in `gaps.md` framing context as a Segunda Onda contributor. Source-language confirmation thin this session.

### 2.4 Segunda Onda (c. 1985–2000)

- **The Segunda Onda is severely under-feminised in Brazilian SF historiography**, even relative to Brazilian SF's overall thinness. Apart from Fideli, few women are canonised in this generation. This is a real historical pattern documented in the *Universo Galaxis* / Causo blog and CLFC's own retrospective.

### 2.5 Terceira Onda / contemporary (2000s–2010s)

- **Giulia Moon** (pseudonym of **Sueli Tsumori**, Japanese-Brazilian, São Paulo) — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** Vampire / urban-fantasy / horror-SF crossover. *Kaori* series (2009–): *Kaori: Perfume de Vampira*, *Kaori 2: Coração de Vampira*, *Kaori e o Samurai Sem Braço* (2012, **Argos best fantasy novel**). Earlier collections: *Luar de Vampiros* (2003), *Vampiros no Espelho* (2004), *A Dama-Morcega* (2006), *Flores Mortais* (2014, all-women protagonists). ~20 books. **Genre is fantasy-horror more than SF** in the strict sense — defensible inclusion in the dataset under existing tolerance for Tanith Lee / Anne Rice-style horror-SF crossover.
- **Cristina Lasaitis** — **Tier-2, Suspected → Well-documented pending corpus verification.** Often called part of the "third wave" (e.g. Selo Multiversos roundup). Bibliography needs confirmation; **do not attribute *A Mão que Pune* to her** (Octavio Aragão's novel). She has appeared at Argos events as a panelist / commentator.
- **Lady Sybylla** (pseudonym of **Amanda Hoelzel**, b. Curitiba 1980-03-13) — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** Geographer / paleontologist (USP master's). Co-organised *Universo Desconstruído* (2013, **first feminist Brazilian SF anthology**) and its 2015/2016 sequel with Aline Valek. Space-opera novellas: *Deixe as Estrelas Falarem*, *Por uma Vida Menos Ordinária*. Maintains a long-running Medium / personal blog cataloguing ~30 SF subgenres.

  > "Lady Sybylla, pseudônimo de Amanda Hoelzel (Curitiba, 13 de março de 1980)"
  >
  > [translated from Portuguese: original / English: "Lady Sybylla, pen name of Amanda Hoelzel (Curitiba, March 13, 1980)"]
  > — pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lady_Sybylla. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* The same article describes *Universo Desconstruído* and its sequel as "as primeiras coletâneas deste subgênero da ficção científica no Brasil" ([translated from Portuguese: original / English: "the first collections of this subgenre of science fiction in Brazil"]) — confirming the "first feminist Brazilian SF anthology" claim. **Note**: the Wikipedia article categorises her under "Feministas do Brasil" but contains *no* mention of Afrofuturismo. Her primary self-positioning is feminist-SF, not Afrofuturismo brasileiro.

  **Note on Afrofuturismo framing**: source-language sources surfaced this session do *not* foreground her as an Afrofuturist author specifically; her primary self-positioning is feminist-SF. The Afrofuturismo brasileiro framing (the project brief's prompt) appears to be a misattribution; the women-writer Afrofuturismo cohort would more accurately centre on figures like Lu Ain-Zaila, Kika Sena, and others — see §3.1.
- **Aline Valek** — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** Co-organiser *Universo Desconstruído*. Subject (with Lady Sybylla) of the Abusões 2018 academic paper. Novel ***As Águas-Vivas Não Sabem de Si*** (Rocco, 2016).
- **Ana Rüsche (b. 1979, São Paulo)** — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** Writer-critic-academic. Novels per her own author-site bibliography: ***Acordados*** (Demônio Negro, 2007 — note: her own site lists Demônio Negro as publisher, while other sources cite Amauta 2007; verify which is the original), ***Do Amor — o Dia em que Rimbaud Decidiu Vender Armas*** (Quelônio, 2018), ***A Telepatia São os Outros*** (Monomito, 2019), ***Carga Viva*** (Rocco, 2025) — **a 2025 novel from a major literary press not previously surfaced**. Poetry: *Rasgada* (Quinze & Trinta, 2005), *Sarabanda* (Demônio Negro 2007 / Patuá 2013 reissue), *Nós que Adoramos um Documentário* (2010), *Furiosa* (2016), *Monstruosidades* (nosotros, 2019). Doctorate FFLCH-USP on Le Guin / Atwood. Postdoc on climate change and SF at USP, culminating in *Quimeras do Agora — Literatura, Ecologia e Imaginação Política no Antropoceno* (Bandeirola, 2025).

  > "Utopia, feminismo e resignação em *The Left Hand of Darkness* (de Ursula Le Guin) e *The Handmaid's Tale* (de Margaret Atwood)"
  >
  > [translated from Portuguese: original / English: "Utopia, feminism and resignation in *The Left Hand of Darkness* (by Ursula K. Le Guin) and *The Handmaid's Tale* (by Margaret Atwood)"]
  > — Ana Rüsche, doctoral thesis title, FFLCH-USP, 2015. As surfaced on her author site https://www.anarusche.com/sobre. *Primary (author's own self-description of scholarly orientation).* Establishes the explicit Le Guin / Atwood feminist-utopian theoretical anchor for her writer-critic hybrid practice.

  **The most academically credentialled feminist-SF writer-critic hybrid in the Brazilian field.**
- **Lidia Zuin (Lidia Zuin de Moura)** — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** Cyberpunk theorist-novelist. PhD Visual Arts (UNICAMP), MA Communication and Semiotics (PUC-SP). REQU13M trilogy (cyberpunk novels); *Dies Irae*; organised *2084: Mundos Cyberpunks* (35-story collection). Theoretical pieces in *Neon Dystopia*, Folha, UOL Tab; talks at FLIP, TED, Roadsec. **Specifically writes on Brazilian transgender cyberpunk** (Medium essay, "The Rise of Brazilian Transgender Cyberpunk"). Adds a documented cyberpunk-women axis the dataset currently lacks for Latin America.

## 3. Categories of invisibility

### 3.1 Afro-Brazilian women's SF / Afrofuturismo brasileiro

The project brief's framing of Lady Sybylla as the lead Afrofuturismo brasileiro author is **not supported by source-language sources surfaced this session**. Lady Sybylla's self-positioning is feminist-SF; her ethnic self-identification has not surfaced in the materials reviewed. The Afrofuturismo brasileiro discourse in Portuguese-language sources clusters around **male** figures including **Fábio Kabral** (*O Caçador Cibernético da Rua Treze*) and **Ale Santos** (*O Último Ancestral*) — and **women** including:

- **Lu Ain-Zaila** — author of *(R)Evolução: Brasil 2049* duology and *Ogum's Toques Negros*. Repeatedly cited in Brazilian Afrofuturism panels.
- **Kika Sena** — Afrofuturist writer; trans Black author cited in some 2020+ rosters.
- **Cidinha da Silva** — primarily literary-essayistic but with speculative threads (*Um Exu em Nova York*, 2018).
- **Heloisa Prieto** — older generation, fantasy-horror not specifically Afrofuturist but worth checking corpus.

The project brief's Afrofuturismo claim should be re-routed to these names rather than Lady Sybylla. **Treat this as a finding correcting the brief's framing.** Source-language verification this session was thin (Lu Ain-Zaila is verifiable via Editora Malê / interviews; Kika Sena and Cidinha da Silva need direct fetches in Phase 2).

### 3.2 Indigenous-Brazilian SF by women

Source-language search this session surfaced **essentially no women's Indigenous-Brazilian SF corpus**. **Daniel Munduruku** writes children's literature; **Davi Kopenawa**'s *A Queda do Céu* (with Bruce Albert) is anthropological-philosophical, not SF. **Eliane Potiguara** is essayistic and oral-tradition-anchored. **Julie Dorrico** and **Márcia Kambeba** publish poetry and oral-tradition-derived prose, not SF. Whether any contemporary Indigenous woman writes work that would be classifiable as SF — under either Western genre conventions or a futurismo indígena reading — is not surfacable in the source-language sources reviewed this session. **Treat as a real gap in the field, not a research failure**, with the caveat that "Indigenous-futurismo" as a self-organised movement is genuinely emergent and any writers may be in chapbook / cordel / oral-performance circuits not indexed online.

### 3.3 Regional / Northeastern voices

Brazilian SF infrastructure is heavily São Paulo / Rio. The Northeast, Centre-West, North, and South are under-represented in the prize-circuit roster. The 2019 *ricardoescreve* essay "Quem pode imaginar o Nordeste?" frames this as a chronic Brazilian-SF problem. Names that may belong to a Northeastern / regional women's-SF roster:

- **Sant'Clair Costa** (Brazil-born, but unclear regional positioning).
- **Lis Vilas Boas** — regional positioning needs verification.
- The cordel-tradition / Northeastern speculative fiction by women axis is genuinely thin in surfaced web sources.

This is the single largest geography gap inside Brazilian women's SF.

### 3.4 Self-published / fanzine / Wattpad authors

A great deal of women's speculative fiction in Brazil lives on **Wattpad, Kindle Direct Publishing (Amazon BR), and direct-to-Catarse / crowdfunding** circuits that the Argos / Odisseia prize architecture does not capture. Mafagafo Revista's Catarse crowdfunding (catarse.me/mafagafo) is one of the few professionalised links between this circuit and the prize-world. A serious dataset would acknowledge this corpus the way it acknowledges the Chinese 晋江 case — the print-prize roster systematically under-counts the women's-SF corpus.

The structural absence parallels the Chinese 晋江 case: women's speculative writing in volume but processed through a different genre / market / venue category and excluded from the SF canon by definition. Brazil's version is less institutionalised (no platform of 晋江's scale) but the genre-policing logic is similar.

## 4. What surprised you

**The dataset's gap isn't a writing gap — it's a translation and recanonisation gap.** Like the Chinese case, Brazilian women's SF is more substantial in source language than `gaps.md`'s framing implies. Dinah Silveira de Queiroz alone — an ABL-elected major Brazilian literary novelist who published two genre-SF collections in 1960 and 1969, and whose erasure from the SF canon was already the subject of a *Suplemento Pernambuco* essay before the 2022 *Dinah Fantástica* reissue — is enough to substantially reshape the chronology. She is contemporary with Carolina Maria de Jesus's late-1950s breakthrough, predates the Brazilian SF Primeira Onda's male-dominated GRD generation framing as the field's start, and is cited by Ginway (2004/2005). Her absence from the dataset is the load-bearing gap.

**The 2013 *Universo Desconstruído* anthology is the single most important woman-organised institutional event in Brazilian SF**, and `gaps.md` does not register it. Lady Sybylla and Aline Valek's editorial intervention created a feminist-SF anthology framework that the field had not previously had — and the framework persisted into the 2010s prize record and Mafagafo's editorial politics. The dataset misses this entirely.

**Ana Rüsche's role straddles writing and field-shaping in ways the dataset's category structure cannot represent cleanly.** She is a novelist (*Acordados*, *A Telepatia São os Outros*), an academic (Le Guin / Atwood thesis at FFLCH-USP), and a canon-formation actor (critical apparatus in *Dinah Fantástica*, 2022). The closest Chinese-case analogue is 程婧波 — writer-editor-organiser. Phase 2 should consider whether the dataset needs a "field-shaper" role tag distinct from "writer."

**Possible documented influence edges** (subject to primary-source verification before recording as edges):
- **Le Guin → Ana Rüsche** (her doctoral thesis is on *The Left Hand of Darkness*; this is a documented scholarly engagement, but a "thesis subject" is not the same as a self-reported influence statement — verification needs an interview where she states Le Guin shaped her own fiction).
- **Atwood → Ana Rüsche** (same caveat).
- **Dinah Silveira de Queiroz → Ana Rüsche** (Rüsche wrote critical apparatus for the 2022 *Dinah Fantástica* reissue — this is recanonisation, defensible as a documented influence-adjacent edge but specifically a critical-engagement edge, not a creative-writing edge).
- **Finisia Fideli ←/→ Roberto de Sousa Causo** (married since 1991; his historical work is the primary source for her career — this is a documented mutual relationship but not the kind of clean influence edge the dataset's strict rule wants. Flag as biographical-collaboration rather than influence.)

**The Sonia Sant'Anna placement in `gaps.md` is wrong** (see Corrections block at top). Whoever drafted the gaps file may have confused her with another author or relied on a misreading of an English-language summary. This is the kind of factual error that justifies the source-language-first rule.

## 5. Sourcing-quality notes

**What worked:**
- **Wikipedia (pt.wikipedia.org)** is generally reliable for biographical baselines on Brazilian SF authors. Lady Sybylla, Giulia Moon, Octavio Aragão, Roberto de Sousa Causo, CLFC entries are well-maintained. Ficção científica do Brasil overview entry is a good landing page.
- **CLFC site (clfc.com.br)** has its own institutional history page (clfc.com.br/quem-somos/) and a Somnium archive. Authoritative for prize and fanzine history.
- **Universo Galaxis / Ficção Científica Brasileira blogs** (Causo-aligned WordPress community) — useful for surfacing obscure authors and historical events; ideologically aligned with Causo's Devir-anchored canon, so read as such.
- **Rascunho (rascunho.com.br)** — quality literary monthly with consistent SF coverage; carried the 2022 *Dinah Fantástica* announcement.
- **Suplemento Pernambuco (suplementopernambuco.com.br)** — quality literary supplement; carried "Comba Malina e seu apagamento" essay.
- **Editora Instante's own substack / suminstante** — first-party publisher coverage of Dinah Silveira de Queiroz reissue.
- **Editora Bandeirola** — Ana Rüsche's current publisher; well-organised author page.
- **Goodreads (PT)** and **Skoob** — confirm publication dates, ISBN, edition history.
- **Revista Abusões (UERJ)** — academic journal; the load-bearing source for Lady Sybylla / Aline Valek critical reception.
- **Cult / Sesc São Paulo / Estado de Minas** — mainstream literary-cultural press; usable for interviews.

**What did not work / requires institutional access:**
- **BDTD (bdtd.ibict.br)** and **Teses USP repository (teses.usp.br)** — partial direct PDF access; some theses paywalled via federation. Ana Rüsche's USP thesis is openly available (teses.usp.br/teses/disponiveis/8/8147/tde-09092015-164853/publico/2015_AnaRusche_VCorr.pdf), but Natascya Pereira Melo's UnB thesis required separate institutional fetch.
- **CNKI-equivalent for Brazilian academia** does not exist as a single platform; SciELO is the closest but covers journal articles not theses.
- **Print-only fanzines (Somnium back issues 1985–2000s)** are partly digitised on yumpu.com but coverage is patchy. Pre-1995 *Somnium* issues are the most likely place to find Segunda Onda women writers and remain hardest to access.
- **Causo's own academic monographs (Editora UFMG, 2003)** — book-length, not on the open web in full text. Phase 2 needs library access.
- **Older author web pages from the 1990s–2000s** (the early Brazilian SF webring era) — many are dead links or absorbed into archive.org; surface erratically.

**Encyclopedia error patterns:** English-language secondary digests on Brazilian SF (PublishNews international summaries, Brazilian Publishers, occasional SFE entries) misattribute authorship and prize records — the *A Mão que Pune* / Lasaitis confusion is one example. **Always verify against the original Argos / CLFC roster** rather than relying on the English digest. Portuguese Wikipedia is generally more accurate than the English Wikipedia or SFE for the Brazilian SF cohort.

**One direct contradiction with `gaps.md`'s framing worth surfacing:** the gaps file frames pre-2000 Brazilian women's SF as a Causo-historiography problem requiring Portuguese-language sourcing to fill. The Causo dependency is real, but the bigger structural fact is that Dinah Silveira de Queiroz — a pre-1970 author with two genre-SF collections — exists in Brazilian literary canon already, was never lost, but was not categorised *as* an SF author until the 2022 Editora Instante reissue. The gap is not "writers who weren't there" but "writers who were never SF-canonised because they were already canonised as something else." This is a different kind of gap than the dataset's framing assumes.
