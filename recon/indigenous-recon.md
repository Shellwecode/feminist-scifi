# Reconnaissance: Indigenous Women's SF (Indigenous Americas, Pacific, Sápmi, Inuit)

A field-shape survey conducted to inform Phase 2 expansion beyond the single Indigenous author currently in the dataset (Rebecca Roanhorse, Diné/African-American descent — currently filed in the "MENA + other" bucket for lack of a regional category). The survey covers Indigenous Americas (North and South), Pacific (Aboriginal Australian, Māori, Pasifika), Sápmi/Sámi-Arctic, and Inuit traditions. **Methodologically this recon is structurally different from the Chinese and Brazilian source-language passes**: most contemporary Indigenous SF is published in colonial languages (English, Spanish, French) and the relevant gap is **non-mainstream-Anglophone** — Indigenous-press, tribal-college, and Native-academic-journal venues where the work is framed as Indigenous-cultural rather than mainstream-genre SF. Indigenous-language original speculative writing exists but is the thinnest stratum of the field; the bulk of the canon-formation work happens in colonial-language Indigenous-press / Native-academic-journal venues that anglophone SF criticism has historically not centred.

> **Header recommendation on regional bucketing for Phase 2:**
> The single most important structural finding of this recon is that **Indigenous SF is misbucketed in the existing dataset's regional taxonomy**. Roanhorse's placement in "MENA + other" treats Indigenous-Americas as a residual; this is incorrect for a feminist-SF dataset because Indigenous Futurisms (term coined by Anishinaabe scholar Grace Dillon, 2003+; canon-anchored 2012 with *Walking the Clouds*) is now a self-organised twenty-plus-year movement with its own scholarly apparatus, anthology series, presses, awards (Indigenous Voices Awards, Canada, 2017+), and a distinct critical vocabulary (slipstream, biskaabiiyang, survivance, Native apocalypse). **The recommendation is: split "Indigenous" into its own Phase-2 regional bucket, defined cross-continentally rather than by colonial nation-state geography.** Sub-region tags (Turtle Island North, Indigenous Latin America, Aboriginal Australian, Māori/Pasifika, Sápmi, Inuit Nunangat) should sit underneath. The dataset's existing Karin-Tidbeck-precedent for non-binary writers should also be extended explicitly to two-spirit / Indigiqueer authors (cf. Joshua Whitehead, the *Love after the End* anthology cohort) — flagged in-file rather than excluded.

> **Corrections from existing dataset / `gaps.md` (logged during this session):**
> - **Roanhorse's existing per-author file**: the dataset's current Roanhorse entry should be flagged for review against the **2018 14-Diné-writers letter**, the **Ohkay Owingeh leadership statement** by former Lt. Gov. Matthew Martinez, and **Dr. Jennifer Denetdale**'s critique. These are Indigenous-press / Indigenous-academic responses (Indianz.com, *American Indians in Children's Literature*) materially complicating Roanhorse's positioning. The recon does not resolve this; it surfaces it as a Phase-2 ethical question.
> - **`gaps.md`**: the named gaps (Cherie Dimaline, Louise Erdrich, Darcie Little Badger) are real and well-evidenced. **The gap list omits Misha (Misha Nogha)** — Métis-Cree author of *Red Spider, White Web* (1990), which is the **earliest Indigenous-woman cyberpunk novel by a substantial margin** and is canonised inside Dillon's *Walking the Clouds* anthology (2012). Adding Misha is the single highest-value historical correction the dataset can make. **The list also omits Alexis Wright** (Waanyi, *The Swan Book* 2013, climate-speculative), **Ambelin Kwaymullina** (Palyku, *Tribe* trilogy 2012–15), **Claire G. Coleman** (Wirlomin Noongar, *Terra Nullius* 2017), and **Tanya Tagaq** (Inuit, *Split Tooth* 2018) — all Pacific/Aboriginal/Inuit Indigenous women writers with explicit speculative-SF corpora.
> - **Indigenous-Latin-American framing**: a Phase-2 dataset should not treat Indigenous Latin America as a sub-bucket of "Latin America." Mapuche (Daniela Catrileo), Mixe (Yásnaya Aguilar Gil) and Quechua/Aymara writers belong with the Indigenous bucket, not under a Spanish-language Latin America bucket organised around Argentine/Chilean/Mexican-mestizo SF.

## 1. Field infrastructure

### 1.1 Indigenous Americas (North / Turtle Island)

**The dominant institution is the Indigenous Futurisms framework**, coined by Anishinaabe scholar **Grace L. Dillon** (Portland State, Indigenous Nations Studies) in 2003 and canon-anchored by her 2012 anthology ***Walking the Clouds: An Anthology of Indigenous Science Fiction*** (University of Arizona Press). The anthology is the foundational text of the field — sub-categorised into "Native Slipstream," "Contact," "Indigenous Science and Sustainability," "Native Apocalypse," and "Returning to Ourselves." It includes Misha (cyberpunk), Vizenor (slipstream), Silko, Sherman Alexie, William Sanders, Stephen Graham Jones, and others; it is the single text that retroactively rebuilt a canon for the field.

> "Although Grace Dillon first coined the term 'Indigenous Futurisms' in 2003, the first publication of its kind with a focus on Indigenous Futurisms, *Walking the Clouds*, was not published until 2012."
>
> — Grace Dillon, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Dillon. *Secondary (encyclopedia, but tracking Dillon's own published statements).* Dillon's 2003 SAIL article is the primary-source coinage; the 2012 anthology is the institutional anchor.

**Anthologies as canon-formation events:**
- ***Walking the Clouds*** (Grace Dillon, U Arizona Press, 2012) — foundational. Included Indigenous women: Diane Glancy, Misha, Celu Amberstone, Andrea Hairston (note Hairston is African-American, not Indigenous — Dillon's inclusion choices are sometimes broader than strict tribal-identity), Nalo Hopkinson (Black/Caribbean-diasporic, similarly), and excerpts from Silko and Erdrich. The "who counts as Indigenous SF" boundary is itself contested in Dillon's editorial frame.
- ***Mitêwâcimowina: Indigenous Science Fiction and Speculative Storytelling*** (Neal McLeod, ed., Theytus Books, 2016). **The first Indigenous SF anthology published in Canada**; eighteen authors. Women contributors: **Kateri Akiwenzie-Damm**, **Cherie Dimaline**, **Tania Carter**, **Rebecca Lafond**, **Lee Maracle**, **Eden Robinson**, **Cathy Smith**.
- ***Take Us to Your Chief and Other Stories*** (Drew Hayden Taylor, Douglas & McIntyre, 2016) — solo collection, not a multi-author anthology, but functioning as a canon-event for Indigenous SF in Canada.
- ***Love after the End: An Anthology of Two-Spirit and Indigiqueer Speculative Fiction*** (Joshua Whitehead, ed., Arsenal Pulp Press, 2020). **Won the Lambda Literary Award for LGBTQ Anthology.** Contributors: **Darcie Little Badger**, **Mari Kurisato**, **Kai Minosh Pyle**, David Alexander Robertson, **jaye simpson**, **Nathan Adler**, **Adam Garnet Jones**, **Gabriel Castilloux Calderón**, **Nazbah Tom**.

  > "Love After the End is an exciting and groundbreaking fiction anthology that showcases a number of new and emerging 2SQ (Two-Spirit and queer Indigenous) writers from across Turtle Island … contributors include Darcie Little Badger, Mari Kurisato, Kai Minosh Pyle, David Alexander Robertson, and jaye simpson."
  >
  > — Arsenal Pulp Press / Birchbark Books product page, https://arsenalpulp.com/Books/L/Love-after-the-End. *Secondary (publisher).* Whitehead is Oji-Cree/nehiyaw, Two-Spirit, Indigiqueer, member of Peguis First Nation (Treaty 1); his self-identification is non-binary (he/they). The anthology is the institutional event that anchored the 2SQ / Indigiqueer SF cohort; it is the closest analog to Whitehead's *Jonny Appleseed* moment but in genre form.

**Awards:**
- **Indigenous Voices Awards (IVA)** (Canada, 2017–): created via crowdfunding by Toronto lawyer Robin Parker as a response to the *Write Magazine* "Appropriation Prize" controversy. Categories include published and unpublished work in English, French, and Indigenous languages; speculative fiction is eligible across categories. **Tanya Tagaq won the 2019 IVA for Published Prose in English** for *Split Tooth*. The IVA has become the central Canadian Indigenous-literary prize and the most reliable vehicle for tracking Indigenous-press SF debuts.
- **Burt Award for First Nations, Inuit, and Métis YA Literature** (CODE, 2014–2019): now defunct but produced cohort-defining wins for Indigenous YA in the period of *The Marrow Thieves*' breakthrough.
- **Lodestar Award** (Hugo-affiliated, 2018–): **Darcie Little Badger won the 2025 Lodestar for *Sheine Lende*** (sequel to *Elatsoe*); *Elatsoe* (2020) was Lodestar/Nebula/Ignyte finalist. This is the cleanest mainstream-anglophone-SF recognition of Indigenous YA SF in the dataset's relevant period.

  > "More recently, at the 83rd World Science Fiction Convention in Seattle, tribe member Darcie Little Badger was awarded the 2025 Lodestar Award for Best Young Adult Book for her novel *Sheine Lende*."
  >
  > — Lipan Apache Tribe of Texas, https://www.lipanapache.org/LAT/Lodestar2025.html. *Primary (tribal-press, the author's own tribal nation reporting on her win).* Tribal-press confirmation matters here: the Lipan Apache Tribe's own newsletter / website is the load-bearing source for the tribal-positioning statement, not the WSFS press release.
- **Governor General's Literary Award** — **Cherie Dimaline** won the 2017 GG English-language children's-text award for *The Marrow Thieves*; the GG jury explicitly framed it as "speculative fiction with a chilling immediacy."

  > "*The Marrow Thieves* is speculative fiction with a chilling immediacy. Its unflinching narrative resonates in our disturbing times. Cherie Dimaline's exceptional writing and authentic characters pull you into a story that lingers and unsettles."
  >
  > — Governor General's Literary Awards 2017 jury citation, via *Quill & Quire* and 49th Shelf, https://quillandquire.com/omni/the-marrow-thieves-author-cherie-dimaline-remains-true-to-her-role-as-a-metis-storykeeper-amid-international-acclaim/. *Secondary (literary-trade press).* The GG citation is the canonical mainstream-Canadian recognition that Indigenous YA dystopian writing is "speculative fiction" rather than YA-realism.
- **Hugo / Nebula / Astounding-Campbell** — Roanhorse won the 2018 **Astounding Award for Best New Writer** (formerly the Campbell), Hugo and Nebula for "Welcome to Your Authentic Indian Experience™" (2018). Mainstream-anglophone SF recognition of Indigenous women began with this single year.

**Presses:**
- **Theytus Books** (En'owkin Centre, Penticton, BC, est. 1980) — the oldest Indigenous-owned publishing house in Canada. Published *Mitêwâcimowina* (2016).
- **Inhabit Media** (Iqaluit, Nunavut, est. 2006) — Inuit-owned, Inuktitut-and-English; publishes the Qitsualik-Tinsleys' speculative work and Inuit-folklore-derived YA SF.
- **Highwater Press / Portage & Main** (Winnipeg) — publishes Indigenous YA speculative including David Alexander Robertson's *Misewa Saga*.
- **Pemmican Publications** (Métis, Winnipeg) — long-running Métis-press; speculative output thinner than Theytus.
- **Arsenal Pulp Press** (Vancouver) — non-Indigenous-owned but the leading publisher of Two-Spirit / Indigiqueer work, Whitehead's *Love after the End* and *Jonny Appleseed*.
- **DCB / Cormorant Books** — Dimaline's *The Marrow Thieves* publisher.
- **Levine Querido** (US, 2019–) — Darcie Little Badger's US publisher (*Elatsoe*, *A Snake Falls to Earth*, *Sheine Lende*).

**Academic / critical infrastructure:**
- **Grace L. Dillon** (Anishinaabe, Portland State Univ., Indigenous Nations Studies) — coined "Indigenous Futurisms"; *Walking the Clouds* editor; bimaashi biidaas mose framework.
- **Daniel Heath Justice** (Cherokee Nation, UBC) — *Why Indigenous Literatures Matter* (2018); his term "wonderwork" is now widely cited as a non-Western-genre alternative to "fantasy/SF" and is invoked in *Split Tooth*'s critical reception.
- **Lou Cornum** (Diné, NYU) — emerging critical voice on Indigenous Futurisms.
- **Niigaan Sinclair** (Anishinaabe, U Manitoba) — editor and field-shaper.
- **Beth LaPensée** (Anishinaabe-Métis, Michigan State) — game-studies / digital-Indigenous work; runs the *Achimostawinan Games* studio.
- Journals: ***Studies in American Indian Literatures (SAIL)*** (peer-reviewed Native-studies journal where Dillon's 2003 coinage appeared); ***Native American and Indigenous Studies (NAIS)*** (NAISA's journal); ***Wicazo Sa Review*** (now-discontinued but historically central); ***American Indian Quarterly***. The NAISA conference has run dedicated Indigenous-Futurisms panels since the mid-2010s.
- **American Indians in Children's Literature** (AICL, Debbie Reese, Nambé Pueblo) — the load-bearing critical-blog venue for Indigenous-children's-and-YA-literature criticism, including the most-cited critique of Roanhorse.

### 1.2 Indigenous Latin America

The honest finding: the **Indigenous-Latin-American women's SF field is the thinnest stratum** of any region in this recon. Indigenous-language literary infrastructure exists (Mapuche/Mapudungun, Mixe/Ayuujk, Quechua, Aymara, K'iche', Yucatec Maya), but speculative-genre output by women is sparse and dispersed across essay, poetry, and oral-tradition forms.

**Mapuche (Chile/Argentina):**
- **Daniela Catrileo** (Mapuche, Santiago, b. 1987) — poet, philosophy teacher, member of Colectivo Mapuche Rangiñtulewfü. Her 2023 novel ***Chilco*** (Pez Espiral, then international editions) is **explicit near-future speculative fiction**: a climate-collapse / capitalism-collapse novel set in a future Santiago/Chilco. Translated into English by Jacob Edelstein (2025). **Tier-1 candidate; the clearest contemporary Indigenous-Latin-American women's SF case.**
- **Liliana Ancalao** (Mapuche, Argentina, b. 1961) — Mapuzungun poet and oral historian. Speculative-adjacent at most; primarily poetry. Probably out of the dataset's scope.

**Mixe (Mexico):**
- **Yásnaya Elena Aguilar Gil** (Ayuujk/Mixe, Ayutla Mixe, Oaxaca) — linguist, essayist, activist. The 2023 essay "Art, Literature, and Collective Earth Aesthetics" (*Words Without Borders*, English translation by John Pluecker) is **framed as an essay written from 2172 by the Mixe Communal Networks** — explicitly speculative-from-the-future, comparing the present "Dark Night of Capitalism" to a 22nd-century post-capitalist Mixe communal world. Whether this qualifies as SF or as essayism with speculative framing is a borderline call; defensible Phase-2 inclusion under the dataset's tolerance for speculative-essayistic work.

  > "Yásnaya Elena Aguilar Gil brings us a dispatch from the future in the form of an essay written in 2172 by the Mixe Communal Networks. Through a comparison between our own era, which they term the 'Dark Night of Capitalism,' and the twenty-second century, the Mixe Communal Networks explore what it means to produce art and literature and describe how Indigenous communities can (and will) lead the way toward a 'collective earth aesthetics.'"
  >
  > — *Words Without Borders*, https://wordswithoutborders.org/read/article/2023-10/art-literature-and-collective-earth-aesthetics-mixe-communal-networks-aguilar-gil-pluecker/. *Secondary (literary-translation venue).* The 2172-future-essay form is the primary-source feature; the WWB editorial framing confirms the speculative reading.

**Quechua / Aymara / K'iche' / Yucatec Maya:** No women's speculative-fiction corpus surfaced this session. Indigenous-language presses exist (Pakarina Ediciones in Peru for Quechua; Editorial 3.600 in Bolivia for Aymara; Editorial Maya'Wuj in Guatemala for K'iche'); whether any women writers in these traditions write what could be classed as SF is **not surfacable in the time-budget of this recon and may reflect a real digital-indexing gap rather than an absence of writers**. Treat as honest gap.

**Indigenous-Brazilian:** As the Brazilian recon already noted (`/Users/shelliehu/feminist-scifi/recon/brazil-recon.md` §3.2), Daniel Munduruku, Davi Kopenawa, and Eliane Potiguara are essayistic / oral-tradition / anthropological. Julie Dorrico and Márcia Kambeba publish poetry. **No Indigenous-Brazilian women's SF corpus surfaced** — treat as real field-shape gap, not a research failure.

### 1.3 Pacific (Māori + Pasifika)

**The Māori women's SF field is thin but emergent.** Māori speculative fiction has been more visible in oral-tradition retellings (the *Pūrākau* anthology, ed. Witi Ihimaera and Whiti Hereaka, 2019) than in genre-SF venues.

- **Whiti Hereaka** (Ngāti Tūwharetoa, Te Arawa) — playwright, novelist. *Bugs* (2013), *Legacy* (2018) — speculative-historical / time-travel YA. Co-edited *Pūrākau*. Tier-2 candidate.
- **Tina Makereti** (Ngāti Tūwharetoa, Te Ati Awa, Ngāti Rangatahi, Pākehā) — *Once Upon a Time in Aotearoa* (2010), *Where the Rēkohu Bone Sings* (2014), *The Imaginary Lives of James Pōneke* (2018). Speculative threads run through her work; not strictly genre SF. Tier-2.
- **Patricia Grace** (Ngāti Toa, Ngāti Raukawa, Te Āti Awa) — foundational Māori novelist; *Potiki* (1986) contains speculative-mythological elements but is primarily literary realism. Probably out of strict scope.
- **Hawai'i / Pacific Islander women SF:** **Lehua Parker** (Native Hawaiian) writes the *Niuhi Shark Saga* YA series — speculative, mythologically grounded, Hawaiian-cultural. **Bryan Kamaoli Kuwada** (male, Native Hawaiian, primarily essayist/translator; not in scope but flagging the institutional anchor). **Hawai'i Review**, **Mauri Ola** anthologies, the Albert Wendt-anchored *Whetu Moana / Mauri Ola* series — institutional scaffolding exists but is Pasifika-poetry-dominant. Speculative-fiction women's-writing in Hawaiian is thin in surfaced sources; warrants a Phase-2 deeper pass.

**Note**: the Pasifika SF field was substantially reshaped by **Audrey Brown-Pereira**, **Selina Tusitala Marsh**, and **Sia Figiel**'s poetry work — but again, these are speculative-adjacent literary, not genre SF. The strictest Pacific genre-SF women's case is currently **Whiti Hereaka** within Aotearoa.

### 1.4 Aboriginal Australian

**The Aboriginal Australian women's SF field has three load-bearing figures**, all post-2010:

- **Alexis Wright** (Waanyi, b. 1950) — *Carpentaria* (2006, Miles Franklin Award), ***The Swan Book*** (2013, climate-speculative), ***Praiseworthy*** (2023, Miles Franklin 2024). The Swan Book is the field's strongest case for Indigenous-Australian climate fiction.

  > "*The Swan Book* is set in the future, with Aboriginal peoples still living under the Intervention in the north, in an environment fundamentally altered by climate change."
  >
  > — Wikipedia, *The Swan Book*, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Swan_Book. *Secondary.* Wright is a Waanyi nation member from the southern highlands of the Gulf of Carpentaria; she has worked with Indigenous-Australian self-determination organisations since the 1970s. Her publisher is **Giramondo Publishing** (Sydney), an independent literary press that has been the venue for the Wright corpus throughout. The "speculative fiction" classification is mainstream-anglophone; Wright's own framing emphasises "all-times" Aboriginal narrative time, which is closer to Dillon's Native Slipstream framework than to Western SF chronology.
- **Ambelin Kwaymullina** (Palyku/Bailgu, Pilbara region, Western Australia) — *Tribe* trilogy: ***The Interrogation of Ashala Wolf*** (2012), ***The Disappearance of Ember Crow*** (2013), ***The Foretelling of Georgie Spider*** (2015), all Walker Books Australia. YA dystopian post-Reckoning (post-environmental-cataclysm) speculative-SF. Critical reception explicitly places the trilogy within Indigenous-Futurisms / "apocalyptic survivance" framing (see Murphy, "For Love of Country," cited in Dillon's editorial circle). **Tier-1 candidate.**
- **Claire G. Coleman** (Wirlomin Noongar) — ***Terra Nullius*** (2017, Hachette Australia, won state Premier's Black & Write Indigenous Writing Fellowship 2016 and Norma K. Hemming Award 2018) — alternate-history SF that begins as an apparent re-staging of the colonial-frontier narrative and reveals partway through to be a **post-human alien-colonisation SF set in the future**. The structural twist is one of the cleanest examples of Indigenous-SF defamiliarisation in the field. Subsequent: ***The Old Lie*** (2019), ***Enclave*** (2022). **Tier-1 candidate.**

Magabala Books (Broome, WA, est. 1987) is the leading Aboriginal-owned publisher and runs an "Indigenous Futurisms" oriented list across YA and adult; Magabala is the primary Aboriginal-press venue for the field. The Australia-specific Norma K. Hemming Award (Australian Science Fiction Foundation, 2010+) for "exploration of themes of race, gender, sexuality, class or disability" has gone to Aboriginal-Australian women writers including Coleman.

### 1.5 Sápmi / Sámi-Arctic

**The Sámi women's SF field is thin and partly miscategorised.** Sámi literature is institutionally Sámi-Council-supported (Sámi Author's Union; Tjállegoahte / Sámi Literature Centre, Jokkmokk) but the mainstream Sámi novel of the past decade is realist or historical-realist rather than speculative.

- **Ann-Helén Laestadius** (Sámi-Tornedalian-Swedish, b. 1971) — the breakthrough adult novel ***Stöld*** (2021, English: *Stolen*, 2023, Bloomsbury) is **Sámi-realist about reindeer-killing crime, not SF**. Her YA novels *SMS från Soppero* and sequels are realist YA. **Probably out of the dataset's strict SF scope** despite her field-shaper status.
- **Linnea Axelsson** (Sámi-Swedish, b. 1980) — ***Ædnan*** (2018, August Prize) is an epic poem about Sámi reindeer-herding families across the 20th century — historical-realist with mythological intertext, not SF.
- **Sigbjørn Skåden** (Sámi, male) — out of scope but the most visible contemporary Sámi-language novelist.
- **Nils-Aslak Valkeapää** (Sámi, male, deceased) — historic foundational figure; out of scope.

**Honest finding**: the Sámi women's-SF stratum is **structurally absent in surfaced sources this session**. Whether this reflects (a) a real absence in the field, (b) Sámi-language publishing genuinely not running speculative-genre titles, or (c) an indexing gap where Sámi-press output (DAT, ČálliidLágádus, Davvi Girji) is not crawled by web infrastructure — cannot be resolved from this recon. Treat as a real gap with the caveat that Sámi-press archives need direct access to verify.

### 1.6 Inuit (Nunangat / Greenland / Alaska / Chukotka)

**The Inuit women's SF field clusters around two events**: Tagaq's *Split Tooth* (2018) and the Qitsualik-Tinsleys' folkloric-speculative work via Inhabit Media.

- **Tanya Tagaq** (Inuk, Cambridge Bay / Iqaluktuuttiaq, Nunavut, b. 1975) — internationally known katajjaq throat singer; ***Split Tooth*** (Penguin Random House Canada, 2018) is the breakthrough literary work. Longlisted Giller, shortlisted Amazon First Novel and Kobo Emerging Writer; **won the 2019 Indigenous Voices Award for Published Prose in English**. The book is officially classed as magical realism by its publisher but more accurately read as **Daniel Heath Justice's "wonderwork"** category — Indigenous literature that defies Western-genre boundaries.

  > "The book has been cited as an example of the genre 'wonderwork' as described by literary academic Daniel Heath Justice, being Indigenous literature that defies conventional Western notions of literary genre."
  >
  > — Wikipedia, *Split Tooth*, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split_Tooth. *Secondary.* The "wonderwork" framing is the defensible Phase-2 categorisation: not strictly genre SF, but speculative-Indigenous in a way that the dataset's tolerance for Le Guin, Tagaq, and Tidbeck precedent should accommodate. The full-page illustrations are by Chicano artist Jaime Hernandez (Love and Rockets) — a documented cross-Indigenous-of-the-Americas / Chicano collaboration worth noting.
- **Rachel A. Qitsualik-Tinsley** (Inuk-Scottish-Cree, b. 1953, northern Baffin Island) — ***Ajjiit: Dark Dreams of the Ancient Arctic*** (Inhabit Media, 2011, with Sean A. Qitsualik-Tinsley) — nine speculative-fiction stories drawing on Inuit shamanism, framed as "dark fantasy for modern audiences." Husband-and-wife co-authorship; the dataset should record her as primary-co-author rather than as part of a male-led duo. Tier-2 candidate.
- **Aviaq Johnston** (Inuk, Pangnirtung / Iqaluit) — *Those Who Run in the Sky* (Inhabit Media, 2017) and *Those Who Dwell Below* (2019) — YA speculative-Inuit-shamanism. **Tier-1 candidate**, especially for the Indigenous-press / Inuit-press SF stratum.
- **Norma Dunning** (Inuk, Edmonton) — *Annie Muktuk and Other Stories* (2017) won the 2018 Howard O'Hagan Award. Speculative threads in some stories. Tier-2.

**Greenlandic Inuit (Kalaallit) and Alaskan Iñupiaq women's SF:** essentially absent in surfaced sources. **Niviaq Korneliussen** (Greenlandic, *HOMO sapienne* / *Crimson*, 2014) is queer-realist, not SF. Yup'ik women's literary fiction (e.g. Velma Wallis, *Two Old Women*, 1993) is oral-tradition-derived realist, not SF in strict genre terms. **Treat the Greenlandic / Alaskan stratum as an honest gap.**

## 2. Author candidates by generation

Confidence tiers: **Well-documented** (multiple Indigenous-press / Indigenous-academic / mainstream-press sources, prize record, established bibliography), **Suspected** (one or two sources surfaced), **Rumored** (mentioned but not yet verified).

### 2.1 Foundational / pre-1990 (proto-Indigenous-Futurisms)

- **Leslie Marmon Silko** (Laguna Pueblo, b. 1948) — *Almanac of the Dead* (1991), *Gardens in the Dunes* (1999), arguably *Ceremony* (1977) at the speculative-mythological edge. **Well-documented**, included in *Walking the Clouds*. Tier-1 add (foundational); the dataset's failure to include her is one of its most visible historical gaps.
- **Anna Lee Walters** (Pawnee/Otoe-Missouria, b. 1946) — *Ghost Singer* (1988), an early speculative novel about Indigenous remains in museum collections. Tier-2; needs source-language verification of her ongoing corpus.
- **Diane Glancy** (Cherokee descent, contested enrollment status; b. 1941) — included in *Walking the Clouds*. Speculative-poetic / experimental. Tier-2 with the enrollment caveat — Glancy's tribal claim has been contested in Cherokee-Nation responses, and any inclusion should flag this in the Roanhorse-parallel manner.
- **Louise Erdrich** (Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa, b. 1954) — *Tracks* (1988) magical-realist; ***Future Home of the Living God*** (2017) is her explicit dystopian-SF novel about reverse evolution and an authoritarian future US. **Tier-1 add** (named in `gaps.md`); *Future Home* is the load-bearing speculative-SF entry but her wider corpus is realist with magical-realist threads.

### 2.2 1990s–2000s: the proto-Indigenous-Futurisms cohort

- **Misha (Misha Nogha)** (Métis-Cree-Nordic, b. 1955) — ***Red Spider, White Web*** (Wordcraft of Oregon, 1990) — **the earliest full-length cyberpunk SF novel by an Indigenous woman**. Won the 1990 ReaderCon Award; finalist for the Arthur C. Clarke Award. Anchored in Dillon's *Walking the Clouds*; John Shirley has stated Misha belongs on any list of seminal cyberpunk writers.

  > "Whereas non-white characters are often assigned supporting roles in conventional cyberpunk, Misha places her Aboriginal-others at the centre of the narrative."
  >
  > — Wikipedia, Misha (writer), en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misha_(writer). *Secondary.* The 1990 publication date is significant: it predates the 2003 Dillon-coinage of "Indigenous Futurisms" and the 2012 *Walking the Clouds* anchor by 22 and 13 years respectively. **Misha is the historical anchor the dataset is missing.** Subsequent work: *Prayers of Steel* (1988 chapbook), various weird-fiction stories collected in *Ke-Qua-Hawk-As* (1994). **Tier-1 add — highest historical value of any single addition this recon recommends.**
- **Eden Robinson** (Haisla / Heiltsuk, Kitamaat, BC, b. 1968) — *Monkey Beach* (2000, magical-realist), the ***Trickster*** trilogy: *Son of a Trickster* (2017), *Trickster Drift* (2018), *Return of the Trickster* (2021). The Trickster trilogy is **explicitly speculative-Indigenous urban-mythology SF**. Adapted as CBC TV series *Trickster* (2020), withdrawn following the showrunner's contested-Indigenous-identity controversy — note this was the showrunner Michelle Latimer's controversy, not Robinson's. **Tier-1 add.**
- **Linda Hogan** (Chickasaw, b. 1947) — *Power* (1998), *Solar Storms* (1995), *Mean Spirit* (1990). Speculative-environmental, Indigenous-ecology framework. Tier-1 candidate.
- **Celu Amberstone** (Cherokee descent) — *Refugees* (2008, Kegedonce Press) is explicit Indigenous-SF; included in *Walking the Clouds*. Tier-2 — verification of tribal-enrollment status pending.

### 2.3 Indigenous-Futurisms wave (2012–2018)

- **Cherie Dimaline** (Métis Nation of Ontario, Georgian Bay Métis Council, b. 1975) — ***The Marrow Thieves*** (DCB / Cormorant 2017, GG Award 2017, Kirkus Prize 2017), ***Empire of Wild*** (2019), ***Hunting by Stars*** (2021, *Marrow Thieves* sequel), ***Funeral Songs for Dying Girls*** (2023). **Tier-1; named in `gaps.md`.**

## 3. Categories of invisibility

### 3.1 Indigenous-language original speculative writing

The thinnest stratum of the field. **No Indigenous-language original-script speculative novels by women surfaced in this session**, with the partial exception of Leanne Betasamosake Simpson's *Noopiming*, which integrates Anishinaabemowin throughout but is primarily English. The Indigenous Voices Awards include an Indigenous-language category — verifying its winners is the most reliable Phase-2 path to surfacing original-language speculative work. Inhabit Media publishes simultaneously bilingual Inuktitut–English titles; Theytus has occasional Indigenous-language imprints; **the K'iche', Quechua, Mapudungun, Ayuujk, and Aymara presses do not appear to publish speculative-genre fiction by women in original language** based on this session's surfacing — but this is most likely an indexing gap, not an absence.

### 3.2 The Roanhorse / Pueblo-positioning question (Phase-2 ethical question, not for the recon to resolve)

The dataset's existing Roanhorse entry should be flagged for review against three Indigenous-press / Indigenous-academic responses:

1. **The 2018 14-Diné-writers letter** (signed by Diné writers including Jennifer Denetdale and others), arguing that *Trail of Lightning* "is an appropriation of Diné cultural beliefs," misrepresents Navajo spiritual content, and was published without sufficient Diné consultation. The letter is documented in the *American Indians in Children's Literature* (AICL) blog post by Debbie Reese (Nambé Pueblo).

  > "[The 2018 letter from 14 Diné writers stated that] *Trail of Lightning* is an appropriation of Diné cultural beliefs … important Navajo spiritual beliefs were portrayed inaccurately and offensively."
  >
  > — paraphrased in en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trail_of_Lightning citing AICL and Indianz; the original letter is on AICL at https://americanindiansinchildrensliterature.blogspot.com/2018/08/concerns-about-roanhorses-trail-of.html. *Primary (Diné-writer collective statement); AICL hosts the letter.*

2. **Ohkay Owingeh Pueblo leadership statement.** Former Lt. Gov. of Ohkay Owingeh **Dr. Matthew Martinez** stated that "by not engaging in any form of cultural and community acknowledgement, Roanhorse has failed to establish any legitimate claim to call herself Ohkay Owingeh" and that "It is unethical for Roanhorse to be claiming Ohkay Owingeh and using this identity to publish Native stories" (Indianz.com, 2020-06-24, "The Elizabeth Warren of the sci-fi set"). This is a tribal-leadership-level statement on enrollment / identity, not merely a literary-critical disagreement.

3. **Dr. Jennifer Denetdale** (Navajo Nation citizen, U New Mexico professor) — has stated that Roanhorse "appropriates [Diné teachings] violently."

The recon's recommendation: **the Phase-2 dataset should retain Roanhorse but record these critiques in-file as a documented community-response context**, in the same way the dataset's Karin Tidbeck entry would record reception complications. The dataset should NOT silently use Roanhorse as the "Indigenous Americas" placeholder; her presence should be supplemented by foundational additions (Misha, Silko, Erdrich) who anchor the field in less-contested ground. **This is a Phase-2 editorial question, not a recon-resolved question.**

### 3.3 Two-Spirit / Indigiqueer SF and the dataset's gender framing

The *Love after the End* (2020) anthology and Joshua Whitehead's wider corpus mark a structural shift in Indigenous SF: **the field's most institutionally-recognised current event is two-spirit / Indigiqueer**, not strictly women's. The dataset's "framed around women, non-binary writers flagged in-file" tolerance (Tidbeck precedent) should extend to this cohort with **explicit pronoun / identity flagging in-file**:

- Joshua Whitehead (he/they, Two-Spirit, Indigiqueer)
- jaye simpson (Two-Spirit, Oji-Cree)
- Kai Minosh Pyle (Two-Spirit, Métis/Anishinaabe)
- Mari Kurisato (Two-Spirit, Cote First Nation Nakoda)
- Smokii Sumac (Two-Spirit, Ktunaxa)
- Arielle Twist (Two-Spirit, Cree)

**The honest framing**: the dataset is built around women writers; extending Tidbeck-precedent tolerance to non-binary / Two-Spirit Indigenous writers is consistent with existing precedent and the alternative — excluding them — would meaningfully under-represent the current institutional shape of Indigenous SF.

### 3.4 Aboriginal Australian women's SF beyond Wright/Kwaymullina/Coleman

The Aboriginal-Australian SF field is dominated by these three women-author cases. **Where does the field continue?** Magabala Books' YA list includes additional Indigenous-Australian women writers (Anita Heiss, Karen Wyld); whether any of them publish SF specifically rather than literary realism with speculative threads is a verification task. **Flag as Phase-2 deeper-pass target** — the regional infrastructure exists; the question is whether the women's-SF cohort extends substantively beyond the visible three.

### 3.5 Greenland / Alaska / Chukotka / Sápmi women's SF

**Structurally absent in surfaced sources.** Greenlandic Inuit / Kalaallit literary infrastructure (Atuagkat, the Greenland Cultural Foundation) exists; whether women's speculative-genre output exists in Kalaallisut is not surfacable. Same for Iñupiaq and Yup'ik in Alaska. Sámi-language speculative output is not surfacable in this session's pass; the Sámi-press infrastructure (DAT, ČálliidLágádus, Davvi Girji) needs direct archival access. **Treat as honest gap with the caveat that print-Indigenous-press archival access in original-language is required to verify whether the absence is real.**

### 3.6 Indigenous-Latin-American women's SF beyond Catrileo and Aguilar Gil

**The single largest invisible mass in this recon.** Quechua, Aymara, K'iche', Yucatec Maya, Náhuatl, Mapudungun: original-language literary infrastructure exists at small-press scale (Pakarina Ediciones; Editorial 3.600; Editorial Maya'Wuj; Editorial Resistencia; the Casa de Escritores Indígenas in Mexico). Speculative-fiction women's work in these languages is **not surfacable in web-indexed sources** in this recon's time-budget. **Treat as a real digital-indexing gap, not as evidence of absence.** A serious Phase-2 pass would require direct-press email outreach and original-language searches in Quechua / Aymara / Mapudungun.

### 3.7 Cordel / oral-performance / chapbook circuits

Indigenous-press fanzine and chapbook output, oral-storytelling-as-performance, and tribal-college-press chapbooks (Tohono O'odham Community College; Diné College Press; Salish Kootenai College Press; Northwest Indian College's Sttósen Press) — **not crawled by web search infrastructure**. The Inuit oral-storytelling tradition's recent transcription-into-print (e.g. *Unikkaaqtuat: An Introduction to Inuit Myths and Legends*, Inhabit Media) is partly visible but the women's-speculative subset is hard to isolate. **Honest gap.**

## 4. What surprised you

**Indigenous-women's SF has been hiding in plain sight under "magical realism" and "literary fiction" labels for at least three decades.** The hypothesis tested in this recon — that the field's apparent thinness is a categorisation artefact — is substantially confirmed. Misha's *Red Spider, White Web* (1990) is a full-length cyberpunk novel by an Indigenous woman published the same year as Pat Cadigan's *Synners* and four years before Melissa Scott's *Trouble and Her Friends* — but it is virtually absent from anglophone-SF canon-formation accounts of cyberpunk. Dillon's 2012 anthology made the retrospective case for it; Reactor (formerly Tor.com) has a 2018 retrospective ("Neither Hero nor Anti-hero: Misha's *Red Spider White Web*") but the field's gatekeeping infrastructure (Locus reviews, *Foundation*, the major SF studies textbooks) systematically did not place Misha in the cyberpunk canon. **This is the cleanest single example in the dataset of what source-language-priority research is meant to surface — except the "source language" here is Indigenous-press / Indigenous-academic English, not a non-anglophone language.**

**Indigenous Futurisms is a self-organised, twenty-plus-year movement with its own scholarly apparatus, and `gaps.md` does not register it as such.** Dillon coined the term in 2003; the 2012 anthology anchored it institutionally; the 2016 *Mitêwâcimowina* anthology Canadianised it; the 2020 *Love after the End* anthology two-spirited it. By 2026 there are at least three substantive critical infrastructures (SAIL, NAIS, NAISA) and one anthology series and three Indigenous-press lists running with Indigenous-Futurisms editorial frames. **The dataset's treatment of Roanhorse as a single placeholder for "Indigenous SF" misses this twenty-year institutional buildup.**

**The Aboriginal-Australian / Inuit / Mapuche cases substantially extend the field's geography**, and none of them are in `gaps.md`. The dataset's existing framing around Roanhorse + Dimaline + Erdrich + Little Badger is **Turtle-Island-North-only**; the Indigenous Pacific, Indigenous Latin America, and Inuit Nunangat are absent. Adding Wright (*The Swan Book*), Kwaymullina (*Tribe* trilogy), Coleman (*Terra Nullius*), Tagaq (*Split Tooth*), Aviaq Johnston (*Those Who Run in the Sky*), and Catrileo (*Chilco*) would extend the dataset's Indigenous coverage from one continent to four within the existing Phase-2 budget.

**The Roanhorse Pueblo-positioning question is structurally similar to two other dataset questions** the recon does not resolve: the Diane Glancy enrollment question, the V. Castro mestizo-Indigenous question. **The pattern is consistent enough to warrant a dataset-wide convention**: per-author files for Indigenous authors should record (a) self-stated tribal affiliation, (b) enrollment status, (c) any documented tribal-leadership / Indigenous-press / Indigenous-academic critique of the affiliation claim. This is parallel to the recon-flagged practice in the Brazilian recon for the Lasaitis/Aragão attribution error — making Indigenous-press / Indigenous-academic responses load-bearing for the dataset's accuracy claims.

**Possible documented influence edges** (subject to primary-source verification before recording):
- **Grace Dillon → Cherie Dimaline / Darcie Little Badger / Roanhorse** (institutional / curatorial influence; *Walking the Clouds* preceded the 2017+ Indigenous-Futurisms wave; Dillon has been cited by all three in author-statements / panels — exact citation needs verification).
- **Misha → Dillon** (Dillon's editorial statement about Misha's centrality to cyberpunk is documented in *Walking the Clouds*' introduction; verifiable).
- **Le Guin → Dillon's framework** (Dillon's Indigenous-Futurisms frame was developed in conscious dialogue with Le Guin's Earthsea / Hainish work — needs interview verification).
- **Daniel Heath Justice "wonderwork" → Tagaq's *Split Tooth* reception** (Justice's term is being used to categorise *Split Tooth* in mainstream Canadian-press reviews — citation chain needs documentation).
- **Inhabit Media institutional influence on Aviaq Johnston, the Qitsualik-Tinsleys** (publisher-as-influence, not strictly an author-to-author edge but a documented field-shaping presence).

## 5. Sourcing-quality notes

**What worked:**
- **Tribal-press sources** (Lipan Apache Tribe website for Little Badger Lodestar) are reliable primary-source venues for tribal-positioning statements and warrant systematic prioritisation over mainstream SF press releases.
- **Indigenous-academic-blog venues** — particularly **American Indians in Children's Literature** (Debbie Reese, Nambé Pueblo) — are load-bearing for Indigenous-critical-response material; Indianz.com is the strongest Indigenous-press venue for political coverage.
- **University of Arizona Press / Theytus Books / Inhabit Media / Magabala Books / Arsenal Pulp Press / Levine Querido / Giramondo Publishing** — Indigenous-anchor publisher pages are consistently strong primary sources for biographical and bibliographic baselines.
- **Words Without Borders** is the most reliable Indigenous-Latin-American translation venue and surfaces speculative-Indigenous work that would otherwise not appear in anglophone search.
- **Indigenous Voices Awards site (indigenousvoicesawards.org)** has a year-by-year winners and finalists archive that is the most reliable single venue for tracking Canadian Indigenous-press speculative output.
- **Wikipedia (en.)** is generally reliable for Aboriginal-Australian and Māori women writers; less reliable for the Indigenous-Latin-American and Sámi cases where original-language Wikipedia (es., sv., fi., se., smj.) holds different content.
- **Goodreads/Wikipedia bibliographic baselines** held up across most contemporary cases.

**What did not work / requires direct access:**
- **Tribal-college press archives** (Diné College Press, Tohono O'odham CC, Salish Kootenai College Press) — not web-crawled at scale; need direct contact.
- **Sámi-press archives** (DAT, ČálliidLágádus, Davvi Girji) — Sámi-language / Norwegian / Swedish content not surfaced in English-language search; would need direct fetches and possibly Sámi-language search.
- **Indigenous-Latin-American small-press archives** (Pakarina Ediciones, Editorial 3.600, Editorial Maya'Wuj, Casa de Escritores Indígenas) — Spanish-language but speculative output is poorly indexed; direct-fetch needed.
- **Greenlandic Atuagkat / Iñupiaq / Yup'ik / Chukchi press output** — essentially absent from English-language web search.
- **WeChat-equivalent for Indigenous social-media community infrastructure** — the Indigenous Futurisms Twitter/Instagram/Bluesky community is real but not crawlable for biographical claims at scale; substack newsletters (Indigenous-Goddess-Gang, Anishinaabemowin Goddamn) require direct-fetch.
- **Studies in American Indian Literatures (SAIL)** — Project Muse / JSTOR paywalled; institutional access needed for the Dillon 2003 coinage article and subsequent Indigenous-Futurisms scholarship.

**Encyclopedia error patterns:** English-language sources frequently conflate distinct Indigenous nations into "Native American" or "First Nations"; tribal-affiliation precision varies widely. Wikipedia's tribal-nation tagging is more reliable than mainstream-press summaries but still inconsistent on enrollment-vs-descent claims. **Always verify tribal-affiliation claims against Indigenous-press / tribal-website primary sources rather than mainstream-anglophone summaries.**

**One direct contradiction with `gaps.md`'s framing worth surfacing:** the gaps file frames Indigenous women's SF as a four-author task (Roanhorse already there, Dimaline + Erdrich + Little Badger missing). **The actual field is at minimum a fifteen-author Phase-2 task** spanning Turtle Island North, Aboriginal Australia, Inuit Nunangat, and Mapuche / Mixe Latin America. The gap is not "three missing authors" but "an entire regional bucket needing definition."
