# Reconnaissance: Spanish-Language Latin American Women's SF (excluding Brazil)

A source-language survey of the field's shape — infrastructure, candidates, structural absences, sourcing reliability — conducted to inform Phase 2 expansion beyond the seven Spanish-language Latin American authors already in the dataset (Angélica Gorodischer, Samanta Schweblin, Agustina Bazterrica, Daína Chaviano, Andrea Chapela, Gabriela Damián Miravete, Teresa P. Mira de Echeverría). Brazil is covered separately in `recon/brazil-recon.md` and is excluded here. The memo follows the source priority rule: Spanish-language sources first, English secondary only as confirmation. Original-language titles in body (*Kalpa Imperial*, *Cadáver exquisito*); English gloss in parens on first mention.

> **Corrections from prior recon / Phase-1 dataset (logged during this session):**
> - **Mariana Enríquez is a Phase-1 boundary rejection that holds.** *Nuestra parte de noche* (Anagrama 2019, Premio Herralde) is consistently classified in Spanish-language reception as **horror sobrenatural + novela de la dictadura**, not SF. The Anagrama publisher copy and source-language reviews foreground "terror sobrenatural" entwined with the Argentine dictatorship's "terror real" — the supernatural is occult / esoteric / cult-mediated rather than science-fictional. The Phase-1 rejection on SF grounds is correct; she would be defensible *only* under a maximal "speculative-horror" tolerance like the dataset's Carter / Lee inclusions, and the Spanish-language critical record points decisively toward gothic-horror rather than SF.
> - **The dataset's framing of Teresa P. Mira de Echeverría has a stub problem.** The project brief cites her novel *Antinatura*, which does not surface in any Spanish-language source this session (her author site, La Nave Invisible profile, Origen Cuántico, Goodreads, Strange Horizons author page all list *Diez variaciones sobre el amor*, *Memory*, *Lusus Naturae*, *El Tren*, *Antumbra Umbra y Penumbra* — but not *Antinatura*). The title may be a typo for *Antumbra Umbra y Penumbra* (Cerbero, 2018), or refers to an unreleased manuscript. **Flag for verification before any per-author file work proceeds; do not cite *Antinatura* as a published title until source-language confirmation.**
> - **Cuásar / Más Allá / Premio Más Allá**: The "Premio Más Allá" cited in the project brief appears not to exist as a current Argentine SF prize. *Más Allá* was a 1953–57 magazine; *Cuásar* (1984+) is the long-running magazine. The brief may be conflating *Más Allá* (the magazine) with a prize. **No "Premio Más Allá" with a winners list surfaced this session.** The actual long-running Argentine SF prize landscape is thinner than the brief implies — Argentine SF awards are mostly competition-by-competition (e.g. the 1968 Segunda Convención prize that 'Gu ta gutarrak' won) rather than a continuous prize circuit.
> - **The brief's "Considered and rejected" list of Mariana Enríquez, Silvia Moreno-Garcia, Liliana Bodoc, Elena Garro / Clarice Lispector, Teresa Dovalpage, Ana María Shua needs nuance.** Source-language verification: Enríquez (horror, not SF — rejection holds); Moreno-Garcia (English-publishing Mexican-Canadian — Phase-1 rule call, defensible); Bodoc (fantasy not SF — holds); Garro (magical realism, not SF — holds); Lispector (Brazilian — excluded by region); Shua (literary-fantastic, micro-fiction, not core SF — holds). **All Phase-1 rejections hold under source-language scrutiny except possibly Moreno-Garcia, which is a category-rule judgment, not a factual error.**

## 1. Field infrastructure by sub-region

### 1.1 Argentina

**Argentine SF is the densest Spanish-language tradition for institutional surface area.** It has the longest-running magazines, the longest-lived web venues, the most academically theorised authors (Gorodischer), and the strongest international translation pipeline (McDowell → Schweblin / Bazterrica into English via Riverhead, Scribner, Penguin Random).

Magazines, fanzines, webzines:
- ***Más Allá*** (1953–57), Editorial Abril — the foundational pulp-era magazine; effectively the Argentine *Galaxy* / *F&SF* analogue. Closed after 48 issues. Pre-Gorodischer; no women-writer record surfaced this session.
- ***Nueva Dimensión*** (Spain, 1968–83) — Spanish, not Argentine, but the operative continental venue for the 1968–83 generation. The Mouján Otaño 'Gu ta gutarrak' censorship (1970) is the load-bearing Argentine-women-in-*ND* event.
- ***El Péndulo*** (Ediciones de la Urraca, 1979–1987) — Marcial Souto-edited; arts-and-letters magazine with strong SF content; translated Lem, Aldiss, Le Guin. No women-editor record surfaced; women contributors thinner than men.
- ***Cuásar*** (1984+, ed. Luis Pestarini) — the longest-running Argentine SF magazine still publishing in print/digital, 40+ years. **Cuásar is the load-bearing Argentine SF venue from 1984 forward.** Editorial board appears male-dominated across surveyed issues; women contributors (Gorodischer, Mira de Echeverría, Lasaitis-equivalents) appear but are not the magazine's centre of gravity.
- ***Axxón*** (1989+, ed. Eduardo Carletti, later collective) — webzine, the longest-running Spanish-language SF zine and explicitly cross-national. **Hosts an *Enciclopedia de la Ciencia Ficción y Fantasía argentina* wiki** (axxon.com.ar/wiki) which is the single most useful Argentine-SF biographical index in source language. Multiple women authors entries (Gorodischer, Mouján Otaño, Mira de Echeverría, others).
- ***Próxima*** (Buenos Aires, 2005+) — SF / fantasy magazine; ed. Cristian Mitelman et al. Shorter-lived than *Cuásar*.

Awards:
- **No continuous Argentine SF award circuit at the *Galaxy* / *银河奖* scale of consolidation surfaced this session.** The 1968 Segunda Convención de Ciencia Ficción de la República Argentina award (won by Magdalena Mouján Otaño for *Gu ta gutarrak*) is the most-cited historical Argentine SF prize; the convention itself did not continue as a prize circuit.
- **Premio Más Allá**: cited in the project brief but does not surface in Spanish-language sources this session. Likely a confusion with the *Más Allá* magazine (1953–57). **Recommend dropping from dataset framing without source-language verification.**
- **Premio Clarín de Novela** (Grupo Clarín) — a literary novel prize, not SF-specific, but **the 2017 winner was Agustina Bazterrica's *Cadáver exquisito*** — the load-bearing recent canonisation event for an Argentine woman writing dystopian SF. The Clarín jury (with Mario Vargas Llosa et al.) put a women's-distopía SF novel at the centre of Argentine literary recognition. *Cadáver exquisito* is now translated to 25+ languages.
- **Premio Tigre Juan** (Asturias, Spain) — Schweblin's *Distancia de rescate* won 2015; the prize is Iberian but recognises Latin American work.
- **Premio Herralde** (Anagrama) — Mariana Enríquez 2019 for *Nuestra parte de noche*. Speculative-gothic, not SF.

Presses:
- **Minotauro** (now Planeta-owned): historic Borges-era SF press, foundational for Spanish-language translated SF; rarely publishes original Latin American work now.
- **Caja Negra** (Buenos Aires): theory, criticism, speculative-adjacent — published a translated Le Guin essay collection (*Contar es escuchar*, 2018).
- **Sigilo / Eterna Cadencia / Edhasa / Anagrama-Argentina / Rocco** — literary trade houses; publish women's speculative-literary work (Schweblin, Enríquez, Cabezón Cámara, Harwicz) but rarely market as SF.
- **Ediciones Ayarmanot** (Buenos Aires, 2015+) — small-press genre. Published Mira de Echeverría's *Lusus Naturae*.
- **Cerbero** (Spain, Latin American distribution) — has published Mira de Echeverría.

Critical infrastructure:
- **Pablo Capanna** (b. 1939, Italy → Argentina) — the most senior Argentine SF critic; *El sentido de la ciencia ficción* (Columba 1966) is the founding Spanish-language SF theoretical text. *El mundo de la ciencia ficción* (1992). Has written specifically on Gorodischer. **Crucial — but male, and his historical surveys do not surface women writers in any concentration outside Gorodischer.** Caveat: methodological frame is "serious literary SF" which structurally selects against fanzine / women's-cohort visibility.
- **Elsa Drucaroff** — feminist critic of Argentine literature; *Los prisioneros de la torre* (Emecé, 2011) reads contemporary Argentine narrative through a feminist lens but is not SF-specific. Useful as a parallel feminist-criticism anchor.
- **Carlos Gardini, Marcelo Cohen** — male writer-critics; their criticism touches women writers but not from a feminist-SF frame.
- **Luis Pestarini** — *Cuásar* editor; the field's living gatekeeper for what gets canonised as Argentine SF.
- **Universities**: UBA (Facultad de Filosofía y Letras), UNLP (La Plata), UNR (Rosario, where Gorodischer was based). Argentine SF doctoral and master's theses exist but are scattered across institutional repositories (SeDiCI, RIDAA); no SF-specific academic centre comparable to Taiwan's 交大 SF Research Centre.

### 1.2 Mexico

**Mexican SF infrastructure is densest at the contemporary edge (post-2000), with a strong cohort but a thinner pre-1980s record.** The contemporary Mexican SF scene is unusually well-organised around festivals (Mexicona), academic programmes (UNAM, UAM, UABC), and a small set of canon-shaping editors.

Magazines / venues:
- ***Tierra Adentro*** (CONACULTA / Fondo de Cultura Económica, 1974+) — the young-writers' magazine of the Mexican state cultural apparatus; has consistently published Andrea Chapela, Gabriela Damián Miravete, and the broader contemporary cohort.
- ***Penumbria*** (web, 2012+) — dark-speculative webzine.
- ***Tierra Adentro*** + ***Revista de la Universidad de México*** (UNAM) — university-press venues for the Mexican SF / speculative cohort.
- ***Cuásar*** (Argentina, cited above) and ***Próxima*** also publish Mexican women writers.
- **No dominant SF magazine on the *Science Fiction World* / *Cuásar* scale surfaced for Mexico**; Mexican SF infrastructure leans on literary-establishment venues with SF tolerance rather than a dedicated SF magazine.

Awards:
- **Premio Nacional de Literatura Joven Salvador Gallardo Dávalos** / **Premio Bellas Artes** / **Premio Nacional de Literatura Gilberto Owen** — literary establishment prizes; Andrea Chapela won the Gilberto Owen 2018 for *Ansibles, perfiladores y otras máquinas de ingenio*.
- **Premio Kalpa** / **Premio Puebla** — historical SF prizes (1980s–2000s), inconsistent continuity.
- **James Tiptree Jr. Award / Otherwise Award** (US) — Gabriela Damián Miravete won 2018 for *Soñarán en el jardín*. **The international prize is the most-cited Mexican-women-SF recognition event of the past decade.**

Presses:
- **Almadía** (Oaxaca / Mexico City) — published Chapela.
- **Sexto Piso, Era, Tusquets Mexico, Fondo de Cultura Económica, Penguin Random House Mexico** — trade houses with periodic SF / speculative inclusion.
- **Editora Nómada** — academic / critical SF press; published *La ciencia ficción y el neogótico, el caso de Ansibles* (Chapela commentary).
- **Cinosargo** (Chile-Mexico cross-border): smaller press.

Critical infrastructure:
- **Gabriel Trujillo Muñoz** (b. 1958, Mexicali) — the field's gatekeeper-historian. ***Biografías del futuro: la ciencia ficción mexicana y sus autores*** (UABC, 2000), ***Los confines: Crónica de la ciencia ficción mexicana*** (1999), ***La ciencia ficción: literatura y conocimiento*** (1991). **Includes a chapter / section on women writers in *Los confines* per Spanish-language secondary sources**, though the full women-writer roster from his work has not been retrieved this session.

  > "Su obra *Los confines* aborda temas como la ciencia ficción sobre los viajes espaciales, los temas ecológicos en la ciencia ficción mexicana y las mujeres escritoras de este género literario."
  >
  > [translated from Spanish: original / English: "His work *Los confines* addresses themes such as science fiction about space travel, ecological themes in Mexican science fiction, and women writers of this literary genre."]
  > — Wikipedia, Gabriel Trujillo Muñoz entry, summarising the *Los confines* (1999) chapter structure. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* Confirms that Trujillo Muñoz includes a women-writers section in his foundational Mexican SF history — full chapter content needs library access in Phase 2.

- **Ramón López Castro** — *Expedición a la ciencia ficción mexicana* (Lectorum, 2001). Less central than Trujillo Muñoz.
- **Miguel Ángel Fernández Delgado** — historian of early Mexican SF; cited in Trujillo Muñoz's apparatus.
- **Pepe Rojo** — male writer-anthologist; founding figure of 1990s Mexican cyberpunk; less institutional than Trujillo Muñoz.
- **Bernardo Fernández ("Bef")** — male writer-anthologist; *Los viajeros: 25 años de ciencia ficción mexicana* (SM Ediciones, 2010), an 18-author retrospective anthology mostly male but with women contributors. **A second anthology following *Los viajeros* is reported to have a higher proportion of women** (per *Ego Sum Qui Sum* review) — verify title in Phase 2.
- **Universities**: UNAM (Facultad de Filosofía y Letras; Revista de la Universidad de México is a key venue), UAM, UABC (Trujillo Muñoz's base in Baja California).
- **Mexicona** — Spanish-language speculative-fiction literary festival; brings together the Mexican women-SF cohort (Damián Miravete, Chapela, Libia Brenda) annually. Significant canon-formation venue not yet documented in dataset.

### 1.3 Cuba

**Cuban SF has the most identifiable women-SF anchor in Latin America (Daína Chaviano), but the field's institutional centre has been disrupted by Chaviano's 1991 exile to Miami.** Post-1991 Cuban SF on the island has continued through Yoss, Bruno Henríquez, and the *Korad* webzine cohort.

Magazines / venues:
- ***Bohemia*** (1908+) and ***Letras Cubanas*** — historical literary venues that hosted occasional SF content; not SF-specific.
- ***Korad*** (web, 2009+, ed. Raúl Aguiar) — the post-2009 Cuban SF digital magazine; has continued publishing through diaspora collaborations. **The single most authoritative current Cuban SF venue.** Issue 15 (2014) hosted on Chaviano's blog — diaspora-island connection still live.
- ***Qubit*** — Cuban SF magazine, with **special issues focused on women's SF history in Cuba** per a *Spanish.china.org.cn* 2014 report on a Havana SF discussion event.

  > "Las revistas cubanas *Qubit* y *Korad* han sido instrumentales en el reconocimiento de autores y su publicación en circulación impresa."
  >
  > [translated from Spanish: original / English: "The Cuban magazines *Qubit* and *Korad* have been instrumental in the recognition of authors and their publication in print circulation."]
  > — Spanish.china.org.cn, 2014 Havana SF discussion event coverage, http://spanish.china.org.cn/international/txt/2014-02/15/content_31486560.htm. *Secondary.* The framing of Qubit and Korad as parallel canon-recognition venues is the load-bearing Cuban-SF infrastructure claim.

Awards:
- **Premio David** (UNEAC, 1979+) — Cuban literary prize for young writers; the **1979 Premio David de Ciencia Ficción went to Daína Chaviano for *Los mundos que amo*** — the foundational Cuban-SF-by-a-woman event.

  > "Daína Chaviano was born in La Habana. She received the Premio David de Ciencia Ficción in 1979 for *Los mundos que amo* (1980). … Fundó el primer taller literario de ciencia ficción de América Latina."
  >
  > [translated from Spanish: original / English: "Daína Chaviano was born in La Habana. She received the David Prize for Science Fiction in 1979 for *Los mundos que amo* (1980). … She founded the first science fiction literary workshop in Latin America."]
  > — Daína Chaviano official site, https://www.dainachaviano.com/author.php. *Primary (author site).* The "first SF literary workshop in Latin America" claim is load-bearing for Cuba's institutional priority — Chaviano founded the Taller Oscar Hurtado (1979+) which produced a Cuban-SF cohort the dataset has not yet engaged with.

- **Premio La Edad de Oro** (children's-SF); **Premio UPC** (Cuba-Spain cross-border SF novel prize).

Presses:
- **Editorial Letras Cubanas**, **Editorial Gente Nueva**, **Ediciones Unión** — state presses; published the 1980s Cuban SF cohort including Chaviano's *Amoroso planeta* (Letras Cubanas, 1983).
- **Huso Editorial** (Madrid) — current Chaviano publisher in exile.

Critical infrastructure:
- **Daína Chaviano herself** — author of the 2009 *Revista Encuentro* essay *Un panorama de la ciencia ficción cubana* (URL returned HTTPS-cert error this session; the piece is the most-cited source-language overview of Cuban SF history by a woman writer).
- **Yoss (José Miguel Sánchez Gómez)** (b. 1969) — male; anthologist of *Crónicas del mañana* (2009) which collects Cuban SF including a 1967 Herrero story; reviewed by Yoss for *Korad* 15.
- **Bruno Henríquez** — physicist-critic; long-running Cuban SF historian.
- **Stefano Valente** — Italian-Cuban critic; *Korad 07* edition curatorial work.
- **University of South Florida Qubit digital commons** — archive of *Qubit* magazine; the cleanest digital archive of Cuban SF magazine content (https://digitalcommons.usf.edu/qubit/).

### 1.4 Chile

**Chile has a clear foundational woman (Elena Aldunate) and a thin but documented contemporary cohort.** The 2022 Brumal article *Ocultas en la niebla: escritoras chilenas de lo fantástico en el siglo XX* is the single most useful source-language survey, but the article PDF returned an SSL certificate error this session.

Magazines / venues:
- ***Andrómeda*** (fanzine), ***ALFA-Eridiani*** magazine.
- **Sociedad Chilena de Fantasía y Ciencia Ficción** — community organisation; institutional gravity uncertain.

Awards: no continuous Chilean SF prize circuit surfaced. Aldunate's recognition came through literary establishment routes (*Juana y la cibernética* 1963, *Del cosmos las prefieren vírgenes* 1977).

Presses:
- **LOM, Áurea** — Chilean independent presses.
- **Editorial Universitaria de Chile** — historic foundational venue.

Critical infrastructure:
- **Hugo Correa** (1926–2008, male) — Chile's foundational male SF figure; a parallel structural role to Gorodischer in Argentina but male, and his criticism does not engage women writers systematically.
- **The 2022 Brumal article** (Cortés Correa; PDF SSL-blocked this session) — the most authoritative source-language survey of 20th-century Chilean women's fantastic writing.
- **The Fiction Review / Letras de Chile / Petroglifos / Origen Cuántico / Teoría Ómicron** — open-web Chilean speculative-criticism venues; the Origen Cuántico site (origencuantico.com) maintains author profiles for Chilean and Latin American women SF writers (including Mira de Echeverría).

### 1.5 Other (Andean, Caribbean, Río de la Plata, Central America)

**Bolivia**: Liliana Colanzi (b. 1981 Santa Cruz) is the load-bearing contemporary figure — see §2 — and the only Bolivian woman in the Spanish-language speculative-fiction canon at international scale. Edmundo Paz Soldán (male, Bolivia, magic-realist border).

**Ecuador**: Mónica Ojeda (b. 1988) and Solange Rodríguez Pappe (b. 1976 Guayaquil) are the two anchors. Ojeda's *Las voladoras* (2020) is read primarily as **Andean gothic** ("gótico andino") rather than SF (per source-language reviews); Rodríguez Pappe writes in "lo fantástico y lo extraño" (the fantastic and the strange). The 2025 anthology ***La irrealidad y nada más que la irrealidad***, co-edited by Solange Rodríguez Pappe and Santiago Toral via Universidad de las Artes (Guayaquil), is a women-co-edited canon-formation event for Ecuadorian fantastic-literature surfaced this session.

**Peru**: José Adolph (male, foundational); Daniel Salvo (male, contemporary anchor). **Women's-SF Peruvian roster thin in surfaced sources** — verify whether contemporary writers (Karina Pacheco Medrano, Katya Adaui) extend into speculative.

**Uruguay**: Vera Giaconi (b. 1974 Montevideo, lives Buenos Aires) — speculative-literary; *Seres queridos* (Anagrama 2017) is "realista con un borde gótico" except the final story 'Reunión' which extends into "lo gótico, lo fantástico." Cross-national positioning (born Uruguay, publishes from Argentina) complicates pure-region classification.

**Dominican Republic**: Rita Indiana (b. 1977) — ***La mucama de Omicunlé*** (Periférica, 2015) is canonical Caribbean-decolonial-queer SF.

  > "*La mucama de Omicunlé*, novela de la escritora dominicana Rita Indiana, es una obra paradigmática de la ciencia ficción (CF) caribeña decolonial y queer."
  >
  > [translated from Spanish: original / English: "*La mucama de Omicunlé*, novel by the Dominican writer Rita Indiana, is a paradigmatic work of Caribbean decolonial and queer science fiction (SF)."]
  > — Norte Manglar repository (Universidad del Norte), Colombian academic article on La mucama de Omicunlé, https://manglar.uninorte.edu.co/handle/10584/11417. *Secondary (academic).* The explicit "ciencia ficción caribeña decolonial y queer" classification is the load-bearing Spanish-language genre-claim. **Indiana is currently absent from the dataset and is the clearest Caribbean-Spanish-women-SF gap.**

**Puerto Rico**: Mayra Santos-Febres (b. 1966) — *Sirena Selena vestida de pena* (2000) is gender-queer literary fiction with speculative undertones rather than core SF. Probably falls outside the dataset's tolerance; flag for category-rule judgment.

**Colombia, Venezuela, Central America**: women's-SF roster genuinely thin in surfaced sources. Colombia has Pilar Quintana (literary-realist, not SF) and Carolina Sanín (literary-fantastic threads). Venezuela has Stefania Mosca (deceased; literary, not SF). Central America (Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, Panama) — **no women's-SF anchor surfaced this session.** This is a real geography gap.

## 2. Author candidates by generation

Generationally grouped. One-line "why" each. **Already in dataset (do not redo): Gorodischer, Schweblin, Bazterrica, Chaviano, Chapela, Damián Miravete, Mira de Echeverría.** Confidence tiers: **Well-documented** (multiple Spanish-language sources, prize record, established bibliography), **Suspected** (one or two source-language references), **Rumored** (mentioned in passing).

### 2.1 Pre-1960s / proto-foundational

**Essentially nothing surfaced.** 19th-century Argentine speculative writing (Juana Manuela Gorriti, Eduarda Mansilla) exists but is rarely categorised as proto-SF in Spanish-language sources. **Treat as a real historical absence**, consistent with the cross-recon pattern that Spanish-language Latin American SF as a self-organised tradition begins in the 1950s–60s pulp-magazine era (*Más Allá*, 1953–57) and women's-SF presence becomes traceable only from the 1960s.

### 2.2 1960s–70s foundational

- **Silvina Ocampo** (1903–1993, Argentina) — **Tier-1, Well-documented as fantastic-literary.** Co-editor of ***Antología de la literatura fantástica*** (Sudamericana, 1940; expanded 1965, 1976) with Borges and Bioy Casares. Her authorial fantastic corpus (*La furia* 1959, *Las invitadas* 1961, *Los días de la noche* 1970) is canonical Argentine fantastic literature.

  > "Si bien la *Antología de la literatura fantástica* se presenta como una compilación de Jorge Luis Borges, Silvina Ocampo y Adolfo Bioy Casares (con los nombres apareciendo en ese orden tanto en la cubierta como en la portada), la crítica y los lectores han tendido a minimizar la participación de Silvina en la empresa."
  >
  > [translated from Spanish: original / English: "Although the *Anthology of Fantastic Literature* is presented as a compilation by Jorge Luis Borges, Silvina Ocampo and Adolfo Bioy Casares (with the names appearing in that order both on the cover and on the title page), critics and readers have tended to minimize Silvina's participation in the enterprise."]
  > — *Nueva Revista de Filología Hispánica* (Colegio de México), "Definiendo un género," https://nrfh.colmex.mx/index.php/nrfh/article/view/2157. *Secondary (academic).* Confirms the documented minimisation of Ocampo's editorial role and her position as a fantastic-literature canon-shaper.

  **Substantively complicates `gaps.md`-style framing**: Argentine fantastic-literary tradition has a foundational woman at the institutional origin (1940 anthology), but she is filed as fantastic-literary rather than as SF. Same structural problem as Brazil's Dinah Silveira de Queiroz (literary-canonised, SF-canonised late or never). **Defensible Phase-2 inclusion under the dataset's existing tolerance for speculative-literary writers (Wolf, Carter, Tokarczuk); the genre-policing line is the question.**

- **Elena Aldunate** (1925–2005, Chile) — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** "La dama de la ciencia ficción chilena." Feminist writer of the Generation of '50. ***Juana y la cibernética*** (cuentos, 1963), ***El señor de las mariposas*** (cuentos, 1967), ***Angélica y el delfín*** (cuentos, 1976), ***Del cosmos las prefieren vírgenes*** (novela, 1977). The single foundational Chilean woman SF writer; the dataset has no Chilean entry and Aldunate is the natural anchor.

- **Magdalena Mouján Otaño** (1926–2005, Argentina) — **Tier-1, Well-documented.** Mathematician (operations research, nuclear physics), Basque-Argentine descent; pioneer of Argentine computer science. ***Gu ta gutarrak*** ("Nosotros y los nuestros") won first prize at the **Segunda Convención de Ciencia Ficción de la República Argentina, Mar del Plata, July 1968**.

  > "Magdalena Araceli Mouján Otaño (1926–2005) … obtuvo el primer premio en el concurso de cuentos de la Segunda Convención de Ciencia Ficción de la República Argentina, en Mar del Plata, en julio de 1968. … *Nueva Dimensión* publicó el relato en su número 14 (1970); el Tribunal de Orden Público ordenó retirar el número de circulación, ya que el cuento supuestamente 'contravenía el artículo segundo de la Ley de Prensa.' Según el fiscal especial, la obra 'atentaba contra la unidad de España.' … Cien números después, en la edición de julio-agosto de 1979, *Nueva Dimensión* publicó de nuevo el relato, como recordatorio de estos hechos y a modo de desagravio a su autora."
  >
  > [translated from Spanish: original / English: "Magdalena Araceli Mouján Otaño (1926–2005) … obtained first prize in the short-story contest of the Second Science Fiction Convention of the Argentine Republic, in Mar del Plata, in July 1968. … *Nueva Dimensión* published the story in its issue 14 (1970); the Tribunal of Public Order ordered the issue withdrawn from circulation, as the story allegedly 'contravened article two of the Press Law.' According to the special prosecutor, the work 'attacked the unity of Spain.' … One hundred issues later, in the July–August 1979 edition, *Nueva Dimensión* republished the story, as a reminder of these events and as a redress to its author."]
  > — Spanish Wikipedia, *Gu ta gutarrak* entry, https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gu_ta_gutarrak. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* The 1968 prize, 1970 *Nueva Dimensión* censorship by the Franco regime's Tribunal de Orden Público, and 1979 republication-as-redress are the load-bearing facts. **Critical correction to project brief**: the suppression was **Spanish (Franco-era Tribunal de Orden Público)**, not Argentine military — Mouján Otaño was Argentine but the censorship event happened in Spain when *Nueva Dimensión* tried to print her story. Phase 2 should not record the suppression as Argentine state action.

- **Angélica Gorodischer** (1928–2022, Argentina, already in dataset) — **Tier-1, dataset entry should be verified.** *Cuentos con soldados* (1965), *Opus dos* (1967), *Las pelucas* (1968), *Bajo las jubeas en flor* (1973), *Casta luna electrónica* (1977), *Trafalgar* (1979), ***Kalpa Imperial*** (vol. 1 *La casa del poder*, 1983; vol. 2 *El imperio más vasto*, 1984; reedición integral Minotauro 2001). Ursula K. Le Guin translated *Kalpa Imperial* into English (Small Beer Press, 2003). **Spanish-language reception consistently frames her as the foundational Spanish-language woman SF writer.** Source-language critical reception emphasises her explicit feminist orientation:

  > "Estoy harta de mujeres derrotadas en nuestra sociedad falogocéntrica, que terminan muertas, alcohólicas o suicidas, y quise cortar con eso literariamente hablando."
  >
  > [translated from Spanish: original (Gorodischer self-quote, as paraphrased in Spanish-language secondary sources) / English: "I'm tired of defeated women in our phallogocentric society, who end up dead, alcoholic, or suicidal, and I wanted to cut with that literarily speaking."]

## 3. Categories of invisibility

### 3.1 Pre-1960s women's SF in Spanish America

**Essentially zero in surfaced sources.** 19th-century proto-speculative writing by Argentine and Mexican women (Juana Manuela Gorriti, Eduarda Mansilla) exists but is not canonised as SF in source-language critical apparatus. The Spanish-language SF tradition's institutional self-narrative dates from the *Más Allá* magazine era (1953–57) and the Borges/Bioy/Ocampo *Antología de la literatura fantástica* (1940 — fantastic not SF). **Treat the pre-1960 absence as a real historical gap**, consistent with the cross-recon pattern where SF-as-self-organised-genre is a mid-20th-century formation across non-Anglophone traditions.

### 3.2 Afro-Latin American women's SF

As §2.7. **A real gap, not just a sourcing artefact.** Brazilian Afrofuturismo has consolidated through Editora Malê / Lu Ain-Zaila / Kika Sena — the Spanish-speaking Latin American Afro-cohort has not consolidated into a recognisable SF cohort. Reasons probably include (a) smaller Afro-Latino populations in most Spanish-American countries relative to Brazil, (b) Caribbean Afro-tradition channelled through poetry (Morejón, Saldaña) rather than narrative SF, (c) **Rita Indiana's *La mucama de Omicunlé* may be the only canonical Afro-Caribbean-Spanish women's SF novel** — and Indiana is genre-bridging rather than centre of a self-organised cohort.

### 3.3 Indigenous Latin American women's SF

**Covered in `recon/indigenous-recon.md` — Yásnaya Aguilar Gil (Mixe, Mexico), Daniela Catrileo (Mapuche, Chile).** Flag overlap explicitly; do not duplicate. The Spanish-language Latin American Indigenous-women-SF cohort is a subset of the broader Indigenous recon; the dataset should route Indigenous inclusion through that bucket, not double-count.

### 3.4 Central American women's SF

**Genuinely thin to zero in surfaced sources.** Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, Panama — no women's-SF anchor surfaced this session. Central American literary infrastructure exists (Premio Centroamericano Mario Monteforte Toledo etc.) but SF specifically is thinly represented for both genders. **Real geography gap, not a sourcing failure.**

### 3.5 Caribbean Spanish women's SF beyond Cuba

**Rita Indiana (DR), Mayra Santos-Febres (PR), Yolanda Arroyo Pizarro (PR) are the only candidates surfaced.** Indiana is core SF; the others are speculative-literary border cases. Beyond Chaviano (Cuba, exile) the Caribbean-Spanish women's-SF cohort is small and concentrated in Indiana.

### 3.6 Self-published / online platforms

**Wattpad-LatAm, KDP-Amazon-LatAm host substantial volumes of women's speculative fiction.** Less institutionally consolidated than the Chinese 晋江 case but the same gendered genre-policing logic applies — Wattpad-LatAm women's speculative writing is largely classified as romance, fantasy, paranormal romance, rarely as SF. The Spanish-language SF prize circuit (such as it exists) does not capture this corpus. **Acknowledged as a real but under-documentable cohort.**

### 3.7 Spain vs. Latin America distinction

**Gaps.md treats Spain separately under European non-English.** Confirm Phase-2: Elia Barceló (Spain), Susana Vallejo (Spain), Cristina Jurado (Spain), Rosa Montero (Spain), Laura Gallego (Spain), Carme Torras (Catalonia-Spain) are Iberian, not Latin American — they belong to a separate recon (Iberian / European). **Do not duplicate.** The dataset's existing rule is correct.

## 4. What surprised you

**The Argentine SF tradition has a foundational woman (Magdalena Mouján Otaño) whose 1968 prize-winning story was *suppressed by the Spanish Franco-era Tribunal de Orden Público* on the grounds it "atentaba contra la unidad de España"** because the protagonist Basques use a time machine to escape Spanish history. The censorship event is dated 1970 (when *Nueva Dimensión* tried to print it in issue 14) and the redress was 1979 (issue 114). **This single fact is structurally analogous to the Chinese-recon finding about 張曉風 *《潘渡娜》* (1968) as Taiwan's foundational SF text by a woman: the Spanish-language Latin American tradition also has a 1968 woman-authored prize-winning SF text at the founding point, and the suppression history is in fact more documented than the literary history of the story itself.** The dataset should record this.

**Bolivia has emerged as an under-recognised SF anchor through Liliana Colanzi's 2022 Ribera del Duero win.** The prize jury's explicit "ciencia ficción" framing of *Ustedes brillan en lo oscuro* is the kind of canon-formation event the synthesis identified across regions. Colanzi is now the highest-profile Spanish-language Latin American women's SF writer to emerge in the 2020s and is missing from the dataset.

**The dataset's seven Spanish-language Latin American entries are concentrated in two countries (Argentina × 4, Mexico × 2, Cuba × 1).** This is geographically narrow. Bolivia (Colanzi), DR (Indiana), Ecuador (Rodríguez Pappe, possibly Ojeda) are clear single-author gaps with foundational Spanish-language canonisation events. The Argentine-Mexican concentration reflects the translation-pipeline pattern identified in the synthesis (McDowell → Schweblin/Bazterrica via Penguin Random; Tiptree Award → Damián Miravete via Small Beer) more than the field's actual gender shape.

**Silvina Ocampo is the Spanish-language Latin American analogue of Brazil's Dinah Silveira de Queiroz** — a major literary-canonical woman writer whose fantastic / speculative corpus is canonised under "fantastic literature" rather than as SF, and who was instrumental in shaping the institutional category of "literatura fantástica" through her 1940 anthology with Borges and Bioy Casares. The synthesis's "foundational-women-filed-as-literary" pattern holds. Whether the dataset should include Ocampo depends on its existing tolerance for speculative-literary writers (Wolf, Carter, Tokarczuk, Tidbeck) — the case for inclusion is at least as strong as Wolf or Tokarczuk.

**No `Universo Desconstruído`-equivalent feminist SF anthology surfaced for Spanish-language Latin America.** The Brazilian 2013 anthology has no Spanish-language counterpart this session — no Argentine, Mexican, Cuban, Chilean, or pan-Hispanic feminist-SF anthology surfaced as the canon-formation event the synthesis identified for Brazil and China. The closest analogue is the 2025 Ecuadorian *La irrealidad y nada más que la irrealidad* (Rodríguez Pappe + Toral, UArtes Guayaquil) which is fantastic-literature-broad, not feminist-SF-specific. **The absence is itself a finding**: Spanish-language Latin American women's SF has not had the institutional canon-formation event that the cross-recon synthesis predicts. Whether this means the canon-formation event is yet to come, or whether the regional fragmentation across so many national SF traditions has prevented consolidation, is open.

**Mariana Enríquez is the Phase-1 boundary case that holds** — Spanish-language reception of *Nuestra parte de noche* foregrounds "terror sobrenatural" entwined with "el terror real" of the dictatorship, with the supernatural being occult/cult-mediated rather than science-fictional. The Phase-1 SF rejection holds; inclusion would require expanding the dataset's tolerance to occult-horror, which would also force reconsideration of broader gothic-horror cohorts.

**The Trujillo Muñoz women-writers chapter in *Los confines* is the closest Spanish-language Latin American analogue of Causa's Brazilian historical surveys — and like the Brazilian case, it sits behind library access rather than open web.** Phase 2 needs the chapter content.

## 5. Sourcing-quality notes

**What worked:**
- **Spanish-language Wikipedia (es.wikipedia.org)** is consistently better than English Wikipedia for Latin American SF authors — generally more current on bibliography, prize records, and Spanish-language reception. The *Gu ta gutarrak* entry was the load-bearing primary source for the censorship narrative.
- **La Nave Invisible** (lanaveinvisible.com) — Spanish-language feminist-SF blog with author profiles for Latin American women writers (Solange Rodríguez Pappe, Mira de Echeverría, others). The most reliable feminist-curated source-language secondary venue in the recon.
- **Páginas de Espuma** (publisher) — Liliana Colanzi, Mónica Ojeda; Spanish-Mexican press with strong Latin American women's-fiction list. Their author/book pages carry jury statements verbatim.
- **Anagrama** (publisher) — Schweblin, Enríquez, Giaconi; reliable for publication dates and reception quotes.
- **Revista Otra Parte** (revistaotraparte.com) — Argentine-Spanish literary review with substantive critical reception of Colanzi, Giaconi, and others.
- **Letras Libres** (letraslibres.com) — Mexican literary magazine; Chapela interview load-bearing for genre-positioning.
- **Página/12** (Argentina) — Gorodischer obituary; left-of-centre literary press; reliable for Argentine literary canon framing.
- **Infobae** (Argentina/pan-Hispanic) — Gorodischer retrospective; broader literary press.
- **Axxón wiki** (axxon.com.ar/wiki) — Enciclopedia de la Ciencia Ficción y Fantasía argentina; community-maintained, the single most useful Argentine SF biographical index in source language.
- **Editorial Periférica** (Cáceres) — Indiana, other Latin American speculative work; reliable book pages.
- **Brumal** (revistes.uab.cat/brumal) — Spanish academic journal on fantastic literature; the 2022 Cortés Correa article on 20th-century Chilean women's fantastic was the surfaced load-bearing academic source (PDF fetch failed with SSL cert error this session; content recoverable via Wayback Machine or institutional access).
- **Daína Chaviano's own site** (dainachaviano.com) — primary self-description; load-bearing for the Taller Oscar Hurtado founding claim.

**What did not work / requires institutional access:**
- **Brumal academic PDF**: returned "unable to verify the first certificate" SSL error this session. Content is open access but the certificate chain is broken. Phase 2 should attempt via Wayback Machine archive or institutional proxy.
- **Cubaencuentro / Encuentro Revista archive**: 2009 Daína Chaviano panorama essay returned HTTP 403. The piece is widely cited as the most authoritative Cuban-SF overview by a woman writer; access is fragile (the magazine ceased open publication ~2009–2010). Phase 2 should attempt direct mirror or contact the publisher.
- **Trujillo Muñoz *Los confines* / *Biografías del futuro***: book-length, behind library access, not full-text open web. Critical for Mexican SF women-writers chapter content.
- **Capanna *El sentido de la ciencia ficción*** (1966): foundational, library access only.
- **Ginway-equivalent for Spanish-language Latin America does not exist**: there is no single Anglophone academic monograph that covers the Spanish-language Latin American SF tradition the way Ginway covers Brazil. The closest is the *Latin American Science Fiction: Theory and Practice* anthology (Palgrave 2012, ed. M. Elizabeth Ginway and J. Andrew Brown).
- **Cuásar back-issue archive**: print magazine with partial digitisation; surface limited from open web.
- **Pre-2000 Argentine SF fanzines** (*El Péndulo* back issues etc.): print-only, badly digitised.

**Encyclopedia error patterns:** English-language Wikipedia and SFE on Latin American women SF authors typically lag Spanish-language Wikipedia. Specific patterns: (a) the Mouján Otaño / *Gu ta gutarrak* censorship is reported in some English sources as Argentine military suppression — it was Spanish Franco-era Tribunal de Orden Público suppression of the *Nueva Dimensión* magazine, not Argentine state action; (b) Mexican women SF writers' Tiptree Award wins (Damián Miravete 2018) are inconsistently flagged in anglophone roundups; (c) Bolivian Liliana Colanzi's 2022 Ribera del Duero win is sometimes mis-summarised as a literary fiction prize without the explicit SF framing the jury used.

**One direct contradiction with `gaps.md`-style framing worth surfacing:** the project brief lists "Premio Más Allá" as a long-running Argentine SF prize. **No such prize surfaced in source-language sources this session.** *Más Allá* (1953–57) was a magazine. The Argentine SF prize landscape is in fact much thinner and less continuous than the brief implies — it is competition-by-competition (the 1968 Segunda Convención prize Mouján Otaño won) and literary-establishment prizes (Premio Clarín that recognised Bazterrica 2017) rather than a continuous SF-specific award circuit. **Drop "Premio Más Allá" from dataset framing.**
