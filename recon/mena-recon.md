# Reconnaissance: MENA Women's SF (Arabic, Hebrew, Persian, Turkish source-language sources)

A source-language survey of the field's shape — infrastructure, candidates, structural absences, sourcing reliability — conducted to inform Phase 2 expansion beyond the four MENA authors already in the dataset (Basma Abdel Aziz, Sophia Al-Maria, Larissa Sansour, Gail Hareven). The memo follows the source priority rule: original-language sources (العربية / עברית / فارسی / Türkçe) first, English secondary only as confirmation. Names given in original script with transliteration and English gloss in parentheses on first mention. Unlike the China and Brazil recons, MENA is not a single tradition: §1 is split across **four largely separable linguistic-institutional fields** (Arabic, Hebrew, Persian, Turkish) which share the geography of "MENA" but have almost no field-level cross-traffic. Where one is essentially empty for women's SF, the recon says so honestly rather than padding.

The *הסולם של יעקב* (*The Confessions of Noa Weber*, Sapir Prize 2002) speculative threads are widely read as literary-postmodern, not SF. **Flag — entry may need to clarify that Hareven is a literary novelist whose speculative threads are critically read, not a genre-SF figure.**

## 1. Field infrastructure by linguistic tradition

### 1.1 Arabic (Egypt + Levant + Maghreb + Gulf)

The Arabic-language SF field is **young, male-dominated, and Egypt-centred** — the founding canon (Mustafa Mahmoud, Nihad Sharif, Ra'uf Wasfi, Sabri Moussa, Ahmed Khaled Tawfik) is uniformly male, and the Wikipedia-AR overview entries name only one woman as a "specialist" of the genre (طيبة الإبراهيمي / Tayba al-Ibrahimi, Kuwait — surfaced via the *majalla* survey but not pursued further this session). The post-2010 dystopia wave is the first generation in which women novelists become institutionally visible.

**Magazines, venues, online infrastructure.**
- The single most important institutional anchor is the **الجمعية المصرية لأدب الخيال العلمي / Egyptian Society for Science Fiction (ESSF)**, founded by **Hosam A. Ibrahim Elzembely (حسام إبراهيم الزمبيلي)** in 2012, in Nasr City, Cairo, after the January 2011 revolution. The Society publishes the **شمس الغد (*Shams al-Ghad* / Sun of Tomorrow)** anthology series — multi-volume, with women contributors appearing from at least volume 4 onward (الـمنتصرون / *Al-Muntasirun* / *The Victors*).
- ESSF is also the publisher of *Arab and Muslim Science Fiction: Critical Essays* (2022, McFarland, ed. Elzembely & Emad El-Din Aysha) — note that despite English publication, the contributors are largely Arab critics with Arabic-source bibliographies. This volume is the closest thing the field has to a self-organised critical anchor.
- The print magazine ecology is **thin**: there is no Arabic-language equivalent to *Science Fiction World* or *Asimov's*. *Al-Hilal* (الهلال, est. 1892) and *Akhbar al-Adab* (أخبار الأدب) are the main literary venues that have given SF coverage; *Mada Masr* (مدى مصر) since 2013 is the dominant English/Arabic bilingual cultural venue covering the post-Arab-Spring dystopia wave (it published critical pieces on Basma Abdel Aziz and Mohammad Rabie in the wake of the Sawiris and Naguib Mahfouz prizes). *Banipal* (in English, but with Arabic-original provenance) and the Kuwaiti monthly *Al-Arabi* (العربي) carry occasional SF coverage but are not specialist venues.
- **Online community infrastructure** for Arabic-language SF is largely Facebook-based — ESSF's Facebook page is the dominant single venue — with no equivalent of Bli Panika (Israel) or Bilim Kurgu Kulübü (Turkey) as a continuous open-web forum. This is a real structural difference from the Hebrew and Turkish fields.

**Awards.**
- There is **no Arabic-language equivalent of the Geffen Award**. The pan-Arab Sheikh Zayed Book Award (جائزة الشيخ زايد للكتاب) and the International Prize for Arabic Fiction (IPAF / "Arabic Booker") have given prizes to dystopia-inflected literary fiction (Mansoura Ez-Eldin's *جبل الزمرد* won the 2014 Sharjah International Book Fair Best Arabic Novel award) but have no SF-specific category.
- The **جائزة اتصالات لكتاب الطفل (Etisalat Award for Arabic Children's Literature)**, run from the UAE, has functioned as the closest thing to a children's/YA-SF prize: **Noura Al Noaman / نورة النومان**'s *أجوان* won "Best Young Adult Book" in 2013, recognising it as the first Emirati / Gulf women's SF novel.
- The **جائزة الشيخ يوسف بن عيسى للكتاب** (Sheikh Yusuf bin Issa Prize) and the **ساويرس** (Sawiris) literary prize have given recognition to dystopia-inflected literary novels (Bothayna Al-Essa, Nora Naguy) but, again, no SF category.

**Anthologies in source-language.** *Iraq + 100* (2016) and *Palestine + 100* (2019) are anthologies organised by Comma Press in English with Arabic-original components — they include women contributors (Anoud, Lana Bastasic, Rasha Abbas, etc.) and are the most internationally visible Arab SF anthologies, but their organisational base is in Manchester, not the Arabic field. The ESSF *Shams al-Ghad* series is the genuinely Arabic-organised anthology infrastructure; **the women's contribution is partial and arrived late** (per the Vector/BSFA roundtable, women contributors became visible only from volume 4 onwards).

**Presses.** Dar al-Shorouq (Cairo), Dar al-Saqi (Beirut), Dar al-Adab (Beirut), Dar al-Tanwir (Beirut, the publisher of Mansoura Ez-Eldin's *جبل الزمرد*), Dar al-Arabiyya lil-‘Ulum (Beirut, *حارس سطح العالم*), and the ESSF's own publication channel are the main outlets. **The dystopia wave clusters at Dar al-Tanwir, Dar al-Arabiyya lil-‘Ulum, and Diwan/Dar al-Shorouq.**

**Academic / critical infrastructure.** The English-language secondary literature anchor is **Ian Campbell** (*Arabic Science Fiction*, Palgrave 2018) and **Yasmine Khayyat**. The Arabic-language critical anchor is the ESSF's own Elzembely + Aysha editorship and the work of **Shahla Ujayli (شهلا العجيلي)**, who is herself a Syrian-Jordanian novelist and academic — her 2017 critical volume **رواية الديستوبيا العربية: السياق والملامح الفنية** (*Al-Riwaya al-Distubiyya al-‘Arabiyya: al-Siyaq wa al-Malamih al-Fanniyya* / *The Arab Dystopian Novel: Context and Artistic Features*, منشورات مجاز / Majaz Publications, Jordan) is the most sustained Arabic-language critical treatment of the dystopia wave to date, reading Ahmed Saadawi (Iraq), Sun‘allah Ibrahim, Basma Abdel Aziz, and Muhammad Rabie (Egypt). Note: Ujayli's analytical frame is **dystopia, not SF** — the canonical Arabic-criticism term that has stuck is الديستوبيا, sometimes ديستوبيا عربية, only rarely خيال علمي عربي.

  > "هل هي ديستوبيا دينية/عسكرية؟ إلى لحد كبير نعم"
  >
  > [translated from Arabic: original / English: "Is it a religious/military dystopia? To a large extent, yes."]
  > — *Noonpost* review of Basma Abdel Aziz, *الطابور*. https://www.noonpost.com/content/18988/. *Secondary (literary review).* Confirms the Arabic-critical reception's classification of *Al-Tabur* as religious/military dystopia, with the البوابة (the Gate) as totalitarian apparatus.

**On women's place specifically — the most direct source-language statement surfaced this session**:

  > "The ESSF can create more coverage, through Facebook and through the media, of female authors in the field. And not just in Egypt, but the Arab world. Distinguished authors need to be popularised and so serve as role-models to other women interested in the field."
  >
  > — Dr. **Kadria Said (قدرية سعيد)**, ESSF roundtable, *Vector* (BSFA), 2019. *Primary (first-person field-participant statement).* https://vector-bsfa.com/2019/08/24/in-conversation-passing-the-baton-of-egyptian-science-fiction-post-arab-spring/. The roundtable also names **Manal Abd Al-Hamid (منال عبد الحميد)** as a contributor to *Shams al-Ghad* vol. 4 — the two ESSF women contributors most explicitly named in the field's own self-narration. (Note: this is an English-translated roundtable; the original speakers were ESSF members speaking in Arabic, but the published text is English.)

### 1.2 Hebrew (Israel)

The Hebrew SF field is **small, well-organised, and disproportionately woman-populated relative to its size** — the contrast with the Arabic-language field is one of the stronger findings of this recon. The institutional spine is:

- **האגודה הישראלית למדע בדיוני ולפנטסיה** (*HaAguda HaIsraelit le-Mada Bidyoni u-le-Fantasia* / Israeli Society for Science Fiction and Fantasy), at sf-f.org.il. Founded in the 1990s; runs the genre's prizes, the Icon convention (כנס אייקון, since 2000), and the community infrastructure.
- **המימד העשירי** (*HaMimad HaAsiri* / *The Tenth Dimension*) — a long-running Hebrew SF magazine and anthology series; part of the field's core memory.
- **בלי פאניקה** (*Bli Panika* / *Don't Panic*, blipanika.co.il) — the dominant open-web Hebrew SF community forum, continuous since the early 2000s; reviews, criticism, fanzine-style content.
- **פרס גפן** (*Pras Geffen* / Geffen Award), from 1999, named after **עמוס גפן** (Amos Geffen), the first Hebrew translator of SF and a co-founder of the Israeli Society. Five categories: original Hebrew book (ספר המקור), original Hebrew short story (סיפור המקור), translated SF book, translated fantasy book, translated YA book. **Women have won in all five categories.**

  > "פרס גפן הוא פרס ספרותי ישראלי שנתי להצטיינות בתחום המדע בדיוני והפנטזיה. הפרס ניתן מטעם האגודה הישראלית למדע בדיוני ולפנטסיה מאז 1999, בהנצחה של עמוס גפן."
  >
  > [translated from Hebrew: original / English: "The Geffen Award is an annual Israeli literary award for excellence in the field of science fiction and fantasy. The award has been granted by the Israeli Society for Science Fiction and Fantasy since 1999, in commemoration of Amos Geffen."]
  > — he.wikipedia.org/wiki/פרס_גפן. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* Confirms 1999 founding date and Society administration.

- **פרס רטרו גפן** (Retro Geffen) is a backfill prize for pre-1999 work — the eligible-list document on the official Geffen site begins in 1999, suggesting that pre-1999 Israeli women's SF/fantasy is genuinely thin in the field's own self-canonisation. This is consistent with the field's male-dominated 1950s–80s (Aharon Hauptman, Abraham Sharon, Ya'akov Shavit). **The Hebrew women's-SF field is essentially a post-1999 phenomenon in canon-formation terms.**

**Women in the Geffen winners' list (verified this session):**
- **Original Hebrew Book**: הגר ינאי (*Hagar Yanai*, *הלווייתן מבבל* 2006, *המים שבין העולמות* 2008, *המסע אל לב התהום* 2018); קרן לנדסמן (*Keren Landsman*, *לב המעגל* 2019); נעמה בר שירה (*Naama Bar Shira*, *שער הצללים* 2024); רעות וייס (*Re'ut Weiss*, *מזל של מתחילים* 2025).
- **Original Hebrew Short Story**: קרן לנדסמן (Landsman) — five Geffen wins total, four for short fiction (*הגורגונה של הייזנברג* 2011, *לבד, בחושך* 2012, *אבק* 2022, plus shared wins); הילה בניוביץ'-הופמן (*Hila Benyovits-Hoffman*, *חמש ארבע שלוש שתיים אחת* 2015, *לפתוח* 2021); רותם ברוכין (*Rotem Baruchin*, *במראה* 2007, *הפוך, לקחת* 2013); בר פישביין (*Bar Fishbein*, *המקום שבו הספרים נמצאים* 2024); אור הנאי (*Or Hanai*, *בכל פעם מחדש* 2025).

**Press infrastructure**: Keter (כתר), Am Oved (עם עובד), and the smaller genre-focused **גרף / Graff** are the main Hebrew SF publishers; **אופוס / Opus** and **ספריית גרף** specifically run SF/fantasy lines.

**Critical infrastructure**: **Sheldon Teitelbaum** (anthologist, *Zion's Fiction*, 2018) and **Aharon Hauptman** (Israeli SF historian) are the field's senior secondary voices. Critical pieces on the Hebrew SF tradition appear in *Haaretz* (gallery section) and on the Bli Panika forum, but there is no Hebrew academic monograph dedicated to women's SF specifically.

  > "ורד טוכטרמן היא סופרת ומתרגמת ישראלית בסוגת המדע הבדיוני והפנטזיה וכלת פרס גפן לשנת 2003. היא פרסמה סיפורים קצרים רבים באכסניות שונות, כולל סיפור באנתולוגיה 'שם יש שושנים' בעריכת הגר ינאי, שהוקדשה לכתיבה אירוטית של נשים."
  >
  > [translated from Hebrew: original / English: "Vered Tochterman is an Israeli writer and translator in the science fiction and fantasy genre and a Geffen Award winner for 2003. She has published many short stories in various venues, including a story in the anthology *There Are Roses There*, edited by Hagar Yanai, dedicated to women's erotic writing."]
  > — he.wikipedia.org/wiki/ורד_טוכטרמן. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* The *שם יש שושנים* (*Sham Yesh Shoshanim* / *There Are Roses There*) anthology, edited by Hagar Yanai, is one of the few Hebrew-language woman-edited speculative anthologies surfaced this session, and would be a Phase-2 fetch target.

### 1.3 Persian (Iran + diaspora)

The Persian-language SF field is **the structurally thinnest of the four**, both in absolute terms and in women's representation. Persian Wikipedia's overview (fa.wikipedia.org/wiki/ادبیات_علمی-تخیلی_در_ایران) explicitly states that "علمی-تخیلی هواداران زیادی در میان خوانندگان عادی ایرانی ندارد" ("science fiction does not have many supporters among ordinary Iranian readers") and that "علمی-تخیلی در ایران هنوز به یک جریان مستقل و جداگانه تبدیل نشده است" ("science fiction in Iran has not yet become an independent and separate movement"). Translation pipelines from English (Asimov, Bradbury, Heinlein) are well-developed; original Persian SF is a smaller field.

**Magazines and institutional anchors.**

  > "مجلهٔ دانشمند، کیهان علمی و اطلاعات علمی از نشریاتی بودند که به داستان‌های علمی‌تخیلی پرداختند."
  >
  > [translated from Persian: original / English: "*Daneshmand*, *Keyhan-e Elmi*, and *Ettela'at-e Elmi* were among the publications that addressed science fiction stories."]
  > — fa.wikipedia.org/wiki/ادبیات_علمی-تخیلی_در_ایران. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* These are general science magazines that hosted SF rather than specialist SF magazines. The closest thing to a specialist platform is the **آکادمی فانتزی** (*Akademi-ye Fantazy* / Fantasy Academy, fantasy.ir) and the **انجمن فیزیک ایران / IPS (Iran Physics Society)** competition for short physics-themed SF, running since 1382 SH (2003). The **جایزهٔ هنر و ادبیات گمانه‌زن** (*Speculative Art and Literature Prize*, since 1388 SH / 2009) is the field's only dedicated speculative-fiction prize, but it is small in scale.

- The **پرسش‌بان** (*Porseshban*) and **شبکهٔ آفتاب** (*Shabake-ye Aftab*) circuits — referenced in the project brief — did not surface as load-bearing institutions in this session's Persian-language Wikipedia and bookshop searches; they appear to be smaller community sites rather than a *Bli Panika*-equivalent.
- Translation publishers — **نشر چشمه (Cheshmeh)**, **نشر مرکز (Markaz)**, **نشر تندیس (Tandis)**, **نشر هیلا (Hila)**, **نشر افق (Ofogh)** — dominate the field's output, with original SF emerging at Cheshmeh, Markaz, Hila and Tandis.

**Women in Persian-language SF are extremely sparse in surfaced sources.** The Persian Wikipedia overview names only one woman writer in its survey paragraph: **مریم نفیسی‌راد** (*Maryam Nafissi-Rad*) for the 808-page novel *فیلی در اتاق* (*Fili dar Otaq* / *An Elephant in the Room*). The Wikipedia category page **رده:نویسندگان زن علمی تخیلی و فانتزی** (*Women SF and Fantasy Writers*) is dominated by entries on translated foreign women (Le Guin, Atwood, Wolfe, Hopkinson, etc.) rather than Iranian women writers — i.e., the category exists but is mostly populated by translation reception.

**The single most substantial Iranian woman SF writer surfaced this session is **Zoha Kazemi (ضحی کاظمی, b. 1361 SH / 1982 Tehran)**.

  > "زمان‌سوار مجموعه‌ای از ۹ داستان کوتاه گمانه‌زن است... شامل حملات فضایی، جهانی که لمس افراد موجب مرگ می‌شود، سفر در زمان و سناریوهای دیگری از تباهی است."
  >
  > [translated from Persian: original / English: "*Zaman-Savar* (*Time-Rider*) is a collection of nine speculative short stories... including alien attacks, a world where touching people causes death, time travel, and other scenarios of devastation."]
  > — Iran Ketab and Goodreads-FA on Zoha Kazemi, *زمان‌سوار*, https://www.iranketab.ir/book/59583-zaman-savar. *Secondary (publisher / aggregator).* Confirms Kazemi as a working speculative-fiction author with multiple SF subgenres in a single collection. Other Kazemi works: novels *کاج زدگی* (*Kaj Zadegi*), *آدم نما* (*Adam Noma*), *سندرم ژولیت* (*Syndrome Juliet*), *باران‌زاد* (*Baran-Zad*).

**Other Iranian women authors with possible speculative threads (verification incomplete):**
- **زویا پیرزاد** (Zoya Pirzad) and **گلی ترقی** (Goli Taraghi) — both major literary writers; no SF/speculative work surfaced this session beyond literary realism with occasional fantastical threads.
- **افسانه نجم‌آبادی** (Afsaneh Najmabadi) — historian, not SF writer; the search tool surfaced her name but for autobiography work, not speculative fiction.
- **ضحی کاظمی** (Kazemi) is the single confirmed Tier-1 candidate; the rest of the Iranian field requires deeper Persian-language fetching. The Iranian SF prize records (گمانه‌زن جایزه, IPS competition) need a roster pull — not done this session — to surface other women writers.

### 1.4 Turkish

The Turkish-language SF field is **smaller than Hebrew but more woman-populated than Persian and arguably than Arabic** — and uniquely has a **clearly identifiable single foundational woman**: Selma Mine, 1973. Institutional spine:

- **Atılgan Bilimkurgu** (1996–2000, 14 issues, owner Hakan Alpin, editor Bülent Akkoç) — the foundational Turkish SF print magazine. Fanzine-format. Women contributors: **Özlem Kurdoğlu** (debut here with *Acıyı Yoketmek*; her trilogy *Son Cephede Şafak* serialised here), **Sezen Kaymak**, others.

  > "Amatör bir çabanın ürünü olan ve fanzin niteliğinde olan dergi, Türk bilimkurgu yayıncılığı tarihinde kendine yer bulan önemli çalışmalardan biridir."
  >
  > [translated from Turkish: original / English: "The product of an amateur effort and fanzine in nature, the magazine is one of the important works that found its place in the history of Turkish SF publishing."]
  > — tr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atılgan_Bilimkurgu. *Secondary (encyclopedia).* The fanzine framing matters: Atılgan was the field's serious magazine but it never reached commercial scale, and after 2000 the Turkish SF print-magazine pipeline did not recover.

- **X-Bilinmeyen** (1976–1979) and the successor **X-Bilinmeyen Evren, Bilim-Sanat ve Aktüel** (1980–81) — Turkey's earliest SF magazines, **founded by a woman, Selma Mine**, who is also the author of the field's first Turkish-language children's SF novel (*Uzay Yolu*, 1973) and ran her own SF archive. Held in the University of Arizona Library among other foreign collections — meaning the Turkish women's-SF originary text and originary magazine are both Selma Mine. This is structurally distinct from any other tradition surveyed in this recon.
- **Bilim Kurgu Kulübü** (bilimkurgukulubu.com) — the contemporary open-web Turkish SF community site, parallels Bli Panika in Hebrew. Runs the **Atılgan Bilimkurgu Dergisi Arşivi** (online archive of the print magazine) and curates anthology projects.
- **Kayıp Rıhtım** (kayiprihtim.com) — the other major contemporary Turkish SF/fantasy/horror community site. Organised the *Türk Bilimkurgu Yazarları Antolojisi* — the field's most authoritative contemporary author roster.

**Awards.** The Turkish SF field has no prize equivalent to the Geffen Award; the **Türkiye Bilim Kurgu Edebiyatı Ödülü** has run intermittently. The **Yerli Bilimkurgu Yükseliyor** (Domestic SF Is Rising) initiative is a community-curated visibility project rather than a juried prize.

**Anthologies.** *Türk Bilimkurgu Yazarları Antolojisi* (Kayıp Rıhtım, contemporary) — covers Selma Mine, Gülten Dayıoğlu, Alev Alatlı, Aysun Bitir, Ayşe Acar, Özlem Kurdoğlu, Çiçek Sekban Tüfekçi as the women authors.

**Press infrastructure.** **Doğan Kitap**, **İletişim**, **Everest**, **April Yayıncılık** publish Turkish SF/dystopia at scale; **6:45 Yayın** (specifically genre) is more recent. Müge İplikçi's *Sahte Cennetten Kaçış* (2025, Doğan Kitap) is the most recent major literary-press dystopia by a woman author surfaced this session.

**Genre-boundary note**: like the Hebrew and unlike the Arabic field, Turkish-language reception **uses "bilim kurgu" (SF) and "distopya" as overlapping but distinct terms**, with a separate "fantastik" register. **Latife Tekin's *Sevgili Arsız Ölüm* (1983) is consistently classified as büyülü gerçekçilik (magical realism), not SF or distopya** — surfaced sources do not place her in the SF tradition. The project brief's tentative "magical realism / dystopia border" framing for Tekin is consistent with what Turkish-language reception actually says: she is a magical-realism anchor adjacent to but not inside SF. **Out of scope for this dataset.**

**Buket Uzuner**'s Doğa Dörtlemesi (Nature Quartet — *Su*, *Toprak*, *Hava*, *Ateş*) reads as climate-myth speculative literature, an explicit attempt at "modern Türk mitolojisi" (modern Turkish mythology). Defensible inclusion under the dataset's existing tolerance for speculative-literary work (e.g., Wolf, Tokarczuk).

### 2.1 Pre-1970s foundational

**Arabic**: No Arabic-language women SF author surfaced this session for the pre-1970s period. The Egyptian SF founders Mustafa Mahmoud (1921–2009) and Nihad Sharif (1932–2011) are uniformly male; the Arab SF historiography (Elzembely; Campbell; Aysha) does not name a pre-1970 woman Arabic SF writer in surfaced sources. **Treat as a real historical absence under the field's own self-narration**, with the caveat that pre-1970 Arabic women's writing in print at all is a constrained corpus, and women's speculative-fiction work may exist in fanzine, manuscript, or magazine archive form not surfaced this session.

**Hebrew**: No Hebrew-language pre-1970s women's SF author surfaced. Pre-1990s Israeli SF was anchored by male writers (Aharon Hauptman, Abraham Sharon, Ya'akov Shavit). **Real historical absence.**

**Persian**: No pre-1979 Iranian woman SF writer surfaced. The Persian Wikipedia overview's pre-revolution SF pioneers (Sadeq Hedayat's *س. گ. ل. ل.*; Sanati-zadeh's *رستم در قرن بیست و دوم*) are male. **Real historical absence in the surfaced record.**

**Turkish**: **Selma Mine (سلمى ميـن / Selma Mine)** — **Tier-1, Well-documented foundational figure.** First Turkish-language children's SF novel *Uzay Yolu* (Milliyet Yayınları, 1973, four printings). Follow-up *Renkli Ülkeler* (1975, also Milliyet Yayınları). Founded **X-Bilinmeyen Bilim Kurgu Dergisi** (1976–1979), then **X-Bilinmeyen Evren, Bilim-Sanat ve Aktüel** (1980–81). Holds extensive personal SF archive (films, slides, posters, magazines, fanzines), held in the University of Arizona Library among foreign collections. Listed as the first Turkish woman SF writer in *listelist.com* and the *Türk Bilimkurgu Yazarları Antolojisi*. **The field's foundational woman; would be a Tier-1 add anchoring the Turkish slot.** Verify whether her later work continued or whether her field-shaping role exceeded her writing output.

  > "Selma Mine, ülkemizdeki ilk kadın bilim kurgu yazarıdır. 1973 yılında ilk çocuk bilim kurgu romanı olan Uzay Yolu'nu yayımlar."
  >
  > [translated from Turkish: original / English: "Selma Mine is the first woman science fiction writer in our country. In 1973 she published *Uzay Yolu* (*Space Path*), the first children's SF novel."]
  > — listelist.com, *Çevirisi Yerine Yerlisini Okumak İsteyenler İçin 12 Türk Bilim Kurgu Yazarı*, https://listelist.com/turk-bilim-kurgu-yazari/. *Secondary (cultural list).* Confirmed against tr.wikipedia and the *Türk Bilimkurgu Yazarları Antolojisi* roster.

**Maghreb/Algeria**: **Safia Ketou (صفية كتو, born Zohra Rabhi, 1944–1989, Algeria)** — **Tier-1, Well-documented foundational figure for francophone Maghrebi SF.** Wrote *La Planète Mauve* (the title novella) between 1962 and 1969; primary-school teacher in Aïn Séfra; later journalist for APS, Horizon, *Algérie Actualité*. Collection *La Planète Mauve et autres nouvelles* published in Quebec by Naâma Editions, 1983 — **the first Algerian SF book of any kind**. 2019 republication by **Éditions Barzakh** (Algiers) reignited interest. Suicide 29 January 1989, Algiers (jumped from a bridge near APS HQ). Other works include children's books, poetry, plays, and short stories. **The single most important woman SF figure for Maghrebi francophone tradition; corrects gaps.md's "Nora Naji" entry by substitution.**

## 3. Categories of invisibility

### 3.1 Pre-2010 Arabic women's SF

**The single largest gap in the recon.** No source-language source surfaced this session names a pre-2010 Arabic-language woman SF writer with a clearly catalogued SF corpus. Tayba al-Ibrahimi (Kuwait, surfaced in *majalla* survey) is the only candidate name and her corpus and dates are not surfaced. The Egyptian, Syrian, Lebanese 1970s–2000s women's SF cohort that the project brief asked about is **not surfaced in Arabic Wikipedia, ESSF self-narration, or the *majalla* and *Akhbar al-Adab* coverage surfaced this session**. Whether this reflects (a) real absence of women writing in this period in Arabic genre venues, (b) clandestine/fanzine/manuscript circulation not digitised, or (c) digital-indexing gaps in Arab fanzine archives, is **not separable from current evidence**. Treat as a real finding: women's Arabic-language SF in the surfaced record is **essentially a post-2010 phenomenon**, with the Mahmoud / Sharif / Ahmed Khaled Tawfik male-dominated 1960s–2000s preceding it.

### 3.2 Pre-1999 Hebrew women's SF

The Geffen retro-eligibility list begins in 1999. The Israeli SF Society itself does not retroactively claim a deep pre-1990s women's tradition. **Real historical absence under field self-narration**, with the same caveats about fanzine and manuscript archive blind spots.

### 3.3 Persian-language women's SF as a whole

The thinnest field in the recon. The Persian Wikipedia overview **explicitly states** that Iranian SF "has not yet become an independent and separate movement," and the women's contribution within an already-thin field surfaces only **two named authors** in this session: Zoha Kazemi (well-documented) and Maryam Nafissi-Rad (named in passing). The fa.wikipedia "category: women SF and fantasy writers" is dominated by Le Guin / Atwood / Hopkinson (i.e., reception of foreign writers translated into Persian), not by Iranian women writers. **This is a real structural absence as well as a digital-indexing absence.** Geo-restrictions on Iranian sites (Shahre-Ketab archive, Sharq newspaper culture sections) prevented some fetches this session — true depth requires a Persian-script researcher with VPN access.

### 3.4 Maghreb (excl. Safia Ketou) and Berber/Tamazight SF

**Safia Ketou is the foundational and essentially solo figure for Maghrebi women's francophone SF**, surfaced this session via ArabLit and the Algerian-women-in-literature blog circuit. Beyond her, the surfaced record names no other Algerian, Moroccan, Tunisian, or Libyan woman SF writer. **Hoda El Shakry**'s scholarly work at Chicago surveys "speculative and science fiction" in North African Francophone and Arabophone fiction, and is the single most credible Anglophone scholarly anchor for the field — but the surfaced reading-lists from her work emphasise the Qur'anic/hermeneutical influence on speculative writing, not a women's-SF subcorpus. Berber/Tamazight-language speculative fiction by women: **not surfaced** in any form this session. Whether this reflects oral-tradition circulation, lack of digital indexing, or genuine absence is unknown.

### 3.5 Yemen, Iraq, Jordan women's SF

Effectively absent from the surfaced record. Anoud (Iraqi-Egyptian-Norwegian) appears in *Iraq +100* (Comma Press); beyond that anthology contribution, no novel-length corpus surfaced. Yemeni and Jordanian women's SF: **no surfaced names this session**. Treat as real absence pending further investigation.

### 3.6 Pre-Arab-Spring queer / feminist clandestine SF

The project brief asks whether such a corpus existed in self-published or clandestine form before social media made it visible. **Not surfaced this session.** The Arabic Bli-Panika-equivalent open-web forum simply does not exist for the pre-2010 period — Arabic SF community digital infrastructure is essentially Facebook-based and post-2010. Whether Arabic women's queer/feminist SF was circulating in samizdat-style fanzine form is a question that requires direct Cairo / Beirut / Casablanca archival presence to answer.

### 3.7 The "السعودية / Saudi Arabia" gap

Despite Vision-2030-era literary investment, women's SF specifically in Saudi Arabia surfaces only Najwa Al-Otaibi this session. The Sheikh Zayed Book Award SF/genre coverage is structurally weak; the Saudi state literary infrastructure has not surfaced a women's SF cohort.

## 4. What surprised you

**The four traditions are genuinely separable, and the ranking of women's SF density is the inverse of what the project brief implicitly assumed.** The brief treats MENA as a single field with Arabic at the centre. What this recon found is that **Hebrew has the densest, best-organised women's SF infrastructure** (Geffen Award, Bli Panika, multiple women winners across all categories, an editor-novelist cohort that parallels the Brazilian Lady-Sybylla / Aline-Valek case in Hagar Yanai); **Turkish has the clearest women-foundational story** (Selma Mine 1973 + magazine-founding 1976, anchoring a continuous tradition through Atılgan into the contemporary cohort); **Arabic has the largest population of writers but the most fragmented institutional memory and a pre-2010 women's-SF gap that may be permanent**; and **Persian is structurally thinnest** with essentially one Tier-1 woman SF writer (Zoha Kazemi). The "MENA" category as a single field flattens these structural differences.

**The Arabic-language genre vocabulary is "ديستوبيا" (dystopia) more than "خيال علمي" (SF).** This is the Arabic field's analogue to the Brazilian "ficção fantástica" / "ficção científica" distinction. Arabic-language critical reception of Basma Abdel Aziz, Bothayna Al-Essa, Mansoura Ez-Eldin, and the post-Arab-Spring wave classifies them primarily as ديستوبيا writers, sometimes with خيال علمي as secondary descriptor. The genre line that the dataset's "what counts as SF" question asks is unusually live for the Arabic case — and the field's own self-narration is telling us that **for Arabic, dystopia is the core women's-SF subgenre** and other modes (space-opera, hard-SF, time-travel) are still rare. Noura Al Noaman's *أجوان* is the major exception, and notably it is YA / Gulf rather than mainland-Arab literary-press dystopia.

**Selma Mine's 1973 *Uzay Yolu* + 1976 *X-Bilinmeyen* magazine-founding pair is unique across the four traditions surveyed.** No other tradition in this recon has a single foundational woman who is simultaneously the first novelist *and* the founder of the field's first specialist magazine. The Chinese case has 张曉風 (foundational story 1968) but not the magazine-founding role; the Brazilian case has Dinah Silveira de Queiroz but inside an already-functioning male press (GRD); the Hebrew, Persian, and Arabic cases have no foundational woman of comparable scale. Selma Mine should be the highest-priority Phase-2 add from this recon.

**The Maghreb finding is also unique**: Safia Ketou is essentially a solo figure, with no second-generation successor catalogued in the surfaced record. Her 1989 suicide closes the foundational moment without the institutional continuity that Selma Mine bequeathed to Turkish women's SF.

**The "Nora Naji" problem.** The author the project brief named as a Maghrebi candidate appears not to exist with that name and that affiliation: there is no Moroccan woman SF writer of that name in the surfaced record, only an Egyptian literary realist (نورا ناجي, b. 1987 Tanta). This is the kind of factual error that justifies the source-language-first rule and is almost identical in shape to the Brazil-recon's "Sonia Sant'Anna" finding. The actual Maghrebi candidate the dataset should add — **Safia Ketou** — was not in `gaps.md` at all. Recommend the gap-list be updated.

**Possible documented influence edges (subject to per-source verification before recording):**
- **Kafka / Carroll / Orwell / Bradbury → Bothayna Al-Essa** — *حارس سطح العالم* explicitly intertexts these in its narrative architecture; this is documented in the *al-Khaleej* and *ida2at* reviews. Defensible as creative-influence edge with author-statement verification.
- **One Thousand and One Nights → Mansoura Ez-Eldin** — *جبل الزمرد* is explicit about its source-text engagement (the *abjjad* and *Ithra* materials state it). Defensible as creative-influence edge.
- **Ann Druyan / Carl Sagan-era astronomy popular-science → Noura Al Noaman** — Noaman has stated in *Asharq Al-Awsat* interviews that Western popular-science writing shaped *Ajwan*; verification of exact wording needed before recording.

## 5. Sourcing-quality notes

**What worked:**
- **Arabic Wikipedia (ar.wikipedia.org)** is reliable for biographical baselines on established Arab literary figures (Mansoura Ez-Eldin, Noura Al Noaman, Salwa Bakr, Rasha Abbas). The genre-classification frame is "ديستوبيا" or "فانتازيا" or "خيال علمي" inconsistently applied; cross-check is needed.
- **abjjad.com** and **Goodreads-AR** for Arabic publication metadata and synopses; useful for tier-1 confirmation.
- **Dar al-Shorouq, Dar al-Tanwir, Dar al-Arabiyya lil-‘Ulum publisher pages** for in-print catalogue verification.
- **Hebrew Wikipedia (he.wikipedia.org)** is excellent for Israeli SF biographical baselines, prize records, and bibliography. The Geffen Award entry is canonical.
- **The official Geffen Award site (geffen.sf-f.org.il)** carries the full winners' list by year and the Retro Geffen eligibility list.
- **Bilim Kurgu Kulübü (bilimkurgukulubu.com)** for Turkish SF bibliography and magazine history.
- **Kayıp Rıhtım (kayiprihtim.com)** for the *Türk Bilimkurgu Yazarları Antolojisi* — the field's most authoritative contemporary roster.
- **Persian Wikipedia (fa.wikipedia.org)** for institutional anchor (magazines, prizes); the women's-author entries are sparse but the overview entry on Iranian SF is canonical.
- **Iran Ketab (iranketab.ir)** and Goodreads-FA for individual Persian-language author bibliography.
- **ArabLit (arablit.org)** for francophone Maghrebi SF bridging into Anglophone scholarly visibility — this is where Safia Ketou is most accessibly anchored.
- **Vector / BSFA Egyptian SF roundtable (2019)** — load-bearing primary-adjacent source for ESSF self-narration on women members.

**What did not work / requires institutional access:**

**Encyclopedia error patterns spotted in passing:**
- **English-language secondary sources misattribute "Nora Naji" to Morocco-Maghrebi SF**; the actual person of that name in Arabic-language sources is an Egyptian literary realist with no SF affiliation. The project brief's gap-list inherited this confusion.
- **English-language summaries occasionally classify Mansoura Ez-Eldin as "magical realist"** — the Arabic-language reception consistently uses "فانتازيا" (fantasy) or "غرائبية" (uncanny), engaging the *Alf Layla wa Layla* tradition explicitly.
- **The "first Arabic SF novel" claim is contested across Mustafa Mahmoud, Nihad Sharif, and Tawfiq al-Hakim depending on source** — this is a male-only debate, not a women's-SF question, but reflects how the field's foundational chronology is itself unsettled.

**One direct contradiction with `gaps.md`'s framing worth surfacing:** the gaps file frames pre-2010 MENA women's SF as "effectively invisible to Anglophone sourcing," with Salwa Bakr and Nora Naji as the suggested anchor names. Source-language sourcing this session shows that **the pre-2010 Arabic women's SF gap is not just an Anglophone-sourcing gap but a real institutional gap in the field's own self-narration** — it is invisible to Arabic-language sourcing too. The actual recoverable pre-2010 women's SF in surfaced sources lives in **Turkish (Selma Mine 1973+, Gülten Dayıoğlu 1984+, Kurdoğlu 1996+)** and in **francophone Algerian (Safia Ketou 1962–78, published 1983)** — the gaps file did not flag either tradition. Hebrew and Persian women's SF is post-1999 / post-2010 in surfaced canon-formation. **The dataset's MENA gap should be reframed: the gap is genuinely empty in Arabic and Persian for pre-2010, fills well from 2010+ in Arabic, and has substantive pre-2010 corpus only in Turkish and francophone Algerian.**
