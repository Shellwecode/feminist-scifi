# Cross-Recon Synthesis: Women's SF Beyond the Anglophone Window

A synthesis across ten source-language reconnaissance memos — China (PRC + Taiwan + HK), Brazil, Korea, South Asia (Bengali + Hindi-Urdu + Tamil + Malayalam + Marathi + Sinhala + Indo-Anglophone), MENA (Arabic + Hebrew + Persian + Turkish), Indigenous (Turtle Island + Aboriginal Australia + Pacific + Sápmi + Inuit + Indigenous Latin America), Japan, Spanish-language Latin America (Argentina + Mexico + Cuba + Chile + others, excluding Brazil), Africa (continental + diaspora), and European non-English (French + German + Russian + Polish + Italian + Spanish-Spain + Czech + Hungarian + Romanian + Greek + Dutch + Scandinavian + Finnish). Each recon followed the same methodology — source-language venues first, English secondary only as confirmation, an 8-fetch budget for verbatim excerpts, and a per-author work-block deliverable for Phase 2.

The recons are at `recon/{china,brazil,korea,south-asia,mena,indigenous,japan,latam-spanish,africa,europe}-recon.md`. This file is meta-commentary; nothing here replaces the per-region detail. This revision (10-region) supersedes the earlier 6-region version, with the following structural changes: the canon-formation-event pattern is now known to have three sub-types and one explicit break; the foundational-woman pattern is now confirmed in 8 of 10 regions; a new pattern on gendered prize-record asymmetry has emerged; the cross-media/`primary_medium` question has been added; and the Tidbeck-precedent expansion list has grown substantially.

---

## 1. The canon-formation event pattern — three sub-types, one explicit break

**Confirmed in 6 of 10 regions, broken in 1, absent in 1, and now known to manifest in three structurally distinct forms.** The earlier synthesis claimed the pattern as approximately universal; the four newest recons forced revision.

| Region | Year | Event | Form | Agent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Indigenous (Turtle Island) | 2012 | *Walking the Clouds* | anthology | Grace Dillon (Anishinaabe) |
| Brazil | 2013 | *Universo Desconstruído* — "*a primeira coletânea do gênero no Brasil*" | anthology | Lady Sybylla + Aline Valek |
| Indigenous (2SQ) | 2020 | *Love after the End* | anthology | Joshua Whitehead (he/they) |
| Brazil | 2022 | *Dinah Fantástica* (Editora Instante) — re-canonisation | press / posfácio | Ana Rüsche |
| China | 2023 | *《她：中国女性科幻作家经典作品集》* | anthology | 程婧波 |
| Bangladesh | 2023 | *Pramila Kalome Kalpabigyan* | anthology | Trishna Basak |
| Africa | 2018 | "Afrofuturism is not for Africans living in Africa" | foundational essay | Mohale Mashigo |
| Africa | 2019 | "Africanfuturism Defined" | foundational essay | Nnedi Okorafor |
| Sweden | 2025 | *Kallocain* (1940) into Sveriges kulturkanon | state-canonisation | (institutional) |
| Japan | 1994 | *女性状無意識* (*Joseijō Muishiki* / *Techno-Gynesis*) | foundational criticism | 小谷真理 |
| Japan | 2000 | ジェンダーSF研究会 founded | institutional | (collective) |
| Japan | 2001+ | センス・オブ・ジェンダー賞 | prize circuit | ジェンダーSF研究会 |

**Three sub-types now visible:**

- **Anthology canon-formation** (Brazil, China, Bangladesh, Indigenous Turtle Island, Indigenous 2SQ) — a women-or-2SQ-edited volume of contemporary writers framed explicitly as the cohort's first comprehensive showing.
- **Foundational-essay diptych** (Africa) — Mashigo 2018 + Okorafor 2019 jointly establish a self-naming movement-political position (Africanfuturism distinct from Afrofuturism). Structurally homologous to Grace Dillon's 2003 Indigenous Futurisms coinage that anchored *Walking the Clouds* nine years later.
- **Institutional/critical apparatus** (Japan) — 小谷真理's *女性状無意識* (1994) + ジェンダーSF研究会 (2000) + センス・オブ・ジェンダー賞 (2001) form a triad that does the same canon-shaping work the anthologies do elsewhere, but ~20 years earlier and through critical/prize infrastructure rather than a single anthology event.

**Pattern breaks in Spanish-language Latin America.** No women-edited feminist-SF anthology has emerged across Argentina, Mexico, Cuba, Chile, Bolivia, Ecuador, Peru, Venezuela, Colombia, DR, Uruguay. The closest analogue is the 2025 Ecuadorian *La irrealidad y nada más que la irrealidad* (Rodríguez Pappe + Toral, UArtes Guayaquil), which is fantastic-literature-broad rather than feminist-SF-specific. **The absence is itself a finding** — possibly explained by the field's fragmentation across so many national SF traditions, which prevents the pan-Hispanic consolidation that would produce a single canonical event.

**Pattern absent in MENA.** No equivalent event has surfaced in Arabic, Hebrew, Persian, or Turkish source-language sources. The closest analogues are *Iraq+100* / *Palestine+100* (Comma Press, English-mediated, not women-edited) and the Geffen Award's category-by-category women's record (Hebrew; an institution, not an event). The four MENA traditions have not consolidated into one.

**The Sweden case (Boye 2025) extends the pattern in a new direction:** state-level cultural-canon inclusion of a 1940 foundational woman's-SF dystopia as a *literary* canon entry that the SF field reads as foundational genre. State canonisation as canon-formation is a fourth form, currently with one instance, worth watching.

## 2. The Anglophone-summary-error pattern

**Now consistent across 7 of 10 regions.** When a phase-1 gaps file is built from English secondary sources, it inherits whatever the English-language summaries got wrong. The errors cluster on names borrowed from anglophone overviews without source-language verification.

|---|---|---|
| Brazil | Sonia Sant'Anna writes SF | Writes historical fiction; no Portuguese SF venue places her there |
| Brazil | Lady Sybylla = Afrofuturismo | Wikipedia PT categorises as "Feministas do Brasil"; actual Afrofuturismo cohort is Lu Ain-Zaila, Kika Sena, Cidinha da Silva |
| MENA | "Nora Naji (Morocco/France)" — Maghrebi SF | Does not exist as a Maghrebi SF writer; actual Maghrebi anchor is Safia Ketou (Algeria) |
| MENA | Salwa Bakr writes speculative-inflected shorts | Catalogued in Arabic as historical-realist + women's-prison-realism; no SF |

| South Asia | Ismat Chughtai had speculative work | Not corroborated; realist — downgrade or remove |
| South Asia | Sukanya Datta writes Bengali SF | Writes in English |
| Korea | The pre-2000 gap is the live one | Pre-2000 thinness is largely real; the actual gap is **post-2014** (criticism consolidated; English reception lagged) |
| China | Chinese women's novel-length SF is sparse | Empirically wrong in source language (凌晨, 钱莉芳, 彭柳蓉, 郝景芳); gap is the **translation pipeline** |
| Spanish-LatAm | Mouján Otaño *Gu ta gutarrak* censored by Argentine military | Censorship was **Spanish Franco-era Tribunal de Orden Público**, suppressing *Nueva Dimensión* issue 14 (1970) for "atentaba contra la unidad de España" |

| Africa | 14 Tier-1 candidates with documented African-press sourcing | Omitted from gaps.md entirely — Mashigo, Tsamaase, Miano, Aguda, Sheree Renée Thomas, Andrea Hairston, Karen Lord, Lesley Nneka Arimah, Chikodili Emelumadu, Nerine Dorman, Buchi Emecheta, Pauline Hopkins, Yvette Lisa Ndlovu, Nana Akosua Hanson |

**The Indigenous and Japanese recons are the only ones without analogous errors in `gaps.md` or the project brief** — their named candidates check out against source-language sources. But Indigenous has its own problem: gaps.md is materially incomplete in a way that loses Misha 1990 *Red Spider, White Web* and most of the Australian/Pacific/Sami/Inuit/Indigenous-Latin-American cohorts. Japan's problem is that "omitted for space" hides 新井素子 — a 30-year institutional apparatus — under a procedural label.

**Cross-recon error rate on Anglophone-derived names: ~30–40%.** This is a Phase-2 quality-control rule worth adopting as policy.

## 3. The foundational-women-in-source-language pattern — now 8/10 regions

**Confirmed as structural, not anecdotal.** Each tradition has a woman at or near its foundational point, filed under "literary" or "essayist" or "children's writer" rather than SF, and missed by Anglophone reception.

| Region | Foundational figure | Work | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sweden / Europe | **Karin Boye** | *Kallocain* | 1940 |
| Brazil | Dinah Silveira de Queiroz | *Eles Herdarão a Terra* + *Comba Malina* | 1960 + 1969 |
| Russia / Europe | **Olga Larionova** | *Леопард с вершины Килиманджаро* | 1965 |
| Taiwan | 張曉風 | *《潘渡娜》* (中國時報) | 1968 |
| Argentina / Spanish-LatAm | **Magdalena Mouján Otaño** | *Gu ta gutarrak* (Mar del Plata Prize; Spanish-state censored 1970) | 1968 |
| Turkey / MENA | Selma Mine | first Turkish SF novel + *X-Bilinmeyen* magazine founding | 1973 + 1976 |
| Japan | 新井素子 | debut at 16 (1st 奇想天外SF新人賞) | 1977 |
| Nigeria / Africa | **Buchi Emecheta** | *The Rape of Shavi* (dystopian-SF) | 1983 |
| Algeria / MENA | Safia Ketou | *La Planète Mauve* | ~1980s |
| PRC | 张静 / 晶静 | *《最美的眼睛》* / *《神秘的声波》* (银河奖) | 1985–86 |
| Indigenous (Turtle Island) | Misha (Misha Nogha) | *Red Spider, White Web* (cyberpunk) | 1990 |
| African diaspora | **Pauline Hopkins** | *Of One Blood* | 1902–03 |
| South Asia | Sukhalata Rao (Hossain-contemporary) + Leela Majumdar | various | colonial → 2007 |

**Foundational-woman pattern is now confirmed in:** Sweden, Russia, Taiwan, Argentina, Turkey, Algeria, Nigeria, PRC, Indigenous, South Asia. **Confirmed differently in Japan** — 新井素子 debuts at 16 inside the genre-magazine pipeline (奇想天外SF新人賞), not as a literary-canon figure being read into SF. **Real absence in Korea pre-1990** (no woman writer of adult Korean SF predates Djuna 1992; source-language search confirms; the single unpulled lever is the 국립중앙도서관 children's-magazine archive). **Real absence in Hong Kong genre-SF.** **Real absence in Bangladesh post-Hossain.**

**Karin Boye 1940 *Kallocain* is the single highest Phase-2 priority** — 23 years before Haushofer's *Die Wand*, included in Sveriges kulturkanon 2025. She predates every other foundational woman in the table except Pauline Hopkins (1902–03 African-diaspora) and is more clearly genre-coded than the other early-20th-c. candidates.

## 4. Translation-pipeline-vs-writing-gap

**The dataset has been treating "scarce in English" and "scarce in source language" as the same claim. Six of ten recons show they aren't.**

- **China** — novel-length women's SF is well-developed in Chinese (凌晨, 钱莉芳, 彭柳蓉, 郝景芳); almost untranslated. Gap is in the pipeline.
- **Korea** — the post-2014 wave was already moving in source language before Anton Hur / Sophie Bowman / Sung Ryu began translating in 2019. Aladin 2020 ties it to the 민주화운동 → 여성운동 → 호주제 폐지 → 페미니즘 리부트 chain.
- **Brazil** — Causo's histories, Editora Instante's reissues, Mafagafo's anthologies all exist in Portuguese.
- **South Asia** — Bengali, Hindi, Marathi, Tamil women's SF in source-language venues (Kalpabiswa, Anandamela, *Ashcharya*); minimal translation.
- **Japan** — *倉橋由美子 アマノン国往還記* (1986, 河出書房新社) is **the single clearest case of a foundational feminist-SF work being untranslated**. Hagio Moto's *11人いる!* and 萩尾望都's broader SF manga corpus are partially translated; her *銀の三角* (1980 prose-SF) is not. 菅浩江's award-winning corpus is largely untranslated.
- **Indigenous** — the inverse case: most work is already in English (the colonial language). Gap is **canon-recognition**: filed under "magical realism" / "literary fiction" / "children's" by anglophone genre infrastructure.

**Partial exceptions:** MENA — pre-2010 Arabic women's SF is genuinely thin even in source language; Persian and Turkish are genuinely thin in women writers regardless of language of access. **Spanish-LatAm** — Argentine writers are well-translated via Megan McDowell pipeline; the gap is geographic narrowness (AR×4, MX×2, CU×1 in dataset) rather than pipeline. **Europe** — well-translated overall but the prize record (see §6) shows a different asymmetry.

**Phase-2 implication:** distinguish writing gap from translation gap as two separate data states.

## 5. Gendered-genre-policing

**Three of ten recons surfaced the same structural mechanism: women's speculative writing at scale gets re-classified as not-SF, while men's speculative writing at scale gets read as SF.** Now extended by a fourth language-asymmetry case.

- **China** — 晋江 (women) vs 起点 (men). Women on 晋江 filed as 玄幻 / 仙侠 / 言情 / 穿越, almost never 科幻. Print-prize circuit (银河奖, 星云奖) excludes by design.
- **Korea** — 조아라 / 노벨피아 / 문피아. Women's speculative writing is 로맨스판타지 (로판) / BL, not SF. Same gendered platform-genre split.
- **South Asia (partial)** — Pratilipi hosts women's speculative fiction at scale, mostly classified as devotional / mythological / romance.
- **Spanish-LatAm (new asymmetry type)** — Bazterrica's *Cadáver Exquisito* is read as **distopía / ciencia ficción in Argentine reception** but marketed as **horror in US reception** (*Tender Is the Flesh*). Same text, different genre-policing across languages. The mechanism is publisher/marketer-driven rather than platform-driven, but it produces the same effect: women's speculative writing gets re-classified out of SF.

**Brazil and MENA have less platform-driven splits but similar boundary work in critical reception.** Brazilian Afrofuturismo women (Lu Ain-Zaila, Kika Sena) read through racial-political frames not SF. Arabic women's literary-fantastic (Ez-Eldin, Humaydan) read as literary not SF. **Indigenous and Africa** show the inverse — overtly genre-coded work is shelved as "magical realism" (Wright, Hogan) or as "literary fiction" (Hopkins). **Europe** shows the inverse-inverse — Maria Galina debuted in 1997 **under a male pseudonym (Максим Голицын)** to enter the SF infrastructure at all, a direct gender-policing event documented in ru.wikipedia.

## 6. Gendered prize-record asymmetry (new)

**A cross-recon pattern that emerged only when the four new recons made the comparison possible.** Even where the field is dense and well-organised, the recognition record skews against women writers more sharply than publication rates would predict.

| Tradition | Prize | Women record |
|---|---|---|
| Germany | Kurd-Laßwitz best-novel | **1 woman laureate in 45 years** (Pausewang 1988) |
| France | Grand Prix de l'Imaginaire roman francophone | 5 women in ~50 years |
| Czechia | Magnesia Litera fantastika | First woman 2023 (Kadlečková) |
| China | 银河奖 | Mid-1990s gap between 张静 1986 and 凌晨/赵海虹 1995/97 |
| MENA Arabic | (any genre-SF prize) | Pre-2010 essentially empty for women |
| Russia | (SF prize entry) | Maria Galina debuted 1997 under male pseudonym *Максим Голицын* |
| Japan | 日本SF大賞 | 7 women in 45 years — but 3 of 7 are manga creators (see §9) |

**Europe's prize-record asymmetry is the most striking finding of the new recons.** Despite the densest non-Anglophone institutional infrastructure of any region surveyed, the Kurd-Laßwitz best-novel category has effectively excluded women since Gudrun Pausewang's 1988 win. This contradicts gaps.md's implicit framing that European women's SF is "well-covered." It is well-published; it is not well-prized.

**Phase-2 implication:** prize-record skew is distinct from publication skew. Adding a `prize_history` data column would surface this asymmetry per-author and let the dataset record cases like Galina's male-pseudonym debut as documented gender-policing events, not as biographical trivia.

## 7. Where structural absences are real

**Updated ledger.** Findings the recons recommend recording as historical absence rather than research failure.

- **Pre-1980s women's SF in PRC** — political-historical absence; first identifiable PRC woman SF writer is 张静 1985–86.
- **Hong Kong genre-SF women's tradition** — 倪匡 monopoly + Cantonese-vernacular market.
- **Bangladesh post-Hossain** — Trishna Basak's own framing: only two regular women SF writers (Tanzina Hosen, Nasreen Mustafa).
- **Pre-2010 Arabic women's SF** — genuinely thin in source-language sources.
- **Sinhala / Tamil-Lankan women's SF** — thin to zero.
- **Sami / Inuit / Latin American Indigenous in Indigenous-language SF** — thin.
- **Pre-1990s adult Korean women's SF** — largely real per source-language search.
- **Pre-1970s Japanese women in genre-SF** — real per source-language histories; the field's women anchor is 新井素子 1977. Pre-1977 women appear only in manga-SF (萩尾望都 *11人いる!* 1975 is the immediate predecessor in a different medium).
- **Spanish-LatAm canon-formation anthology** — no equivalent of *《她》* / *Universo Desconstruído* exists.
- **North Korean women's SF** — structurally absent.

## 8. Documented influence edges

**The dataset's strict influence-edge rule (documented statement only) yields edges that source-language verification can both extend and tighten.** Updated list:

- **China** — Liu Cixin → 钱莉芳 *《天意》* (2004) → *《三体》*. Liu credited *Tianyi* as the success that gave him confidence to write *San Ti*. Verbatim source still pending (Baidu URLs returned 404 in china-recon).
- **South Asia** — Lila Majumdar → Mimi Mondal. Verbatim documented in Mondal's *Feminism In India* interview.
- **Spanish-LatAm** — **Le Guin ↔ Gorodischer**. Documented translation relationship (Le Guin translated *Kalpa Imperial* for Small Beer 2003); Gorodischer's own statements in Página/12 and Infobae interviews position the relationship as mutually feminist-political.
- **Brazil** — Roberto de Sousa Causo ↔ Finisia Fideli (married 1991).
- **Korea** — Le Guin → Kim Bo-Young; Le Guin → Djuna (both via interviews); Bora Chung → Lem / Sapkowski (translation pipeline).
- **Indigenous** — Grace Dillon → most Indigenous Futurisms writers post-2012 (anthology-mediated); Misha → later Indigenous-women cyberpunk via Dillon's anthology.
- **MENA** — Hosam Elzembely → Egyptian SF women's cohort (institutional/ESSF mediated).
- **Africa** — **Mashigo 2018 ↔ Okorafor 2019** as a foundational debate (each writer cites/responds to the other); Octavia Butler → Tananarive Due (documented; verify sourcing); Nnedi Okorafor → most post-2019 Africanfuturism writers.
- **Europe** — **Leena Krohn → Johanna Sinisalo** (Finnish Weird succession, documented in Finnish criticism); Christa Wolf ← Anna Seghers (GDR literary-political); Boye ← Karen Blixen (Scandinavian literary-feminist axis, to verify).
- **Japan** — 萩尾望都 → many later women SF/manga creators (cross-generational, well-documented in Japanese criticism); 小谷真理 as critic-influence on the whole post-1994 cohort.

**Three influence-edge types continue to hold:** direct statement (Liu Cixin → 钱莉芳, Mashigo ↔ Okorafor), mediated-through-editor (Dillon → Indigenous Futurisms, 小谷真理 → Japanese feminist-SF cohort), translation-pipeline (Le Guin → Gorodischer, Bora Chung → Lem). Phase 2 should record these as distinct edge categories.

## 9. Cross-media / `primary_medium` category question (new)

**Japan is the type-specimen and forces the dataset to confront a category question the other regions only hint at.** Three of seven women winners of the 日本SF大賞 (Japan's most prestigious SF prize, 45 editions) are manga creators: **萩尾望都** (2006, for life work + *バルバラ異界*), **白井弓子** (2016, for *WOMBS*), **市川春子** (2024, for *宝石の国*). The field-internal canon ratifies manga as SF at the highest institutional level.

**The dataset's current prose-only filter excludes them.** This is not a marginal exclusion: 萩尾望都's *11人いる！* (1975) is the foundational woman's SF work in Japan, a year earlier than 新井素子's 1977 debut. *銀の三角* (1980), *バルバラ異界* (2002–05), *ポーの一族* (1972–76 + 2016–) form a corpus that any honest mapping of Japanese women's SF must include.

**Adjacent cases in other recons:**
- **Indigenous** — Beth LaPensée (Anishinaabe-Métis) is a game designer + comics writer; Roanhorse's RPG / Marvel work; *Native Realities* press for Indigenous comics.
- **MENA** — Sophia Al-Maria and Larissa Sansour are primarily film/visual artists (already noted in research-log as a structural extension to the dataset).
- **Africa** — Wanuri Kahiu is primarily film (*Pumzi* 2009; *Rafiki* 2018). Already in dataset under cross-media tolerance.
- **Korea** — webcomic (웹툰) speculative work by women is uncatalogued in the recon and may form a parallel manga-Japan body.

**Phase-2 recommendation:** add `primary_medium` as a separate tag (prose / manga / film / visual / game / mixed). Apply Tidbeck-precedent-style inclusion gated on field-internal canonisation — i.e. 日本SF大賞 / 星雲賞 SF-category presence for manga, Nommo for African film, etc. Without this, the dataset's structural representation of Japan, MENA, Indigenous, and Africa is incoherent.

## 10. Phase-2 structural recommendations — refreshed

**Thirteen recommendations follow from the recons.**

1. **Indigenous as its own regional bucket** (cross-continental: Turtle Island North + Indigenous Latin America + Aboriginal Australia + Māori/Pasifika + Sápmi + Inuit).

2. **Subdivide MENA into four traditions** (Arabic / Hebrew / Persian / Turkish). Density inverts the brief's implicit framing.

3. **Subdivide East Asia into Japan / PRC / Taiwan / Hong Kong**. Japan is substantively distinct from Sinophone traditions on canon-formation form (institutional/critical not anthology), foundational-figure trajectory (genre-pipeline not literary-recanonised), and primary medium (manga + prose). Taiwan has its own foundational figure (張曉風 1968) and queer-SF anchor (洪凌 1994). HK is genuinely thin and that thinness is itself a finding.

4. **Subdivide South Asia by linguistic tradition** (Bengali / Hindi-Urdu / Tamil / Malayalam / Marathi / Sinhala / Indo-Anglophone).

5. **Split African region into continental + diaspora sub-buckets under unified "African + Diaspora"** with dual-tagging for explicit cross-positioning (Okorafor, Onwualu, Bacon, Serpell, Hopkinson). Collapsing the two flattens the Mashigo/Okorafor debate.

6. **Re-bucket Vonarburg from European-non-English to francophone-Canadian / North-American francophone.** Born France 1947, emigrated Québec 1973; institutional home is *Solaris* (Longueuil, QC), Prix Boréal, Prix Aurora. Parallels Aliette de Bodard in reverse.

7. **Recognise Spanish-LatAm has no canon-formation anthology event** — record the absence, don't construct a fictional one.

8. **Add `movement-politics` tag set**: `afrofuturism` / `africanfuturism` / `africanjujuism` / `indigenous-futurisms` / `2sq-indigiqueer` / `gulf-futurism` / `cyberfeminism` / `solarpunk`. Movement-political identity is separable from geographic location; the same author (Okorafor) is US-diaspora-located but Africanfuturism-positioned, *against* the diaspora-anchored Afrofuturism frame.

9. **Add `primary_medium` tag** (prose / manga / film / visual / game / mixed). Japan, Indigenous, MENA, Africa all require this for honest representation.

10. **Add `prize_history` as a separate data column.** Surface gendered prize-record asymmetries (Kurd-Laßwitz 1/45, Magnesia Litera first 2023, 银河奖 mid-90s gap, Maria Galina male pseudonym) as documented field facts.

11. **Carry the canon-formation events as primary sources.** Anthologies (*Walking the Clouds*, *Universo Desconstruído*, *Take Us to Your Chief*, *Love after the End*, *Dinah Fantástica*, *《她》*, *Pramila Kalome Kalpabigyan*) AND foundational essays (Mashigo 2018, Okorafor 2019, Dillon 2003 coinage) AND institutional events (ジェンダーSF研究会 2000, センス・オブ・ジェンダー賞 2001, Sveriges kulturkanon 2025).

12. **Distinguish writing gap from translation gap** as two separate data states.

13. **Source-language verification rule for Phase 2.** Cross-recon error rate on Anglophone-derived names is ~30–40%. Any author named in `gaps.md` or considered for inclusion should be confirmed against at least one source-language venue before per-author work begins.

14. **Extend Tidbeck-precedent (non-binary inclusion with in-file flag) to:**
    - **Joshua Whitehead** (Oji-Cree, he/they)
    - **jaye simpson** (Sturgeon Lake Cree, they/them)
    - **Kai Minosh Pyle** (Métis + Sault Ste. Marie Nishnaabe, they/them)
    - **Mari Kurisato** (Ojibwe + Cote First Nation, they/them)
    - **Akwaeke Emezi** (Igbo + Tamil, ze/zir + they/them; ọgbanje cosmology)
    - **Rivers Solomon** (African-American + Cherokee, fae/faer + they/them)
    - **Tlotlo Tsamaase** (Motswana, xe/xem + she/her)

The *Love after the End* (2020) anthology marks 2SQ Indigenous SF as a self-organised cohort; Emezi and Solomon were flagged in Phase-1 research-log as Afrofuturist exclusions on pronoun-accuracy grounds; Tsamaase is the most-decorated Nommo-winning xe/xem author. All are defensible under the existing in-file-flag tolerance.

## 11. Methodology findings — refreshed

**What worked, across all ten recons:**

- **Source-language Wikipedia in 15+ languages** consistently outperformed English Wikipedia for non-Anglophone authors. zh, pt, ko, bn, he, tr, ar, ja, es (multiple national variants), fr, de, ru, sv, fi, cs, hu, pl all held up.
- **Source-language press archives** — *Munhwa Ilbo* (Korea), *中国作家网* / *澎湃新闻* (China), *Editora Instante / Folha* (Brazil), *Prothom Alo* (Bangladesh), *Mada Masr* (Egypt), *Indian Country Today* (Indigenous), *Página/12* / *Infobae* (Argentina), *Le Monde Diplomatique* (France), *Le Bélial' / Bifrost* (France SF), *Tähtivaeltaja* (Finland), *Tydskrif vir Letterkunde* (SA Afrikaans-academic), *Johannesburg Review of Books* (SA), *Munhwa Ilbo* (Korea).
- **Editorial substacks and small-press blogs** — Editora Instante (Brazil) was the best single source on Dinah re-canonisation. Páginas de Espuma jury statements (Spain, for Colanzi 2022) were primary-quality.
- **Women-edited anthology TOCs** — densest single-source author rosters across regions. *《她》* (33 authors), *Pramila Kalome Kalpabigyan*, *Walking the Clouds*, *Love after the End*, *Universo Desconstruído*. Phase-2 starting points.
- **Sveriges kulturkanon 2025** — state-level canon documents surface foundational-genre work that anglophone reception missed entirely (Boye).

**What didn't work:**

- **CNKI (China), Shodhganga (India), KISS (Korea), Cairn (France-academic), JSTOR** paywall most full-text. Phase-2 needs librarian assist.
- **WeChat-public-account pieces** unreadable from outside ecosystem.
- **PC통신-era Korean SF (1989–1999) archives** largely lost.
- **Baidu Baike URL slugs** for Chinese authors require disambiguation parentheticals; search-redirect should default.
- **Indigenous-language source access** (Diné Bizaad, Inuktitut, Cree, Quechua, Aymara, Te Reo Māori) structurally limited.
- **Suplemento Pernambuco, Causo Trasgo interview, CLFC site, Giulia Moon Fandom, Tashan Mehta site, Foundation Essay Prize venue, Cubaencuentro 2009 panorama essay, Chilean Brumal 2022** all returned 403/SSL-error/empty/repurposed-domain during fetching.
- **Trujillo Muñoz (Mexico) and Capanna (Argentina) load-bearing SF-history chapters** are library-access only — institutional access needed.
- **Cross-recon success rate on excerpt-fetching: ~75%** (52 of 70 priority fetches succeeded; 18 marked pending with reasons).

## 12. Shape of the field, restated

**The recons substantially complicate `gaps.md`'s working assumption that women's SF outside the Anglophone canon is mostly absent.** What's missing from the dataset is not the writing — it's the source-language verification, the canon-formation events (in their three forms), the foundational figures filed under "literary" or "essayist," the woman-editor / woman-prize-administrator / woman-critic infrastructures, and the institutional knowledge of how each tradition organises itself.

**Across the ten regions, the recons identify roughly 100+ Tier-1 authors** who would defensibly extend the dataset under its existing inclusion rules — China names 13; Brazil ~10; Korea ~8; South Asia ~10; MENA ~12; Indigenous ~20; Japan ~8; Spanish-LatAm ~8; Africa ~14; Europe ~12. Some overlap (Bacon crosses Africa / diaspora; Vonarburg crosses Europe / francophone-Canadian; Léonora Miano crosses Africa / France).

**The ratio of "filed in dataset" to "documented in source-language" remains approximately 1:5 across the surveyed regions.** Phase-2 is a 2–3× expansion of the current 76-author dataset, not a marginal addition.

**The single most actionable Phase-2 recommendation** remains the source-language verification rule. The methodology pays off; the error rate without it is high enough to compromise the dataset's credibility on regional claims.
