# Research Log — Phase 1

## Overview

76 per-author markdown files produced across 7 regions, committed region-by-region. This log records what was searched, what could not be sourced, who was considered and rejected, and proposed adjustments to the theme vocabulary.

## Region-by-region notes

### Anglophone (29)

**Density**: easiest region to source. Wikipedia, SFE (sf-encyclopedia.com), SFWA press releases, and major publisher catalogues give strong coverage of most entries. ISFDB.org supplied translation-edition records.

**Approach**: started with foundational figures (Shelley, Gilman, Mitchison, Burdekin, Moore, Brackett, Norton) to anchor the influence graph; moved through mid-20th-century (McCaffrey, Le Guin, Russ, Tiptree/Sheldon, Wilhelm, Atwood, McIntyre, Butler, Cherryh, Lessing, Carter, Lee), then to contemporary (Bujold, Willis, Kress, Cadigan, Jones, Griffith, Jemisin, Leckie, Chambers, Lanagan).

**Over target**: went to 29 (target 20–25) because excluding any core anchor would weaken downstream influence edges. Kept the region here; intentionally did not further expand because the brief flagged not spending half the session on Anglophone.

**Considered and rejected**:
- **Mercedes Lackey**, **Jacqueline Carey**, **Sharon Shinn**: fantasy-primary; kept out to hold SF emphasis.
- **Marion Zimmer Bradley**: moral/professional issues around her history of child abuse; decided to omit.
- **Jo Walton**, **Justina Robson**, **Tricia Sullivan**, **Storm Constantine**, **Naomi Alderman**, **Elizabeth Bear**: strong candidates; space-constrained. Flagged in gaps.md.
- **Martha Wells** (Murderbot): omitted only because the category was already full of Hugo-recent writers; genuine candidate for later inclusion.
- **Zenna Henderson**: considered; reluctantly omitted.
- **Tiptree/Sheldon**: filed under her Tiptree pen name because this is how she functioned in the genre and how her award/influence is framed.

### East Asian (12)

**Density**: asymmetric. Japanese SF criticism in English is thin for the pre-2020 period; the 2021 Verso *Terminal Boredom* translation is the main vehicle for English reception of Suzuki Izumi. Kurahashi Yumiko's *A Round Trip to the Land of Amanon* — her clearest feminist SF — **is not translated into English**. Chinese SF coverage is strong via Ken Liu's translation projects (*Clarkesworld*, *Invisible Planets*, *Broken Stars*). Korean SF coverage in English has exploded since 2019 (Anton Hur, Sophie Bowman, Sung Ryu).

**Approach**: 4 + 4 + 4 balance (Japan + China + Korea). Anchor figures (Suzuki, Ōhara; Hao Jingfang, Xia Jia; Djuna, Kim Bo-Young, Kim Choyeop, Bora Chung) plus a representative working-writer in each (Kurahashi, Ogawa, Chi Hui, Tang Fei).

**Considered and rejected**:
- **Yoko Tawada** (Japanese-German, writes in both): strong candidate but cross-category; placed on the watchlist. *The Emissary* (2014) is speculative.
- **Arai Motoko** (Japanese YA-SF pioneer): considered; omitted for space.
- **Tsushima Yūko**: more literary than SF; omitted.
- **Liu Cixin, Chen Qiufan, Han Song**: male; excluded by brief.
- **Regina Kanyu Wang**: more editorial/academic role; included indirectly through sourcing.
- **Gu Shi**: strong contemporary candidate; omitted for space.
- **Yoo Sujin**, **Jeon Samhye**, **Ha Seong-nan**: Korean candidates; less internationally visible than the included four.

### Latin American (8)

**Density**: variable. Argentine SF is the most internationally visible thread (Gorodischer, Schweblin, Bazterrica, Enríquez, Ocampo — mostly via Megan McDowell translations and Small Beer Press / Penguin Random imprints). Cuban, Mexican, Brazilian women's SF is patchier in English. Portuguese-language Brazilian women's SF is the weakest area.

**Approach**: 4 Argentine anchors + Cuba + 2 Mexican + 1 Brazilian. Mira de Echeverría included as a small-press working-writer to balance the Booker-tier breakout names.

**Considered and rejected**:
- **Mariana Enríquez**: more horror than SF; left out (she fits but is already extensively profiled elsewhere).
- **Silvia Moreno-Garcia**: writes in English, Mexican-Canadian; considered for "other" (MENA + other) placement; rejected to keep that bucket focused on MENA + Indigenous.
- **Liliana Bodoc**: Argentine fantasist; largely outside SF.
- **Elena Garro**, **Clarice Lispector**: magical-realist / literary fantastic; decided not strictly SF, though influence on feminist speculative tradition is real.
- **Teresa Dovalpage**, **Ana María Shua**: candidates for expansion in future phases.
- **Sonia Sant'Anna**, **Ana Rüsche**, **Giulia Moon** (Brazil): under-documented in English; Fideli represents this stratum as the most clearly canonical figure but the whole Brazilian women's SF cohort is a real gap.

### African + diaspora (9)

**Density**: strong for US diaspora (Butler is in Anglophone; Okorafor, Hopkinson, Due, Shawl, Jemisin in Anglophone via US publisher track record). Continental African SF is sparser; Beukes (South Africa) and Kahiu (Kenya) were the clearest canonical choices. *AfroSF* (2012), *Omenana* magazine, Nommo Awards are the central institutional sources.

**Approach**: 4 diaspora + 3 continental + 1 Zambia/diaspora + 1 UK-Nigerian diaspora. Cross-category: Butler and Jemisin are in Anglophone, Okorafor's Africanfuturism is documented in the Okorafor entry as an explicit counter-term to Afrofuturism.

**Considered and rejected**:
- **Tade Thompson, Biram Mboob, Wole Talabi**: male; excluded.
- **Akwaeke Emezi**: non-binary (uses they/them); excluded on pronoun-accuracy grounds — the user's dataset is framed around women writers.
- **Rivers Solomon**: non-binary (fae/faer, they/them); excluded on the same grounds. This is a notable gap for Afrofuturist SFF and is flagged.
- **Chibundu Onuzo**, **NoViolet Bulawayo**, **Tsitsi Dangarembga**: primarily literary; SF content limited.
- **Eugen Bacon** (Tanzania/Australia): strong candidate; omitted to stay within 9. Flag for expansion.
- **Suyi Davies Okungbowa, Pemi Aguda**: male (Okungbowa) / emerging (Aguda).

### European non-English (8)

**Density**: uneven. Strong English sources for Christa Wolf, Tokarczuk (Nobel), Tidbeck (self-translation), Sinisalo (Tiptree 2004). French feminist SF (Wittig, Vonarburg) well-documented. Russian women's SF thinner in English — Tatyana Tolstaya anchors the region but contemporaries are patchy.

**Approach**: French 1 + Francophone Canadian 1 + Austrian 1 + German 1 + Polish 1 + Russian 1 + Swedish 1 + Finnish 1.

**Considered and rejected**:
- **Marie NDiaye** (France): speculative-literary; left out because closer to magical-realism than SF.
- **Angela Steinmüller** (GDR SF, with husband Karlheinz): not fully independent authorship.
- **Maria Galina** (Russia): strong candidate, omitted for space.
- **Joanna Bator, Marta Kisiel, Anna Kańtoch** (Poland): Tokarczuk covers the Polish slot; these are gaps.
- **Leena Krohn** (Finland): considered for second Finnish slot; Sinisalo's international visibility won.
- **Margareta Karoline Lasker**, **Clemence Dane**: too dated/marginal.
- **Aliette de Bodard** (France, writes in English): category ambiguity; omitted from this region, though she'd fit a cross-diaspora category.
- **Karin Tidbeck**'s pronouns: they/them; included with a brief in-file note because their work has been consistently housed in women-SF anthologies and their earlier publications used she/her.

### South Asian (5)

**Density**: thin for English-language sources generally. Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain is extensively documented; Vandana Singh has visible English-language output (she writes in English); Padmanabhan, Chabria, Shah all require deeper recourse to Indian publishers' catalogues.

**Approach**: one foundational anchor (Hossain) + 4 contemporary figures spanning India and Pakistan.

**Considered and rejected**:
- **Tashan Mehta** (India): strong recent candidate, omitted for space.
- **Shveta Thakrar**, **Premee Mohamed**: diasporic SFF; Mohamed is more clearly in the Anglophone market, omitted here.
- **Arunava Sinha**, **Samit Basu**: male.
- **Sumana Roy**, **Deepa Anappara**: mostly literary, not SF.
- **Krishna Sobti**, **Mahasweta Devi**: limited SF content; primarily literary-political.
- **Sukanya Datta**: Bengali SF writer; very hard to source in English.
- **Sri Lankan and Bangladeshi women SF writers beyond Hossain**: a genuine gap.

### MENA + other (5)

**Density**: thin to very thin. Arabic women's SF outside the post-2013 Egyptian dystopia wave is hard to locate in English-language sources. Maghreb women's SF is particularly under-represented.

**Approach**: 4 MENA (Egypt, Qatar-US, Palestine-Denmark, Israel) + 1 Indigenous-Americas (Roanhorse).

**Category extension**: Al-Maria and Sansour are primarily essayist/visual-artists. Kahiu (filed under African) is similarly cross-media. I flag this as a meaningful structural extension to the dataset; these cross-media figures are disproportionately important in regions where prose-SF markets are thin, and excluding them would make the dataset misleading about where the living imaginative work happens.

**Considered and rejected**:
- **Noor Naga**: more literary-weird than SF.
- **Leila Aboulela**: largely literary.
- **Rasheda Zakaria**, **Fatma Aydemir** (Turkey/Germany): partial fits; omitted for space.
- **Savyon Liebrecht**: realist.
- **Ofir Touché Gafla**, **Shimon Adaf**, **Saad Z. Hossain**: male.
- **Monira Al Qadiri**: Kuwaiti Gulf-Futurist artist; overlaps with Al-Maria; omitted for space.

## Proposed theme vocabulary additions (flagged as proposed)

The controlled vocabulary was generally adequate, but three gaps emerged repeatedly:

1. **`disability`** — distinct from `embodiment`. Kim Choyeop (hearing loss, explicit in fiction), Butler (chronic-illness threads in Earthseed), Vonarburg, Nicola Griffith (MS), McIntyre's medical fiction all treat disability as *theme* not just bodily condition. `embodiment` collapses this.
2. **`translation/crossing`** — the act of linguistic or cosmological translation as a structural concern. Le Guin, Leckie (pronoun/gender in Radchaai language), Wittig (grammatical insurrection), Tidbeck (labelling in *Amatka*), Xia Jia, Roanhorse. This is distinct from `language` as diegetic topic because it marks the authorial-political stance.
3. **`activism/movement-politics`** — Afrofuturism / Africanfuturism / Gulf Futurism as explicit political-aesthetic positions. Al-Maria, Okorafor, Hopkinson, Kahiu, Shawl all engage SF *as* a movement-political project in ways that e.g. Le Guin or Bujold mostly do not.

Phase-2 decision to make: adopt these, leave them as optional tags, or discard.

## Process issues worth noting

- **Session interruption mid-African-region**: the sandbox temporarily lost access to the working directory after the 9th African file was written. No content was lost; a Claude Code session restart restored access and the African region was committed normally.
- **Influence edges**: held to the strict rule of requiring a documented source. Where I was confident but could not point to a specific URL/book/interview, I omitted the edge. This will likely leave some real edges unrecorded; phase-2 reviewers may want to fill them in.
- **Cross-media inclusions** (Kahiu, Al-Maria, Sansour): flagged explicitly in-file. The alternative — a pure-prose dataset — would over-represent Anglophone/Anglo-publishing conventions at the expense of MENA and African speculative imagination.

## Regions where sourcing was hardest

1. **Latin American Brazilian women SF** (pre-2010): essentially dependent on Roberto de Sousa Causo's Portuguese-language historical volumes; English sources are minimal. Fideli is placeholder-quality.
2. **MENA pre-2010 women's SF**: thinnest. Basma Abdel Aziz is the earliest clearly SF figure.
3. **Chinese women's SF novel-length work**: short fiction is well-translated; novels are not. Hao Jingfang and the two collections by Xia Jia/Chi Hui via Ken Liu are almost the entirety of the novel-length English access.
4. **Indigenous women's SF outside Roanhorse/Dimaline**: a whole tradition that deserves its own regional slot in a phase-2 redefinition.
5. **South Asian pre-21st-century women SF beyond Hossain**: a century-sized gap.
